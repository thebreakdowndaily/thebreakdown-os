I. Executive Summary
callout b-exec-summary
India began its independent existence on 15 August 1947 amid the trauma of Partition — the largest forced migration in human history and one of the bloodiest communal conflagrations of the twentieth century. The division of British India into two sovereign states, India and Pakistan, displaced an estimated 14.5 million people, killed between 200,000 and 2 million, and left an enduring legacy of hostility between the two nuclear-armed neighbours. This chapter examines how the foundational experience of Partition shaped every dimension of India's foreign policy — its security doctrine, its approach to Pakistan, its engagement with international institutions, its commitment to secular nationalism, and its pursuit of strategic autonomy through non-alignment.
II. Evidence Summaries
evidence-summary b-evidence-trauma · depth: scholar, researcher
Claim: Partition's violence constituted a foundational trauma that permanently shaped India's security consciousness
The mass migration of 14.5 million people, the death of 800,000 to 1 million, and the abduction of 100,000 women created an existential security crisis that defined India's founding moment. The collapse of civil administration, the communalisation of the police and army, and the sheer scale of human catastrophe convinced India's founding elite that national survival required a strong centralised state, a unified military command, and a security doctrine that treated the Pakistan border as the primary strategic challenge.

evidence-summary b-evidence-kashmir · depth: scholar, researcher
Claim: India's decision to refer the Kashmir dispute to the UN Security Council internationalised a bilateral conflict and created a permanent diplomatic vulnerability
On 1 January 1948, India invoked Article 35 of the UN Charter. Nehru believed international law would vindicate India's position. Instead, Resolution 47 placed India and Pakistan on equal footing as disputants, called for a plebiscite, and effectively treated Kashmir's accession as provisional. This is widely regarded by Indian strategic analysts as one of independent India's most consequential diplomatic errors.

evidence-summary b-evidence-demographic · depth: scholar, researcher
Claim: The mass migration of 14.5 million people constituted the largest forced migration of the twentieth century and permanently transformed the demographic landscape of South Asia
Between August and December 1947, approximately 14.5 million people crossed the new borders. The 1951 Census documented the transformation: West Punjab's non-Muslim population fell from 16% to less than 1%; East Punjab's Muslim population fell from 33% to less than 5%.

Claim: India's constitutional secularism was forged in direct opposition to the Two-Nation theory and became the foundational principle of both domestic governance and foreign policy
The Indian Constitution adopted a distinct model of secularism that required the state to treat all religions equally. In foreign policy, this became a diplomatic asset, particularly in relations with Muslim-majority nations. Nehru understood that a Hindu nationalist India would lack credibility with the Muslim world.

III. Part I: The End of Empire
paragraph b-p-empire-1 · b-p-empire-2 · b-p-empire-3 · b-p-empire-4 · b-p-empire-5 · b-p-empire-6
Explains the collapse of the British Indian Empire after WWII: Britain's financial exhaustion (Keynes's "financial Dunkirk"), the US termination of Lend-Lease, Attlee's Labour government's commitment to decolonisation, the Royal Indian Navy mutiny of February 1946, the INA trials, and the breakdown of British administrative control. The British withdrew in 1947 not because they chose to, but because they could no longer afford to stay.
IV. Part II: Why Did Partition Happen?
A. Explanations: Imperial Strategy
British 'divide and rule' policies from the 1909 Morley-Minto Reforms through the 1919 Government of India Act, the Communal Award of 1932. The British systematically favoured Muslim separatism as a counterweight to the nationalist movement.
B. Explanations: Congress Decisions
Congress's rejection of the Cabinet Mission Plan, Nehru's refusal to accept the grouping arrangement, the Muslim League's entry into the Interim Government, and the breakdown of the coalition. The Congress's insistence on a strong central state versus the League's demand for provincial autonomy.
C. Explanations: Muslim League Politics
Jinnah's strategy, the Lahore Resolution of 1940, the League's use of Direct Action Day (16 August 1946), and the transformation of the Pakistan demand from bargaining counter to non-negotiable objective after the Great Calcutta Killing.
D. Explanations: Communal Violence
The Great Calcutta Killing, the Noakhali riots, the Bihar massacres, and the Rawalpindi violence. The violence was not spontaneous but organized, with political and communal organizations executing attacks.
E. Explanations: Administrative Collapse
Yasmin Khan's argument that the British administrative capacity collapsed in 1946-47, enabling the violence and making an orderly transfer of power impossible. The 'everyday state' — policing, food distribution, railways — broke down.
F. Explanations: Strategic Pressure
Mountbatten's accelerated timetable (June 1947 rather than June 1948), the Radcliffe commission's five-week deadline, and the withdrawal of British troops created the conditions for catastrophe. The British prioritized a quick exit over a just settlement.

X. Historiography: How Scholars Debate Partition
historiography b-historiography · depth: scholar, researcher
Six approaches:
1. Nationalist Historiography (Chandra, Gopal) — Partition was the inevitable result of British divide-and-rule and Muslim League communalism. Congress wanted unity but was forced to accept Partition by British machinations.
2. Revisionist / Cambridge School (Jalal, Seal) — Partition resulted from elite political competition, not mass communal sentiment. Jinnah used Pakistan as a bargaining counter; Congress miscalculations made it real.
3. Subaltern / Social History (Pandey, Khan, Chatterji) — Centres the experience of ordinary people. Examines violence, everyday life, and how ordinary Indians experienced the division.
4. Gender and Feminist Historiography (Butalia, Menon, Bhasin) — Recovers women's experiences of Partition: abduction, rape, recovery operations. Reveals the state's patriarchal assumptions in recovery operations.
5. Pakistani Nationalist Historiography (Sayeed, Ahmed) — Partition as legitimate Muslim self-determination. Pakistan as the culmination of Muslim nationalism. Less critical of the League's role.
6. Strategic / International Historiography (Raghavan, Raja Mohan, Cohen, Ganguly) — Views Partition through geopolitical and military lens. Assesses the strategic consequences for India's foreign policy.

XXII. Misconceptions (21 total)
Examples:
1. Partition was inevitable by 1940. → Correction: Not inevitable until at least early 1947.
2. Hindus and Muslims had always lived in irreconcilable conflict. → Correction: Coexistence was the historical norm.
3. Only Muslims supported the Muslim League. → Correction: Some Hindu landowners in Sindh and Bengal supported the League.
4. Gandhi could have prevented Partition if he had tried harder. → Correction: Gandhi's influence had declined significantly by 1946-47.
5. Pakistan was created as a secular state. → Correction: Pakistan was explicitly created as a homeland for Indian Muslims.
