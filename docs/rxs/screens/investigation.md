# Investigation Experience

> **RXS v1.0 (Frozen)** — Part of the Reader Experience System. Do not modify outside the governance process defined in `README.md`.

## Goal

Design the surface where readers interrogate complex issues through evidence — not narrative. An Investigation is not a long story. It is a structured case file organised around a central question. The reader follows the evidence, compares competing explanations, understands what is known and what is not, and reaches their own informed conclusion. The institution's findings are presented transparently, but the reader is never asked to accept them on authority.

## Reader Intent

**Understand allegations.** The reader has heard a claim — "Namami Gange failed to meet its targets" — and wants to evaluate it against evidence. They are not looking for a conclusion. They are looking for the evidence behind the conclusion.

**Follow evidence.** The reader wants to see every piece of evidence, its provenance, its confidence score, and how it relates to the central question. They are building their own understanding from the ground up.

**Verify sources.** The reader encounters a cited source and wants to inspect it directly. Primary document, witness testimony, government audit — they want the raw material, not the summary.

**Understand chronology.** The reader wants to see what happened and when. Events, decisions, reports, and their sequence. They need a timeline that connects evidence to dates.

**Identify uncertainty.** The reader wants to know what is not known. Where evidence is missing, contradictory, or inconclusive. They are evaluating the reliability of the investigation itself.

**Reach informed conclusions.** The reader wants to decide what they believe based on the balance of evidence. The investigation equips them to do so but does not force a verdict.

**Track updates.** The reader has already read the investigation. They return because new evidence was added, a finding was revised, or an open question was resolved.

**Report new evidence.** The reader has information relevant to the investigation. They want a path to contribute it to the evidence record.

## Investigation Lifecycle

**Question.** Every investigation begins with a question — not a thesis. The question defines the scope. "What were the outcomes of the Namami Gange program?" is an investigation. "Why Namami Gange failed" is a thesis masquerading as an investigation. The question is visible at the top of the investigation as long as it exists. It is the reader's north star.

**Orientation.** The reader arrives at the investigation. Before they engage with evidence, they need: what question this investigation asks, why it matters, what the key known facts are, and what the competing explanations are. Orientation gives the reader a mental model for the evidence that follows. Without orientation, every evidence block lands without context.

**Evidence Collection.** The reader explores the evidence blocks. Each block is self-contained: one claim, its supporting sources, its confidence score, its evidence level (documented fact, inference, hypothesis, speculation, editorial judgment). Evidence blocks are organised by category — financial evidence, documentary evidence, testimonial evidence, statistical evidence. The reader moves through evidence by category, not by narrative.

**Verification.** The reader opens a source and verifies it independently. They inspect the primary document, examine provenance, check the confidence score methodology. Verification is the moment the reader decides whether to trust the evidence. The investigation must never ask the reader to trust without the means to verify.

**Alternative Explanations.** The reader encounters competing explanations for the same evidence. The investigation explicitly presents "Explanation A" and "Explanation B" with the evidence that supports each. This is not a pro-con list. It is an honest accounting of where evidence is ambiguous and reasonable people disagree.

**Findings.** The investigation presents its findings — what the balance of evidence supports, what it refutes, and what remains unresolved. Findings are presented with their supporting evidence visible in the same view. A finding without linked evidence is not a finding. It is an assertion.

**Open Questions.** The investigation lists what it does not know. Open questions are not buried in a footnote. They are a named section visible alongside findings. An investigation that claims certainty where evidence is incomplete has failed.

**Updates.** The investigation records every change since publication. New evidence added, findings revised, corrections issued. Updates are logged with date, nature of change, and impact on findings. The update log is visible from the investigation landing. A reader who returns after six months can see exactly what changed.

## Investigation Anatomy

**Case Overview.** One paragraph describing the investigation's scope and significance. Answers: what question, why it matters, and what is at stake. Exists so the reader can decide whether to invest time before engaging with evidence.

**Executive Summary.** Key findings in brief — what the investigation concluded, what remains contested, and what the institution recommends as next steps. Exists for the reader who needs the answer now and the evidence later.

**Key Questions.** The specific questions the investigation seeks to answer. Three to seven precise questions. Every evidence block maps to at least one question. Exists so the reader can navigate directly to the question they care about.

**Known Facts.** What is uncontested. Facts that all parties agree on and that are verified by multiple independent sources. Exists to establish common ground before entering contested territory.

**Evidence.** The body of the investigation. Self-contained evidence blocks organised by category. Each block contains: the claim, the source, the provenance, the confidence score, the evidence level (documented fact, inference, hypothesis, speculation, editorial judgment), and links to related evidence. Exists as the primary surface for reader interrogation.

**Primary Documents.** Full facsimiles or transcripts of original documents cited in the evidence blocks. Organised by document, not by evidence block. Exists so the reader can verify claims at the source.

**Timeline.** Chronological view of all events referenced in the investigation. Each event links to the evidence that establishes it. Exists so the reader can understand sequence — what happened before what, what decisions led to what outcomes.

**Actors.** Profiles of every person, organisation, and institution referenced. Each profile contains: role in the investigation, relationship to other actors, relevant documents, and evidence blocks involving this actor. Exists so the reader can track who did what and who said what.

**Money Flow.** Financial relationships — budget allocations, expenditures, contracts, audits. Visualised as flow diagrams where possible. Exists for investigations involving government spending, corporate finance, or economic impacts.

**Relationships.** How actors connect to each other and to the evidence. Organisational hierarchies, contractual relationships, personal connections. Visualised as relationship graphs. Exists so the reader can see the structure beneath the events.

**Competing Explanations.** Alternative interpretations of the evidence, presented with their supporting evidence and the reasons reasonable people hold them. Each explanation is attributed to its proponents. Exists so the reader understands where certainty ends and judgment begins.

**Findings.** What the balance of evidence supports. Each finding links to the evidence blocks that justify it. Findings are labelled by confidence: Established, Probable, Contested, Inconclusive. Exists as the institution's transparent conclusion — presented as a starting point, not a verdict.

**Open Questions.** What remains unknown. Each open question describes what evidence is missing and what would resolve it. Exists to honour the reader's intelligence and the truth's complexity.

**Methodology.** How the investigation was conducted — search methodology, source selection criteria, confidence scoring rubric, verification process, peer review. Exists so the reader can evaluate the investigation's rigour independently of its findings.

**Sources.** Complete list of every source cited, with provenance, access date, and archival location where applicable. Each source links to its evidence blocks. Exists as the permanent evidentiary record.

**Corrections.** Every correction issued, with the original error, the corrected state, the date, and the reason. Corrections are permanent and visible. Exists because transparency about error is more important than the appearance of infallibility.

**Version History.** Every version of the investigation, with the date, nature of changes, and impact on findings. Exists so the reader can track the investigation's evolution over time.

## Evidence Philosophy

**Documented Fact.** A claim verified by multiple independent primary sources with complete provenance. The confidence score is 90% or higher. The reader can verify the fact themselves by inspecting the cited sources. Documented facts are the foundation of the investigation. They are not contested, and they are not debatable within the bounds of reasonable scholarship.

**Inference.** A logical conclusion drawn from documented facts but not directly evidenced. The chain of reasoning is explicit. "The documents show expenditure of X and completion of Y; therefore, the average cost per unit was Z" is an inference if Z is not directly stated in any source. Inferences are labelled and attributed. The reader can accept or challenge the reasoning independently.

**Hypothesis.** A proposed explanation that is consistent with available evidence but not yet verified. A hypothesis identifies what additional evidence would confirm or refute it. Hypotheses are explicitly provisional. They exist to guide further investigation, not to fill gaps.

**Speculation.** Informed conjecture about what may have happened or what may be true, based on partial evidence or analogy to known cases. Speculation is explicitly labelled as such and separated from evidence-backed claims. It appears only in context where the reader can clearly distinguish it from documented fact.

**Editorial Judgment.** The institution's interpretation of the balance of evidence. "On balance, the evidence suggests..." Editorial judgment is the institution's voice, presented transparently and as one perspective among the competing explanations. It is never presented as the final word.

**Why this separation matters.** An investigation that conflates these five categories asks the reader to trust without understanding. When a documented fact and an editorial judgment share the same presentation, the reader cannot distinguish what is verified from what is asserted. The separation is not a nicety. It is the epistemological foundation of the investigation. Every evidence block carries exactly one label. No block has more than one. The label is visible before the content.

## Reader Decisions

**Open evidence.** The reader clicks on a claim to see its full evidence block — source, provenance, confidence score, evidence level. The default view shows the claim with a confidence badge. One action opens the supporting evidence.

**Compare claims.** The reader selects two or more evidence blocks and views them side by side — competing sources, different confidence scores, conflicting findings. The comparison view highlights discrepancies. Exists for the reader evaluating contested territory.

**Jump to actor.** The reader clicks on any named actor and sees their full profile — role, documents, evidence blocks, relationships. One action from any mention.

**Inspect document.** The reader opens a primary document facsimile or transcript. Full provenance visible. The reader verifies the claim against the original source.

**View timeline.** The reader opens the chronological view of all events. Events are filterable by actor, category, and confidence level. The reader places events in sequence.

**View money flow.** The reader opens the financial visualisation. Expenditure flows, budget allocations, and financial relationships. The reader follows the money.

**View relationships.** The reader opens the actor relationship graph. Organisational connections, contractual links, and personal relationships visible. The reader sees the structure.

**Open competing explanation.** The reader switches from evidence blocks to alternative explanations. Each explanation displayed with its supporting evidence and its proponents. The reader evaluates the case for each explanation.

**Track updates.** The reader subscribes to updates on the investigation. They are notified when evidence is added, findings revised, or open questions resolved. The reader maintains a relationship with the investigation over time.

**Bookmark.** The reader saves the investigation or a specific evidence block for later reference. Position is preserved.

**Report new evidence.** The reader has information relevant to the investigation — a document, a source, personal knowledge. They submit it through a structured form. The submission enters the institution's evidence review pipeline. The reader's contribution is acknowledged.

**Request correction.** The reader believes an error exists in the investigation. They flag it with a specific reference to the claim or evidence block. The request enters the corrections workflow.

**Export evidence block.** The reader extracts a specific evidence block with full source trail — claim, evidence, sources, provenance — for use in their own work. Export format includes citation metadata.

**Share investigation.** The reader shares the investigation with a specific context — "read this for the evidence on X." The shared link preserves the reader's context.

**Return after update.** The reader who has been notified of an update returns to the investigation. They are shown what changed on their first view.

## Living Investigation

**Draft.** Research in progress. Not publicly visible. The question is defined, evidence collection has begun, but no findings have been reached. Transition to Active occurs when the first evidence block is published.

**Active.** Published and actively gathering evidence. New evidence is being collected, sources identified, and findings may change as the evidence base grows. Active is the default state for new investigations. The reader sees "Active — New evidence being gathered" on the investigation landing.

**Evidence Added.** New evidence has been incorporated since the reader's last visit. The investigation may have new evidence blocks, revised findings, or updated confidence scores. Transition occurs when new evidence is published. The reader is notified of what changed and which findings were affected.

**Updated.** Existing evidence has been re-reviewed, sources refreshed, or the investigation restructured. No new findings, but the evidence base has been maintained. Transition occurs on a scheduled verification cycle or when a source is re-accessed. The investigation's "Last Verified" date reflects the update.

**Closed.** No active evidence gathering. Stable findings. The investigation has reached a conclusion that the institution stands behind. Transition occurs when the institution determines that further evidence collection would not materially change the findings, or when the question has been resolved by external events. Closed does not mean frozen — corrections and updates are still possible but not actively sought.

**Archived.** Historical record. The investigation is preserved as a snapshot of what was known at a point in time. No further updates expected. Transition occurs when the question is no longer relevant, the subject is historical rather than contemporary, or the investigation is superseded by a more comprehensive one. The archived investigation remains accessible with a clear banner: "Archived — This investigation is no longer actively maintained."

**Why living.** Investigations deal with complex reality. New evidence emerges. Sources are refreshed. Findings are refined. A static investigation is a snapshot of what the institution knew at one point in time. A living investigation is the institution's ongoing commitment to the truth as it evolves. The reader needs to see not just what the institution knows now, but how that knowledge has changed.

## Trust Signals

**Primary document facsimiles.** Every major claim links to a scanned or transcribed primary document. The reader can verify the claim against the original source. A document is worth more than a citation.

**Evidence count.** Total number of evidence blocks in the investigation. Visible at the top of the investigation. A higher count signals thoroughness. A lower count signals a focused scope. Either is acceptable. The number is not a quality signal — it is a transparency signal.

**Contradictory evidence count.** Number of evidence blocks that support each competing explanation visible alongside each explanation. The reader sees at a glance whether the evidence is one-sided or contested.

**Evidence-to-finding ratio.** Average number of evidence blocks supporting each finding. A ratio below 3:1 triggers a confidence warning. The ratio is visible in the methodology section.

**Open question count.** Number of unresolved questions. Visible at the top of the investigation alongside evidence count. A non-zero open question count is a signal of rigour, not weakness. It means the institution acknowledges uncertainty.

**Last verified date.** The date every evidence block was last verified against its source. Visible on the investigation landing and on each evidence block. The reader can assess freshness at a glance.

**External review count.** Number of external experts who reviewed the investigation. Visible in the methodology section. Each reviewer's disciplinary perspective is listed. Cross-disciplinary review increases confidence.

**Source diversity index.** Number of independent sources — categorised by type (government, academic, journalistic, archival, testimonial). Visible in the methodology section. A diverse source base is harder to challenge than a narrow one.

**Correction velocity.** Average time between error identification and correction publication. Visible in the corrections section. A low correction velocity signals organisational responsiveness to error.

**Evidence level distribution.** The percentage of evidence blocks at each level — documented fact, inference, hypothesis, speculation, editorial judgment. Visible in the methodology section. A high proportion of documented facts signals a rigorous evidence base.

**Confidence score average.** Average confidence score across all evidence blocks. Visible on the investigation landing. A score above 80% is strong, 60–80% is moderate, below 60% suggests precaution.

**Competing explanation parity.** Whether the investigation presents at least two competing explanations when the evidence supports more than one interpretation. Signalled by a label: "Multiple explanations presented" or "Single explanation available."

**Methodology access count.** Number of readers who viewed the methodology. Visible to the institution only. A high methodology view rate signals a reader base that cares about rigour.

**Version count and latest version.** Current version number and total versions. Visible on the investigation landing. A version history is a commitment to transparency.

**Corrections count.** Total corrections issued. Visible on the investigation landing. Zero corrections on a long-running investigation may suggest insufficient scrutiny. Non-zero is healthy.

## Failure Modes

1. **Thesis masquerading as question.** The investigation starts with a conclusion and backfills evidence. The reader encounters a closed finding before an open question.

2. **Buried uncertainty.** Open questions relegated to a footnote or omitted entirely. The investigation claims more certainty than the evidence supports.

3. **False equivalence.** Competing explanations presented as equally valid when the evidence strongly favours one. False equivalence masquerades as balance.

4. **Evidence-free narrative.** The investigation tells a story without linking every claim to evidence blocks. The reader cannot distinguish prose from evidence.

5. **Single-source conclusions.** A finding supported by only one source, presented without caveat. The reader cannot assess the evidentiary foundation.

6. **Confidence without methodology.** The investigation assigns confidence scores but does not explain how they are calculated. The reader cannot evaluate the scoring.

7. **Prosecutorial tone.** The investigation reads as an indictment. Language that signals guilt before evidence. The reader's conclusion is pre-framed.

8. **Apologetic tone.** The investigation reads as a defence. Language that signals innocence before evidence. The reader's conclusion is pre-framed.

9. **Static document.** The investigation never updates. The reader returns to find the same evidence even when the world has changed. The investigation is a fossil.

10. **Silent revision.** Evidence or findings changed without notice. The reader who returns sees a different investigation and cannot determine what changed.

11. **Missing actors.** Key people or organisations referenced without profiles. The reader cannot investigate the actors behind the evidence.

12. **No timeline.** Events discussed without chronological structure. The reader cannot place evidence in sequence.

13. **Blocked verification.** Primary documents referenced but not accessible. The reader cannot verify claims independently.

14. **Undisclosed methodology.** The investigation presents findings but never explains how it arrived at them. The reader cannot evaluate rigour.

15. **Merged evidence levels.** Documented facts and editorial judgments presented identically. The reader cannot distinguish what is verified from what is asserted.

16. **No update notifications.** The investigation changes but the reader is not informed. The reader operates on outdated evidence.

17. **Correction opacity.** Errors corrected but the original error and correction not logged. The reader cannot see what changed or why.

18. **Disappeared evidence.** Evidence blocks removed without explanation. The reader who remembers a specific piece of evidence cannot find it.

19. **Reader evidence ignored.** Reader-submitted evidence enters a black hole. No acknowledgement, no update, no explanation of what was done with it.

20. **Unsignalled archive.** An archived investigation presented without the archive banner. The reader treats yesterday's knowledge as today's.

## Success Metrics

1. **Evidence block open rate.** Percentage of readers who open at least one evidence block. Measures whether the evidence layer is discoverable and compelling.

2. **Primary document view rate.** Percentage of readers who open at least one primary document. Measures depth of verification behaviour.

3. **Competing explanation view rate.** Percentage of readers who view at least one alternative explanation. Measures engagement with contested territory.

4. **Open question view rate.** Percentage of readers who view the open questions section. Measures whether uncertainty is acknowledged and explored.

5. **Timeline interaction rate.** Percentage of readers who interact with the timeline. Measures chronological engagement.

6. **Actor profile view rate.** Percentage of readers who view at least one actor profile from within the investigation. Measures character/entity tracking.

7. **Time on evidence vs. time on narrative.** Ratio of time spent on evidence blocks to time spent on introductory or summary sections. Measures whether the reader engages with the evidentiary core.

8. **Return rate after update.** Percentage of readers who return to the investigation within 7 days of an update notification. Measures whether the living-investigation model retains readers.

9. **Evidence export rate.** Percentage of readers who export an evidence block. Measures whether the investigation is used as a research resource.

10. **Reader evidence submission rate.** Number of reader-submitted evidence items per investigation. Measures whether the reader community participates in evidence gathering.

11. **Correction request rate.** Number of correction requests per investigation. A non-zero rate is healthy. Zero on a complex investigation may signal reader disengagement.

12. **Correction velocity.** Average time from error identification to correction publication. Measures institutional responsiveness.

13. **Methodology view rate.** Percentage of readers who open the methodology section. Measures whether readers care about epistemic rigour.

14. **Evidence-to-finding ratio per investigation.** Average number of evidence blocks per finding. A ratio below 3:1 triggers editorial review.

15. **Archived investigation access rate.** Percentage of archival accesses relative to active investigation accesses. Measures whether archived knowledge retains value.

## Immutable Rules

1. Never present allegations as facts. Every claim about what someone may have done carries its evidence level label before the reader engages with the claim.

2. Every conclusion links to evidence. A finding without a linked evidence block is not a finding. It is an assertion. The link is visible before the finding text.

3. Contradictory evidence is always visible. If evidence supports more than one explanation, all evidence is surfaced — not just the evidence that supports the institution's preferred explanation.

4. Open questions are never hidden. The open questions section is navigable from the investigation landing. It is not a footnote. It is not in the appendix.

5. Corrections are permanent. A correction creates a permanent record showing the original error, the corrected state, the date, and the reason. Corrections are never deleted.

6. Evidence is never removed. It may be superseded by newer evidence, labelled as outdated, or corrected if the source was misrepresented. It is never deleted from the record.

7. Every evidence block carries exactly one evidence level label. No block is labelled as both "Documented Fact" and "Inference." The label is determined at publication and may be revised only through the corrections process.

8. The investigation question is immutable once published. The scope of the investigation may be refined, but the central question cannot change without creating a new investigation version.

9. Methodology is always accessible. The methodology does not require the reader to reach the end of the investigation. It is navigable from the investigation landing.

10. Reader-submitted evidence enters a visible pipeline. The reader who submits evidence receives an acknowledgement. The evidence's status — received, under review, reviewed, incorporated — is visible to the submitter.

11. Actor profiles exist for every named entity referenced more than once. If a person or organisation is significant enough to name in multiple evidence blocks, they are significant enough to profile.

12. Timeline covers every dated event referenced in evidence blocks. If evidence establishes an event, the event appears on the timeline. No event is discussed without chronological placement.

13. Version history is never lost. Every version is preserved and accessible. The reader can view the investigation as it existed at any previous version.

14. Confidence scores are always shown for evidence blocks. A block without a score defaults to "Unscored — Editorially Reviewed" rather than assuming maximum confidence.

15. The investigation's current status is visible on every page within the investigation. The reader never has to scroll to the investigation landing to determine whether the investigation is Active, Evidence Added, Updated, Closed, or Archived.
