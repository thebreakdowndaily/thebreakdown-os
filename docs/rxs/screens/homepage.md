# Homepage Experience

> **RXS v1.0 (Frozen)** — Part of the Reader Experience System. Do not modify outside the governance process defined in `README.md`.

## Goal

Design the front door to a digital knowledge institution. The homepage is not a news feed, not a marketing landing page, and not a catalogue. It is the reader's first encounter with the institution — and the returning reader's anchor. Its purpose is to help readers begin learning. Everything else is subordinate.

## Reader Intent

**Start learning.** The reader is new. They have heard of The Breakdown and want to understand what it offers and whether it is worth their time. The homepage must orient, establish trust, and provide a clear starting point — all within the time it takes to scan one screen.

**Continue learning.** The reader has been here before. They have an in-progress chapter, a volume they are working through, or a thinker they are following. The homepage must return them to context. A return that requires reorientation is a failure.

**Explore collections.** The reader wants to see what knowledge exists. They are browsing breadth — scanning collections, volumes, and topics. The homepage must reveal the scope of the institution without requiring the reader to enter every collection to understand it.

**Find trusted analysis.** The reader has a specific question — "What does The Breakdown say about India's nuclear doctrine?" — and wants to know whether the institution has addressed it. The homepage must surface search prominently while making clear that the answer may exist in a story, an investigation, a timeline, or a thinker profile.

**Search for a topic.** The reader knows what they want. The homepage is not their destination — it is their entry point. The search bar must work, and the path from search to content must be immediate.

**Resume previous reading.** The reader returns after days or weeks. They remember reading something but not where it was. The homepage must surface their recent history and bookmarked positions.

**Evaluate the institution.** The reader is a journalist, academic, or policy professional deciding whether to cite or recommend The Breakdown. They need to assess rigour, transparency, and scope. The homepage must surface institutional signals without overwhelming the casual reader.

## First 30 Seconds

Within the first 10 seconds, a new visitor must understand: what institution this is, what it produces, and whether it is trustworthy. The institution's name and tagline are visible immediately. Trust signals — chapters published, claims registered, last verified — are visible without scrolling. The reader can answer "What is this?" and "Why should I trust it?" before they have read a full paragraph.

Within the next 10 seconds, the visitor must see where to begin. A single primary path — "Start with Volume I" or "Begin the featured investigation" — is the most prominent action on the page. The reader can answer "Where should I start?" without evaluating multiple options.

Within the final 10 seconds, the visitor must see what knowledge exists. The Library preview shows the scope of collections, featured volumes, and recent investigations. The reader can answer "What can I learn?" and see the breadth of the institution.

After 30 seconds, the reader has either chosen a path or the homepage has failed. The homepage does not ask for more time.

## Homepage Lifecycle

**Arrival.** The reader lands. The page loads with one priority: communicate identity within the first second. Name, tagline, and institutional purpose. The first visual impression is informational, not decorative. No hero image that delays comprehension. No animation that competes with identity.

**Recognition.** The reader recognises the institution. For a first-time visitor, this means understanding what The Breakdown produces — evidence-based knowledge libraries, not news, not opinion, not aggregated headlines. For a returning visitor, recognition means seeing their own context — their name, their progress, their history. Recognition answers "Do I know this place?" and "Does it know me?"

**Trust.** The reader encounters evidence of rigour before they encounter content recommendations. Trust signals — Institutional Trust Score, chapters published, claims registered, last verified — are the second thing the reader sees after identity. Trust must precede engagement because a reader who does not trust the source cannot properly evaluate the knowledge.

**Orientation.** The reader sees where they are in the institution. The primary path — the recommended starting point — is the most visible action. The Library preview shows the scope of knowledge. The reader can answer "Where am I?" and "Where can I go?" before making a decision.

**Decision.** The reader chooses a path — start the primary volume, resume an in-progress chapter, browse a collection, search for a topic, or evaluate the institution's governance. The homepage's job is to make this decision obvious and easy. The decision is single — one click, one path. Choice paralysis is the homepage's greatest risk.

**Learning.** The reader has left the homepage and entered a Story, Investigation, or Library. The homepage has completed its job. Learning happens elsewhere. The homepage does not attempt to teach — it only helps the reader decide where to begin.

**Return.** The reader comes back. The homepage must recognise the return, surface what changed, and return the reader to their context. A return visit that looks identical to a first visit is a failure. The return stage is the most important lifecycle stage because it determines whether the reader's relationship with the institution deepens or ends.

## Canonical Sections

**Institution Introduction.** The top of the page. Name, tagline, and one-sentence description. Exists so the reader knows what institution they are on within one second. This is the only section that appears on every visit without variation. It is the homepage's anchor.

**Trust Bar.** Trust signals visible in the first viewport. Institutional Trust Score, chapters published, claims registered, primary sources cited, last verified date. Exists so the reader encounters evidence of rigour before any content. This section exists because trust precedes engagement.

**Primary Path.** A single recommended starting point for a first-time reader. "Start with Volume I: Foundations (1947–1962)." A clear call to action with a brief explanation of why this is the best place to begin. Exists to eliminate choice paralysis. There is always exactly one primary path.

**Continue Learning.** A personalised section for returning readers showing in-progress chapters, recent bookmarks, and followed collections. Exists so the returning reader is returned to context. This section is empty for first-time visitors.

**Knowledge Collections.** A curated view of the Library's breadth — featured collections displayed with their title, scope, and trust score. Three to five collections. Exists so the reader can see what knowledge exists without entering the full Library. This is a preview, not a catalogue.

**Featured Investigations.** Ongoing investigations displayed with their central question, status, and evidence count. Exists so readers know the institution investigates as well as narrates. Investigations differ from collections in structure and intent; this section signals that difference.

**Latest Research.** Recently published chapters, updated volumes, and fresh evidence. Each item includes a date and a one-line summary. Exists so returning readers see that the institution maintains its knowledge.

**Explore by Topic.** A curated list of topics — non-proliferation, federalism, monetary policy — each linking to the relevant chapters across collections. Exists so the reader who thinks in topics rather than collections can find their entry point.

**Thinkers.** Featured thinker profiles — Nehru, Patel, Ambedkar — with a link to the full thinkers index. Exists so the reader who learns through individuals can enter through a person rather than a topic.

**Learning Paths.** Curated sequences of volumes and chapters for systematic study. "Understanding India's Foreign Policy: A Learning Path." Exists for readers who want a curriculum, not a collection.

**Primary Documents.** Featured primary sources with provenance summaries. Exists so the reader who values original evidence can enter through documents.

**Newsletter.** A sign-up invitation with a clear statement of what subscribers receive. Exists as an optional connection point, not a primary call to action.

**Footer.** Links to the Editorial Constitution, methodology, trust dashboard, corrections policy, about page, and AGENTS.md. Exists so the reader who wants to evaluate the institution can find every governance document from any page.

## Reader Decisions

**Start the primary path.** The reader clicks the single recommended starting point. This is the default action for first-time visitors. The decision requires no evaluation — only trust that the institution's recommendation is well-founded.

**Resume reading.** The returning reader clicks their in-progress chapter. The most important decision for returning visitors. The homepage must surface this decision before any other.

**Browse collections.** The reader enters the Knowledge Library. They are moving from orientation to exploration. The decision signals that the reader wants to understand the scope of the institution before committing to a specific path.

**Explore investigations.** The reader enters an investigation. They are choosing depth over breadth — a single complex issue examined through evidence.

**Search.** The reader uses the search bar. They have a specific target. The homepage's role shifts from orienting to getting out of the way.

**Follow a topic.** The reader selects a topic tag and views all chapters across all collections that address it. The decision signals topic-first learning.

**Explore a learning path.** The reader starts a curated sequence. They are committing to systematic study. The decision signals that the reader trusts the institution to guide their learning.

**Subscribe to the newsletter.** The reader opts into an ongoing relationship. The decision signals that the reader values the institution enough to invite it into their inbox.

**View governance documents.** The reader opens the Editorial Constitution, methodology, or trust dashboard. The decision signals institutional evaluation. This is a decision the homepage must make easy because it is the reader's due diligence.

**Give feedback.** The reader reports an issue, suggests an improvement, or challenges a finding. The decision signals that the reader cares about the institution's quality.

## Trust Signals

**Institutional Trust Score.** A single number from 0–100, displayed prominently in the trust bar. Weighted composite of evidence quality, primary source ratio, review completion, freshness, correction quality, transparency, reader trust, and expert review. Computed automatically from the canonical data layer. Exists because a single verifiable number is more credible than a paragraph of claims about rigour.

**Chapters published count.** Total published chapters across all collections. Links to the Library for verification. Concrete proof of output.

**Claims registered count.** Total claims backed by verified evidence. Links to the trust dashboard. Proof of evidentiary depth.

**Primary sources cited count.** Total primary sources. Links to the trust dashboard. Proof of source-based methodology.

**Last verified date.** The date of the most recent verification cycle. Signals living knowledge.

**Editorial Constitution version.** Current version with link to full text. Signals that the institution operates under published, versioned standards.

**Methodology link.** Persistent link explaining how research is conducted, how claims are verified, and how confidence scores are assigned. Signals transparency about process.

**Trust dashboard link.** Persistent link to the public transparency dashboard displaying all institutional metrics. Signals that the institution publishes its performance data.

**Open corrections link.** Link to the corrections log showing all issued corrections. Signals that the institution corrects its errors transparently.

**Evidence freshness by collection.** Each collection in the Library preview displays its last-verified date. Signals that staleness is visible and managed.

## Personalisation Philosophy

**Principles over algorithms.** The homepage adapts based on the reader's own signals — their history, their bookmarks, their followed topics — never based on aggregate behaviour, engagement metrics, or demographic profiling. Personalisation is a service to the reader, not a tool for the institution.

**First-time readers see the canon.** A new visitor sees the same homepage as every other new visitor. The primary path is the same. The featured collections are the same. The institutional introduction is the same. The first experience is curated, not personalised. The reader earns personalisation through their actions.

**Returning readers see their context.** A returning reader sees their in-progress chapters, recent bookmarks, and followed collections before anything else. Personalisation means returning the reader to context, not recommending content based on past behaviour.

**Researchers see depth.** A reader who consistently opens evidence panels, views primary documents, and exports evidence blocks is a researcher. The homepage may surface document updates, new evidence added to investigations, and methodological changes. The homepage adapts to depth of engagement, not volume of visits.

**Students see paths.** A reader who follows learning paths, completes chapters sequentially, and uses the glossary is a student. The homepage may surface the next chapter in their learning path, review materials, and related learning paths. The homepage supports systematic study.

**No algorithmic recommendations.** The homepage never displays "readers like you also read" or "trending now" or "popular this week." Recommendations are editorially curated or derived from the reader's own signals. The institution does not optimise for engagement.

**Reader control.** The reader can dismiss sections, hide personalisation, or reset to the canonical first-time view. Personalisation is transparent and reversible.

## Failure Modes

1. **No identity.** The reader cannot determine what institution they are on within the first five seconds. The brand, purpose, and genre are ambiguous.

2. **News-site resemblance.** The homepage looks like a newspaper. The reader assumes it is a news site and expects daily headlines rather than permanent knowledge.

3. **Marketing tone.** The homepage reads like a startup landing page. Value propositions, buzzwords, and claims without evidence. The reader detects a sales pitch and leaves.

4. **Choice paralysis.** Twelve collections, six investigations, and a primary path that is not distinguished from the rest. The reader cannot decide and leaves.

5. **Trust below the fold.** Trust signals require scrolling. The reader encounters content before credibility.

6. **No starting point.** Every option has equal visual weight. The first-time reader has no way to distinguish where to begin.

7. **Identical first and return experience.** The homepage does not recognise returning readers. Every visit is treated as a first visit.

8. **Buried continuing path.** The returning reader's in-progress chapters require clicking through navigation to find. The reader must search for their own context.

9. **Search desperation.** The search bar dominates the page. The homepage gives up on orientation and asks the reader to know what they want before the institution has introduced itself.

10. **Freshness over importance.** The homepage features recent updates over foundational volumes. The reader sees what is new rather than what is essential.

11. **Unverifiable claims.** "Rigorous, evidence-based, trusted by thousands" without links to methodology, trust dashboard, or evidence counts. The reader cannot verify any claim about the institution.

12. **Genre confusion.** Stories, investigations, collections, and learning paths presented with identical visual treatment. The reader cannot distinguish a chapter from a multi-volume series.

13. **Navigation-heavy chrome.** The page has more navigation elements than content. The reader is overwhelmed by choices before they have context.

14. **Broken trust after exploration.** The reader visits the trust dashboard or Editorial Constitution and returns to the homepage. The homepage does not acknowledge what they explored.

15. **Personalisation without disclosure.** The homepage shows personalised content without explaining why. The reader does not know why something is there.

16. **No pathway for feedback.** The reader spots an error on the homepage itself — a broken link, an outdated trust score — and has no way to report it.

17. **Mobile-first failure.** The trust bar occupies the full viewport on mobile. The primary path is below the fold. The reader on a phone cannot make a decision.

18. **Design churn.** The homepage changes its structure, visual language, or information hierarchy every few months. Returning readers must re-learn how to navigate.

19. **Footer-only governance.** The Editorial Constitution, methodology, and trust dashboard are accessible only by scrolling to the bottom. The reader who wants to evaluate the institution must search.

20. **False urgency.** "Limited time," "exclusive," "breaking" language on a knowledge platform. The reader detects temporal manipulation and loses trust.

## Success Metrics

1. **First-visit path selection rate.** Percentage of first-time readers who click a primary path within thirty seconds. Measures whether the homepage answers "Where should I start?"

2. **Return-to-continue rate.** Percentage of returning readers who click directly to their in-progress chapter. Measures whether the continuing-learning section works.

3. **First-visit bounce rate.** Percentage of first-time readers who leave without any interaction. Measures whether the homepage communicates value quickly enough.

4. **Recognition rate.** Percentage of new visitors who, after 30 seconds, can correctly describe what The Breakdown produces. Measured via survey.

5. **Trust comprehension rate.** Percentage of new visitors who, after 30 seconds, can name one reason to trust The Breakdown. Measured via survey.

6. **Library entry rate.** Percentage of homepage visitors who navigate to the full Knowledge Library. Measures whether the Library preview drives depth.

7. **Trust signal interaction rate.** Percentage of visitors who click a trust signal — trust score, constitution, methodology, or trust dashboard. Measures whether institutional signals are discovered.

8. **Search usage rate.** Percentage of visitors who use the search bar. A high rate relative to browsing may indicate that orientation is insufficient.

9. **Time-to-first-action.** Median time from page load to first click. Measures speed of orientation. Target: under 15 seconds.

10. **Return rate (7-day).** Percentage of first-time visitors who return within seven days. Measures whether the first session earned a return.

11. **Path abandonment rate.** Percentage of readers who start a primary path but return to the homepage within 60 seconds. May indicate the wrong path was recommended.

12. **Freshness click rate.** Percentage of returning readers who click a recently updated chapter. Measures whether freshness signals motivate re-engagement.

13. **Learning path start rate.** Percentage of visitors who start a curated learning path from the homepage. Measures whether systematic study paths are discoverable.

14. **Institutional evaluation conversion.** Percentage of visitors who view governance documents and then read a chapter. Measures whether transparency converts to engagement.

15. **Genre accuracy.** Percentage of visitors who, after visiting the homepage, correctly identify that The Breakdown is a knowledge institution rather than a news site or blog. Measured via survey.

## Immutable Rules

1. The homepage never competes with the Story page. The Story page is where learning happens. The homepage is where the reader decides to learn. They have different jobs and different measures of success.

2. There is always one obvious place to begin. A first-time reader must never face a homepage where no option is distinguished as the starting point.

3. Trust appears before persuasion. Trust signals are visible in the first viewport. The reader encounters evidence of rigour before content recommendations.

4. The homepage promotes knowledge, not recency. Foundational volumes and permanent collections outrank recent updates in the information hierarchy. Freshness is a section, not the organising principle.

5. Learning paths outrank chronology. Curated sequences for systematic study are more prominent than chronological content. The institution values understanding over timeliness.

6. Every claim on the homepage is verifiable. If the homepage says "10,000 claims registered," that number links to a verifiable source. No unverifiable assertions appear on the front door.

7. The homepage does not distinguish between signed-in and anonymous readers. Personalisation is based on reader signals — history, bookmarks, follows — not on authentication status. An anonymous returning reader (different device, cleared cookies) sees the canonical first-time view.

8. There is exactly one primary call to action for first-time visitors. Not two. Not three. One.

9. The homepage never auto-plays media. No video, no audio, no animation that starts without reader action. The reader's attention is earned, not taken.

10. The homepage never uses urgency language. No "breaking," "exclusive," "limited time," or "trending." Knowledge is permanent. Temporal manipulation has no place on a knowledge platform.

11. The returning reader's context appears above the fold. In-progress chapters, bookmarks, and followed collections are visible without scrolling.

12. The homepage has no pagination. No "load more" button. No infinite scroll. The homepage is a complete statement within one viewport height. The reader scrolls for secondary information but is never required to scroll for primary decisions.

13. Every section has an explicit reason for existing. If a section cannot justify its presence in thirty words, it is removed.

14. The homepage is the most stable page on the platform. It changes only when the institution's scope or standards change — not on a sprint cadence, not for seasonal campaigns, not for A/B testing.

15. The homepage serves the institution, not the session. Its success is measured in trust and return, not in time on page or click-through rate. A reader who stays on the homepage for five seconds and then starts a volume has succeeded. A reader who scrolls for two minutes and leaves without a path has failed.
