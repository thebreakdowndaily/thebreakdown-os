## 1. HERO

### India–China Border: The Cold Peace Along the Line of Actual Control

**Subtitle:** An Evidence-First Examination of the World's Most Volatile Undefined Border

### Executive Summary

The India–China border dispute is the longest unresolved land border conflict between major powers in the contemporary world. Stretching 3,488 kilometres across the Himalayan arc, the Line of Actual Control (LAC) separates the world's two most populous nations — together representing over 2.8 billion people — along a frontier that has never been formally delimited or demarcated by any bilateral treaty accepted by both sides.

The dispute originated in the colonial cartography of the British Raj, was inflamed by the Chinese annexation of Tibet in 1950, exploded into a brief but devastating war in 1962, and has since festered through cycles of diplomatic engagement and military confrontation. The 2020 Galwan Valley clash — the first combat fatalities on the border since 1975 — fundamentally reset the strategic relationship. As of mid-2026, a "cold peace" prevails: both sides maintain 50,000–60,000 additional troops deployed since 2020, border infrastructure is being built at an unprecedented pace, and diplomatic mechanisms are being tested to their limits.

This monograph examines the dispute across its full historical arc, evaluates the evidence underpinning competing claims, profiles the major schools of interpretation, and assesses the prospects for resolution. It is designed as a public knowledge resource for scholars, policy professionals, journalists, civil service aspirants, and any reader seeking an evidence-first understanding of a conflict that will shape Asian geopolitics for decades to come.

### Six Key Questions

1. **What happened?** A colonial-era boundary, never fully accepted by China, became the flashpoint for a war in 1962 and remains the world's longest unresolved major-power border dispute.

2. **Why did it happen?** The confluence of British imperial cartography, Chinese irredentism, Tibetan statehood questions, Nehru's idealistic diplomacy, Mao's strategic calculus, and the Cold War's penetration of the Himalayas.

3. **What alternatives existed?** A compromise settlement in 1960 (Zhou Enlai's offer of swap), acceptance of the status quo without escalation, or earlier Indian military preparedness that might have deterred Chinese action.

4. **Why did India and China choose this path?** India invested its identity in the McMahon Line as a legacy of independent statehood; China viewed the border as unresolved imperial-era business and used territorial pressure as strategic leverage.

5. **What were the consequences?** 4,000+ casualties in 1962, permanent militarisation of the border, a traumatised Indian strategic psyche, Chinese control of Aksai Chin, and an open wound in Asia's most important bilateral relationship.

6. **Why does it matter today?** Two nuclear-armed powers face each other along an undefined frontier in extreme terrain. A miscalculation could escalate into a conflict with global consequences. The dispute is the single greatest obstacle to Asian strategic integration.

### Why This Matters Today

The India–China border is not a historical curiosity. It is an active, ongoing crisis with the potential for catastrophic escalation. As of July 2026:

- Approximately 100,000–120,000 troops face each other along the LAC (50,000–60,000 additional Indian troops since 2020)
- China has built dual-use infrastructure across Tibet: roads, railways, airfields, and villages
- India is responding with its own infrastructure push: the Arunachal Frontier Highway (1,840 km), Sela Tunnel, Darbuk-Shyok-DBO road, and new railway lines ($3+ billion allocated)
- Both nations possess nuclear weapons and are engaged in simultaneous competition across the Indo-Pacific
- The border dispute is now inextricably linked to broader strategic competition: QUAD, Belt and Road, Indian Ocean access, and supply chain decoupling

Understanding this dispute is not an academic exercise. It is essential knowledge for anyone seeking to comprehend the central geopolitical fault line of the 21st century.

### Academic Consensus

**Areas of broad agreement:**
1. The India-China border was never fully delimited by any treaty accepted by both parties
2. The Simla Convention (1914) was signed by Britain and Tibet but not ratified by China
3. China's annexation of Tibet (1950) fundamentally altered the strategic context
4. The 1962 war was a Chinese military victory but resulted in no permanent settlement
5. The Line of Actual Control does not correspond to any consistent legal principle
6. Both sides' cartographic claims are inconsistent and self-serving

**Areas of continuing disagreement:**
1. The legal validity of the McMahon Line under international law
2. The primary attribution of responsibility for the 1962 war
3. Whether China's position on the border is fundamentally expansionist or defensive
4. The feasibility of converting the LAC into a formal international boundary
5. Whether the Tibet factor is a necessary condition for resolution

### Evidence Gaps

1. **Chinese decision-making in 1962**: No comprehensive account based on Chinese archival sources. The key Politburo meetings (August–October 1962) remain undocumented in accessible archives.

2. **Henderson-Brooks-Bhagat Report**: Full text remains classified by the Indian government. The leaked portions (via Maxwell) cover approximately one-third of the report.

3. **Tibetan perspectives**: The voice of Tibetan authorities and communities is almost entirely absent from the official record. The 1959 Tibetan uprising and its aftermath are documented mainly through exile accounts.

4. **Post-2000 diplomatic negotiations**: The Special Representatives mechanism (est. 2003) has produced no published records. Negotiating positions and trade-offs are inferred from occasional public statements.

5. **LAC alignment maps**: The exchanged maps under the 1993/1996 agreements were never made public. The extent of divergence between Indian and Chinese LAC perceptions is known only to participants.

6. **Military order of battle**: Precise deployment numbers, unit locations, and equipment holdings are unavailable for both sides, though satellite imagery provides partial visibility.

## 3. HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

### British India and the Northern Frontier

The British Raj's approach to the Himalayan frontier was shaped by two competing imperatives: the desire for secure, defensible borders against Russian expansion (the "Great Game"), and the reluctance to assume administrative responsibility for remote, inhospitable territories that offered little economic return.

Throughout the 19th century, British India's northern boundary remained deliberately undefined. The "natural frontier" of the Himalayan crest was a guiding concept, but actual administrative control rarely extended to the watershed. Between British India and Tibet lay a series of buffer states: Kashmir, Sikkim, Bhutan, and the North-East Frontier Tribal Areas. The British approach was to maintain influence over these buffers while avoiding direct confrontation with China, which claimed suzerainty over Tibet.

### Qing China and Tibet

The Qing Dynasty (1644–1912) exercised a form of suzerainty over Tibet that was ambiguous in both Chinese and international law. The Qing appointed Ambans (imperial commissioners) to Lhasa, but Tibetan internal administration remained in the hands of the Dalai Lama and the monastic establishment. The 1906 Anglo-Chinese Convention recognised Chinese "suzerainty" over Tibet — a term that deliberately avoided the question of sovereignty.

The Qing collapse in 1911–12 created a power vacuum. Tibet declared independence under the 13th Dalai Lama, expelled Chinese forces, and functioned as a de facto independent state from 1912 to 1950. It was in this context that the Simla Conference was convened.

### The Great Game and the Need for a Defined Frontier

Russian expansion into Central Asia in the late 19th century — the conquest of Khiva, Kokand, and Merv — raised the prospect of a Russian presence on the Tibetan plateau. The British response was the "forward policy" in the Himalayas: securing buffer zones and, where possible, defined boundaries with Tibet.

The 1904 Younghusband Expedition to Lhasa was the most dramatic expression of this policy. Ostensibly a trade mission, it was in fact a military invasion that forced a Tibetan acceptance of British trade posts and established a British presence in the Tibetan capital. The resulting Anglo-Tibetan Convention (1904) gave Britain significant influence over Tibetan external affairs.

### The Simla Conference (1913–1914)

The Simla Conference convened in October 1913 against the backdrop of Qing collapse and Tibetan assertion of independence. British India was represented by Sir Henry McMahon (Foreign Secretary of the Government of India), China by Ivan Chen, and Tibet by Lonchen Shatra.

The conference had two principal agendas:
1. Define Tibet's political status vis-à-vis China
2. Delimit the boundary between Tibet and British India

The conference produced the Simla Convention (signed 3 July 1914), which created a tripartite framework for Tibet: an "Outer Tibet" (autonomous under Chinese suzerainty) and an "Inner Tibet" (under more direct Chinese influence). Chinese delegate Ivan Chen initialled the convention but, under instructions from Beijing, refused to sign the final document. Britain and Tibet proceeded to sign a bilateral declaration.

**Critical evidentiary point:** The Chinese government's position is that since the Simla Convention was not ratified by China, it has no legal force. The Indian position is that the India-Tibet boundary was a bilateral matter between Britain and Tibet, and China's non-ratification does not affect its validity. International legal opinion is divided.

### The Johnson Line, Macartney-MacDonald Line, and Colonial Cartography

The western sector of the border — the Aksai Chin region — has a separate cartographic history. Several colonial-era boundary lines were proposed:

**The Johnson Line (1865):** Drawn by W.H. Johnson of the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India along the Kunlun Mountains, this line placed Aksai Chin within Indian territory. It was never officially adopted but appeared on Indian maps after independence.

**The Macartney-MacDonald Line (1899):** A British proposal to China for a boundary along the Karakoram Range. China did not formally respond, and the line was never implemented.

**The Ardagh Line (1897):** Proposed by Sir John Ardagh along a more conservative alignment than Johnson's, it placed the boundary at the crest of the Karakoram.

These multiple lines — each reflecting different strategic priorities and geographic knowledge — created a legacy of cartographic confusion that both sides would later exploit.

### The McMahon Line

The McMahon Line was the boundary between Tibet and British India agreed in the exchange of notes between McMahon and Lonchen Shatra on 24–25 March 1914. It runs approximately 890 kilometres from the eastern corner of Bhutan to the Isu Razi Pass on the Burma border, largely following the Himalayan crest.

Key characteristics:
- **Alignment:** Largely follows the watershed principle (the crest of the Himalayas)
- **Territory allocated to British India:** Approximately 50,000 square kilometres south of the line (including the Tawang tract)
- **Tibetan concessions:** Tibet ceded the Tawang area, which had historically been under Tibetan administration
- **Chinese position:** China claims the line was imposed unilaterally and without Chinese consent
- **Indian position:** The line was a valid bilateral agreement with Tibet, and China as Tibet's suzerain is bound by it

A persistent controversy concerns the omission of the McMahon Line from the 1928 edition of Aitchison's Treaties (the standard British treaty collection). The explanation — that the volume covering the Simla Convention was printed but suppressed due to Chinese objections — has been verified by historians but remains a source of Chinese criticism.

## 4. GEOGRAPHY

### Overview

The India-China border extends across three distinct sectors, each with its own geography, strategic characteristics, and dispute dynamics.

### Western Sector (Ladakh)

**Length:** ~1,597 km

**Key features:**
- **Aksai Chin:** A high-altitude desert plateau (~4,800 metres) under Chinese administration since the 1950s. India claims it as part of Ladakh. China claims it as part of Xinjiang/Tibet. Estimated area: 38,000 sq km.
- **Depopulated frontier zones:** Both sides maintain a policy of restricted habitation near the LAC
- **Strategic significance:** Aksai Chin connects Xinjiang to Tibet via the Xinjiang-Tibet Highway (G219), China's primary military artery
- **Key passes:** Karakoram Pass (5,575 m), Chang Chenmo Valley, Chip Chap Valley
- **Major rivers:** Shyok, Nubra, Indus (headwaters)

**Indian infrastructure:**
- Darbuk-Shyok-Daulat Beg Oldie (DBO) road — completed 2022, all-weather access to forward posts
- Advanced Landing Ground at DBO (highest airstrip in the world)
- Nyoma Airfield — upgraded for fighter operations
- 50,000–60,000 additional troops deployed since 2020
- New all-weather roads and tunnels in Eastern Ladakh

**Chinese infrastructure:**
- G219 National Highway (Xinjiang-Tibet Highway) — running through Aksai Chin
- Railway extension from Kashgar to Hotan (completed), with spur to Aksai Chin
- Military bases at Tianwendian and along the LAC
- "Vibrant" civilian villages for dual-use infrastructure

**Friction points (2026):**
- Depsang Plains: India claims PLA blocks patrol routes; access to DBO airfield constrained
- Pangong Tso: Partial disengagement completed 2021 but buffer zones remain contested
- Galwan Valley: Site of 2020 clash; now a no-patrol zone
- Hot Springs and Gogra: Disengagement completed but restricted access

### Middle Sector (Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand)

**Length:** ~545 km

**Key features:**
- **Least disputed sector:** Both sides have largely accepted the traditional watershed boundary
- **Limited infrastructure:** Lower military presence compared to western and eastern sectors
- **Key passes:** Shipki La, Lipulekh, Kungri Bingri
- **Major rivers:** Sutlej, Kali (Mahakali), Bhagirathi

**Status (2026):** Mostly peaceful with periodic Indian patrol incursions alleged by China. Barahoti and Lapthal remain minor friction points.

### Eastern Sector (Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh)

**Length:** ~1,346 km

**Key features:**
- **Sikkim sector:** The 1890 Anglo-Chinese Convention defined the Sikkim-Tibet boundary. China accepted this boundary in 2003 as part of the Sikkim recognition agreement.
- **Arunachal Pradesh:** The core of the eastern dispute. China claims the entire state (~90,000 sq km) as "South Tibet" (Zangnan).
- **Tawang:** The most emotionally charged town in the dispute. Birthplace of the 6th Dalai Lama. Important Buddhist monastery.
- **Key passes:** Bum La, Tawang Chu Valley, Yangtse
- **Major rivers:** Brahmaputra (Tsangpo), Subansiri, Siang, Lohit, Dibang

**Indian infrastructure:**
- Sela Tunnel — provides all-weather access to Tawang (completed 2024)
- Arunachal Frontier Highway (1,840 km) — under construction along the LAC
- Advanced Landing Grounds at Tawang, Mechuka, Pasighat, Walong
- Brahmaputra River bridges for rapid troop movement

**Chinese infrastructure:**
- Roads and railways in the Chumbi Valley (south of the Himalayan crest)
- "Vibrant" villages along the Chinese side of the LAC
- Military bases at Lhodong, Yadong, and Chushul
- Dual-use tunnels and bridges in Tibet Autonomous Region

**Friction points:**
- Upper Subansiri: Chinese patrol incursions alleged
- Yangtse (2022): PLA helicopter incursion into Indian airspace
- Tawang sector: Chinese claims remain active despite Indian administration

### Climate and Logistics

**Climate:**
- Winter (November–March): Heavy snowfall blocks most passes above 4,000 m
- Summer (June–September): Monsoon in eastern sector; arid in western sector
- Temperature range: -30°C (winter, Ladakh) to 30°C (summer, Arunachal valleys)
- Extreme altitude: Most LAC posts at 4,000–6,000 m

**Logistics challenges:**
- India: Long supply chains from main bases to forward posts; winter isolation
- China: Shorter supply chains from Tibetan plateau; all-weather roads; tunnels
- Air resupply: Essential for both sides in winter; India operates world's highest airstrips
- Railway: China has extensive rail network across Tibet; India's railway approaches but does not reach the LAC

## 5. HISTORICAL NARRATIVE

### 1947 — Independence and Inheritance

India achieved independence on 15 August 1947, inheriting the British Raj's undefined northern frontier. The new Indian government, led by Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru, adopted the McMahon Line as India's boundary in the east and the Johnson/Ardagh lines in the west. China, under Mao Zedong's Communist Party, was still fighting its civil war and posed no immediate challenge.

Nehru's approach to the border was characterised by what scholars have called "idealistic legalism" — a belief that China would accept the colonial-era boundary as a matter of international law and that the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence (Panchsheel) would govern bilateral relations.

### 1950 — Chinese Annexation of Tibet

In October 1950, the People's Liberation Army (PLA) invaded Tibet, then a de facto independent state under the 14th Dalai Lama. India protested but accepted the fait accompli. Nehru's decision not to contest the Chinese takeover of Tibet — despite British-era commitments to Tibetan autonomy — reflected both military weakness (India was preoccupied with the Kashmir war with Pakistan) and a strategic bet that a friendly China would respect Indian borders.

The annexation of Tibet fundamentally changed the border's strategic context. For the first time, India and China shared a long, mountainous frontier — but one delimited by treaties that China had never accepted.

### 1954 — Panchsheel Agreement

The Sino-Indian Agreement on Trade and Cultural Relations (29 April 1954) enshrined the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence (Panchsheel): mutual respect for territorial integrity and sovereignty, mutual non-aggression, mutual non-interference in internal affairs, equality and mutual benefit, and peaceful coexistence.

The 1954 agreement is significant for two reasons. First, it implicitly accepted Chinese control of Tibet (India relinquished its extraterritorial rights in Tibet inherited from the British). Second, the Chinese representative initialled a map showing the McMahon Line as the boundary — a fact India would later cite as evidence of Chinese acceptance.

Chinese scholars argue that the initialling of the map was procedural and did not constitute acceptance of the McMahon Line.

### 1959 — Longju and Kongka Pass Incidents

**Longju (August 1959):** Chinese troops crossed the McMahon Line and established a post at Longju in the North-East Frontier Agency (NEFA, now Arunachal Pradesh). Indian attempts to evict them failed. The incident was the first direct military confrontation between Indian and Chinese forces.

**Kongka Pass (October 1959):** In the western sector, Chinese forces attacked an Indian Police patrol, killing 10 and capturing 7. The incident was the first combat fatality of the dispute.

These incidents shattered the Panchsheel-era optimism. Nehru faced political pressure at home to take a firmer stand against Chinese encroachment.

### 1959 — Dalai Lama's Asylum in India

Following a failed uprising in Lhasa in March 1959, the 14th Dalai Lama fled Tibet and was granted political asylum in India. He established a government-in-exile in Dharamshala. China viewed this as Indian interference in China's internal affairs and as evidence of India's intention to undermine Chinese rule in Tibet.

The Dalai Lama's presence in India remains a point of friction. China demands that India prevent "Tibetan separatist" activities on its soil; India maintains that the exile community has a right to political activity as long as it does not engage in violence.

### 1959–1962 — The Road to War

The period between the 1959 incidents and the October 1962 war was marked by:

1. **Diplomatic correspondence (1959–1962):** An extensive exchange of letters between Nehru and Zhou Enlai (published as the "White Papers") in which both sides laid out their legal claims. The correspondence revealed fundamental and perhaps irreconcilable differences.

2. **Chinese construction in Aksai Chin:** China built a strategic road through Aksai Chin (the Xinjiang-Tibet Highway) between 1956 and 1958. India discovered this after the fact and considered it a violation of Indian sovereignty.

3. **Indian Forward Policy:** In 1961, India adopted the "Forward Policy" — establishing military posts as close as possible to Chinese positions to assert Indian claims and deter further Chinese encroachment. The policy was under-resourced: posts were lightly manned with poor logistics.

4. **Zhou Enlai's 1960 visit:** Zhou visited New Delhi in April 1960 for direct talks with Nehru. He reportedly offered a compromise: India could retain the McMahon Line territory in the east (90,000 sq km) in exchange for Chinese retention of Aksai Chin (38,000 sq km). Nehru rejected the offer, insisting on the full Indian claim.

5. **Thagla Ridge and Dhola Post (July–September 1962):** Indian forces established a post at Dhola (below the Thagla Ridge), which India claimed lay within the McMahon Line. China disputed the alignment and encircled the post on 8 September. India's decision to expel the Chinese by force (Operation Leghorn) triggered the war.

### 1962 — The Sino-Indian War

**Duration:** 20 October – 21 November 1962 (33 days)

**Eastern Sector (NEFA/Arunachal Pradesh):**
- 20 October: PLA attacked across the Namka Chu River. Indian forces (4th Infantry Division) were outnumbered, outflanked, and routed.
- Chinese forces captured Tawang on 25 October.
- By November, PLA columns were advancing rapidly, threatening the Assam plains.

**Western Sector (Ladakh/Aksai Chin):**
- Simultaneous PLA offensive in the Chip Chap Valley and other areas.
- Indian posts were overwhelmed. PLA secured full control of the Aksai Chin region.

**Chinese Unilateral Ceasefire (21 November):**
- China declared a unilateral ceasefire and withdrew to positions 20 km north of the McMahon Line (in the eastern sector).
- In the western sector, China retained all territory it had captured — effectively establishing the current LAC.

**Interpretations of the ceasefire:**
- **Chinese explanation:** China had achieved its limited objectives (teaching India a lesson, securing Tibet) and did not seek to advance into the Assam plains.
- **Indian explanation:** International pressure (US, UK) and the threat of Soviet intervention forced China to stop.
- **Revisionist view:** China never intended a full invasion; the war was a punitive expedition designed to establish a favourable LAC.

**Casualties:**
- India: 1,383 killed, 1,696 wounded, 1,967 missing/captured (official figures)
- China: Never officially released. Western estimates: 722–1,400 killed. Chinese sources: ~1,500 total casualties.

**Aftermath:**
- The Indian military was comprehensively humiliated. Defence Minister Krishna Menon was forced to resign.
- The Henderson-Brooks-Bhagat Report (classified) was commissioned to investigate the debacle. It was critical of political leadership, military preparedness, and operational planning.
- India undertook a massive military modernisation and reoriented defence policy toward China.
- The war shattered Nehru's reputation and contributed to his decline and death in 1964.
- China's international standing was damaged (seen as aggressor by much of the developing world).
- The border was permanently militarised. Both sides established permanent high-altitude military infrastructure.

### 1967 — Nathu La Incident

In September 1967, Chinese and Indian forces clashed at Nathu La (Sikkim). The fighting involved mortars, artillery, and small arms for several days. India estimated 340 Chinese casualties; China estimated 600 Indian casualties. The incident was contained and did not escalate into full-scale war. It demonstrated that border clashes need not spiral into general conflict — a pattern that would hold for most subsequent incidents.

### 1975 — Tulung La Incident

On 20 May 1975, India formally annexed Sikkim after a referendum. China did not recognise the annexation until 2003. The Tulung La incident (1975) involved a brief Chinese incursion into Indian territory in the Sikkim sector, resolved through diplomatic channels.

### 1986–1987 — Sumdorong Chu Incident

A significant military confrontation occurred in the Sumdorong Chu valley (Arunachal Pradesh) in 1986–87, described by some as the "1967-style crisis." Both sides deployed substantial forces and came close to war. India established a post at Wangdung. Chinese patrols crossed the McMahon Line near Sumdorong Chu.

The crisis was defused through diplomatic channels during a period of improving relations. Some analysts argue this was the closest the two sides came to a second war since 1962.

### 1988 — Rajiv Gandhi's Visit to China

Indian Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi visited Beijing in December 1988 — the first visit by an Indian PM since Nehru in 1954. The visit established the Joint Working Group on the boundary question and set a framework for gradual normalisation. Both sides agreed to "maintain peace and tranquility" while the border was discussed.

### 1993 — Border Peace and Tranquility Agreement

The Agreement on the Maintenance of Peace and Tranquility along the Line of Actual Control (7 September 1993) was a breakthrough:

- Both sides agreed to resolve the boundary question through peaceful consultations
- Neither side would use or threaten force
- Both sides would "strictly respect and observe" the LAC
- Military forces would be reduced to minimum levels

**Critical feature:** The agreement did not define the LAC. Both sides had different maps of where the LAC lay. The agreement papered over this fundamental divergence.

### 1996 — Agreement on Confidence-Building Measures

Signed during Chinese President Jiang Zemin's visit to India (29 November 1996), this agreement built on 1993:

- Prior notification of military exercises near the LAC
- No military exercises in mutually identified zones
- No use of firearms within 2 km of the LAC
- Overflights by combat aircraft prohibited across the LAC
- Exchange of LAC maps for the middle sector

The map exchange process was significant: both sides exchanged their perceptions of the LAC for the middle sector (Himachal-Uttarakhand). However, when maps for the western and eastern sectors were exchanged, the divergences were so large that the process collapsed in 2002–2003.

### 2003 — Recognition of Sikkim

A significant diplomatic milestone: China recognised Sikkim as part of India (ending its claim that Sikkim was an independent state). In exchange, India recognised Chinese sovereignty over Tibet. The Sino-Indian border in the Sikkim sector was formally clarified, though China's official position remains that Sikkim's boundary with Tibet is not fully settled.

### 2005 — Political Parameters and Guiding Principles

Signed during Chinese Premier Wen Jiabao's visit (11 April 2005), this agreement established "political parameters and guiding principles" for a final settlement:

- Both sides would make "meaningful mutual adjustments" to their positions
- The settlement should be "fair, reasonable, and mutually acceptable"
- Both sides would "safeguard due interests of their settled populations"
- The border should be peaceful, stable, and durable

**Significance:** For the first time, both sides formally accepted the principle of compromise. The agreement implicitly acknowledged that neither side would get everything it claimed.

### 2012 — Working Mechanism for Consultation and Coordination

The WMCC was established (January 2012) as a standing institutional mechanism for border management — separate from the Special Representatives mechanism for negotiating a final settlement. The WMCC meets regularly to discuss operational issues.

### 2013 — Border Defence Cooperation Agreement (BDCA)

Signed during Prime Minister Manmohan Singh's visit (23 October 2013), the BDCA established:

- Direct hotline between defence ministries
- Flag meetings at the local commander level
- "No tailing" of patrols — both sides agreed their patrols would not follow each other
- Maximum self-restraint in areas where the LAC is unclear

**Context:** The BDCA was a direct response to the 2013 Daulat Beg Oldi (DBO) incident, where a Chinese PLA platoon crossed the LAC in the western sector and established a tented camp 19 km inside Indian territory. The incident was resolved diplomatically but exposed the fragility of the CBMs.

### 2017 — Doklam Standoff

A 73-day standoff (June–August 2017) in the Doklam plateau (Bhutan-China-India trijunction) was the most serious crisis since 1987. Chinese forces began constructing a road in territory claimed by Bhutan (an Indian ally). Indian forces crossed into the area to block construction.

**Key features:**
- Different legal basis from previous incidents: India intervened on Bhutan's behalf, not directly on its own claim
- The standoff was resolved diplomatically after intense bilateral negotiations
- It established the precedent that third-party territory could become a flashpoint
- Chinese patrols and construction activity continued in Doklam after the standoff

### 2020 — Galwan Valley and the Post-Galwan Crisis

**Background:** In April–May 2020, PLA forces crossed the LAC in multiple sectors of Eastern Ladakh — the Galwan Valley, Pangong Tso, the Depsang Plains, Hot Springs, and Gogra — establishing new positions that blocked Indian patrol routes.

**Galwan Valley clash (15 June 2020):**
- A confrontation between Indian and Chinese soldiers in the Galwan Valley escalated from a face-off into hand-to-hand combat using clubs, rocks, and improvised weapons
- 20 Indian soldiers were killed (including a commanding officer, Colonel Santosh Babu)
- Chinese casualties: officially denied, but widely reported as 4+ (Indian sources claim much higher)
- First combat fatalities on the border since 1975

**Aftermath:**
- Both sides deployed substantial reinforcements: India moved 50,000–60,000 additional troops; China matched
- India imposed an economic boycott of Chinese goods, banned Chinese apps (including TikTok and WeChat), and restricted Chinese investment
- A series of Corps Commander-level talks (ongoing) sought disengagement
- India's trust in China's adherence to bilateral agreements was shattered

**Strategic significance:** Galwan was not 1962. India responded with military mobilisation, diplomatic resolve, and economic measures. The crisis demonstrated that India would no longer accept Chinese border violations without response. But it also revealed that China's military advantage along the LAC had grown significantly since 1962.

### 2022 — Yangtse Incident

In December 2022, a PLA helicopter intruded into Indian airspace near Yangtse (Arunachal Pradesh), allegedly to monitor Indian fencing activity. The incident was publicised by India but did not escalate. It highlighted ongoing Chinese concern about Indian border infrastructure development in the eastern sector.

### 2024–2026 — Partial Disengagement and Cold Peace

In October 2024, India and China announced an agreement on disengagement in the Depsang Plains and Demchok — the last two friction points from the 2020 crisis. Patrolling rights were partially restored but under mutually agreed restrictions.

**Current status (mid-2026):**
- 35th WMCC meeting held in Beijing (May 2026)
- 25th round of Special Representatives talks expected
- Situation described as "generally stable" by both sides
- Military forces remain at elevated levels
- Infrastructure construction continues on both sides
- No progress on the fundamental boundary question

## 6. KEY CLAIMS

### Claim 1: The Simla Convention (1914) established a legally binding India-Tibet boundary

**Confidence:** Medium-High (Indian position) / Medium (Chinese position)

**Evidence:**
- Signed text of Simla Convention (full text available at Tibet Justice Center)
- Exchange of notes between McMahon and Lonchen Shatra (24–25 March 1914)
- Map initialled by all three delegations

**Primary documents:**
- Simla Convention text (Articles 1–11)
- Anglo-Tibetan Declaration (3 July 1914)
- McMahon-Lonchen correspondence (March 1914)
- Aitchison's Treaties, Volume XIV (1928 edition — suppressed)

**Supporting historians:** Alastair Lamb, Parshotam Mehra, Claude Arpi, Steven Hoffmann
**Counterargument historians:** Neville Maxwell (qualified), Karunakar Gupta

**Counterarguments:**
- Chinese delegate initialled but did not sign the convention
- China was not a party to the bilateral McMahon-Lonchen agreement
- Tibet lacked treaty-making capacity under Chinese law
- The 1928 Aitchison's Treaties omitted the Simla Convention (though this is now accepted as a suppression, not a repudiation)
- The 1954 Panchsheel Agreement did not explicitly reference the McMahon Line

**Editorial synthesis:** The Simla Convention's legal status is ambiguous under international law. It was not ratified by China, rendering it non-binding on Beijing under the pacta tertiis principle (treaties do not bind third parties). However, the India-Tibet boundary was a bilateral matter between Britain and Tibet, and China's non-ratification of the overall convention does not automatically invalidate the boundary agreement. The matter is further complicated by whether Tibet possessed treaty-making capacity in 1914 — a question on which international lawyers remain divided. The weight of post-1950 practice (Chinese maps, the 1954 initialling, and Chinese acceptance of the McMahon Line in Burma) suggests that the line has acquired some status in practice, if not in law.

**Related claims:** Claim 2, Claim 3, Claim 4

---

### Claim 2: China's 1954 initialling of a map showing the McMahon Line constituted acceptance of the boundary

**Confidence:** Medium

**Evidence:**
- Chinese representative initialled a map attached to the 1954 Trade Agreement
- Zhou Enlai stated in 1956 that China "did not recognise" the McMahon Line but would accept it as a "de facto boundary"
- Chinese maps before 1959 showed the McMahon Line as the boundary

**Primary documents:**
- 1954 Sino-Indian Agreement text
- Initialled map (held in Indian archives)
- Nehru-Zhou correspondence (1959–1962 White Papers)

**Counterarguments:**
- Initialling was procedural, not substantive
- Chinese maps after 1959 reverted to pre-McMahon alignment
- Zhou's 1956 statement was private, not official
- The 1954 agreement dealt with trade, not boundary delimitation

**Editorial synthesis:** The evidence suggests Chinese acceptance of the McMahon Line was conditional and temporary. The 1954 initialling is evidence of Chinese acknowledgment of the line's existence but falls short of formal acceptance. China's claim that it "never accepted" the McMahon Line is contradicted by some evidence (the 1954 map, Zhou's 1956 statement) but consistent with its consistent treaty position.

---

### Claim 3: The 1962 war was caused primarily by Indian intransigence and the Forward Policy

**Confidence:** Medium (debated)

**Evidence (Indian intransigence thesis):**
- Nehru's rejection of Zhou's 1960 compromise offer
- India's Forward Policy established posts in disputed areas
- Indian political leadership overrode military advice on preparedness
- Henderson-Brooks report (partial) criticised Indian decision-making

**Evidence (Chinese expansionism thesis):**
- Chinese construction of the Aksai Chin road (1956–58) on claimed Indian territory
- Chinese suppression of the 1959 Tibetan uprising and blame of India
- PLA's deliberate escalation at Longju (1959) and Thagla (1962)
- China's refusal to accept the McMahon Line despite earlier indications
- Chinese occupation of territory after the 1962 ceasefire

**Supporting historians:**
- "Indian blame" school: Neville Maxwell, Karunakar Gupta, Alastair Lamb
- "Chinese blame" school: Margaret Fisher, Leo Rose, John Garver (qualified)
- "Mutual misperception" school: Steven Hoffmann, Allen Whiting, Berenice Guyot-Rechard

**Editorial synthesis:** The evidence supports the conclusion that both sides contributed to the war through mutual misperception and failed diplomacy. India's Forward Policy was provocative and poorly executed. China's decision to respond with full-scale military force was disproportionate to the immediate provocation. The war cannot be attributed to a single cause. A 2018 scholarship review by Sumit Ganguly concluded that "for all its tactical shortcomings, the Forward Policy was a response to Chinese encroachments, not the original cause of the dispute."

---

### Claim 4: The Line of Actual Control (LAC) is a mutually recognised de facto border

**Confidence:** Low (the LAC is not agreed by both sides)

**Evidence:**
- 1993 and 1996 agreements reference the LAC but do not define it
- Map exchange process (1996–2002) revealed massive divergence in LAC perceptions
- Chinese maps show LAC different from Indian maps in multiple sectors
- The LAC was not established by any agreement but emerged from the 1962 war outcome

**Supporting historians:** Manoj Joshi, John Garver
**Counterargument:** Chinese official position (ambiguous)

**Editorial synthesis:** The LAC is a diplomatic fiction that has been useful for maintaining peace but is not a mutually recognised line. Both sides have different maps and different understandings. The 2020 crisis demonstrated that when one side acts on its understanding of the LAC, conflict results. The LAC's ambiguity is simultaneously a source of conflict and a necessary ambiguity that allows both sides to avoid admitting their positions are incompatible.

---

### Claim 5: China's border policy is fundamentally expansionist

**Confidence:** Low-Medium

**Evidence for:**
- Continued claims to Arunachal Pradesh (90,000 sq km of Indian-administered territory)
- Construction of dual-use infrastructure on the Tibetan side of the LAC
- "Salami slicing" tactics: incremental advance followed by negotiation from strength
- Land Border Law (2021) codifying rejection of "unequal treaties"
- PLA construction activity after disengagement agreements

**Evidence against:**
- China has settled all other land borders through negotiation (12 of 14 neighbours)
- China accepted the Sikkim boundary (2003)
- China has not attempted large-scale invasion since 1962
- China has consistently participated in border management mechanisms
- The Land Border Law did not change Beijing's operational approach

**Supporting analysts:** Manoj Joshi, Brahma Chellaney, Kunal Verma
**Counterargument analysts:** M. Taylor Fravel, John Garver (qualified)

**Editorial synthesis:** The evidence for a coherent Chinese expansionist strategy is inconclusive. China's border behaviour appears opportunistic rather than strategic — advancing when the opportunity arises but not pursuing a systematic plan. China's 2020 actions were a response to Indian infrastructure development that Beijing perceived as changing the status quo. However, the cumulative effect of Chinese tactics over six decades has been to increase the territory under its control.

---

### Claim 6: The 2020 Galwan clash was a Chinese violation of bilateral agreements

**Confidence:** High

**Evidence:**
- Both sides confirm PLA crossed the LAC in multiple sectors from April 2020
- India's position before April 2020 was that the status quo had been stable
- Chinese actions violated 1993 (respect LAC), 1996 (no use of firearms), and 2013 (BDCA) agreements
- Indian patrols were blocked from areas both sides had mutually recognised as patrollable
- Corps Commander-level talks eventually produced disengagement, implying Chinese acknowledgment of overreach

**Counterarguments:**
- China claims it was responding to Indian infrastructure construction that altered the status quo
- China argues the LAC in these sectors was disputed and its actions were within its interpretation
- The Indian version of events is based largely on official sources

**Editorial synthesis:** The weight of evidence supports India's claim that China's 2020 actions violated the spirit and letter of the bilateral CBMs. However, the Indian claim that the status quo was entirely stable before 2020 is complicated by the fact that both sides had been building infrastructure and reinforcing positions for years. The pre-2020 status quo was not static — it was a gradual, mutual military buildup.

## 7. PRIMARY DOCUMENTS

### Document 1: Simla Convention (3 July 1914)

**Full title:** Convention Between Great Britain, China, and Tibet

**Repository:** Wikisource / Tibet Justice Center
**Archive reference:** India Office Records, British Library

**Historical context:** Signed at the conclusion of the Simla Conference (October 1913 – July 1914), the convention sought to define Tibet's status and boundaries amid the collapse of the Qing Dynasty and Tibetan assertion of independence.

**What it says:**
- Article 1: Previous conventions remain binding
- Article 2: Britain and China recognise Chinese suzerainty over Tibet and Tibetan autonomy
- Article 3: Tibet non-transferable to any foreign power
- Article 9: "The borders of Tibet, and the boundary between Outer and Inner Tibet, shall be as shown in red and blue respectively on the map attached hereto"

**Important passages:**
> "The Governments of Great Britain and China recognizing that Tibet is under the suzerainty of China, and recognizing also the autonomy of Outer Tibet, engage to respect the territorial integrity of the country..."

> "Nothing in the present Convention shall be held to prejudice the existing rights of the Tibetan Government in Inner Tibet..."

**Why it matters:** The Simla Convention is the foundational document of the India-China border dispute in the eastern sector. Its ambiguous legal status (signed by Britain and Tibet; initialled but not signed by China; not ratified by China) is at the heart of the legal disagreement.

**Claims supported:** 1, 2
**Scholarly debates:** Whether the convention acquired legal force through subsequent practice; whether the India-Tibet boundary was separable from the overall convention.

---

### Document 2: Exchange of Notes between McMahon and Lonchen Shatra (24–25 March 1914)

**Repository:** National Archives of India

**Historical context:** These notes established the McMahon Line as the boundary between British India and Tibet, preceding the full Simla Convention.

**What it says:** The notes recorded agreement on the alignment of the boundary, with the line "following the crest of the Himalayas from the eastern corner of Bhutan to the Isu Razi Pass on the Burma border."

**Why it matters:** This is the document that actually created the McMahon Line. India considers it a binding bilateral agreement.

**Scholarly debates:** Whether the boundary was validly agreed between two parties with treaty-making capacity under international law.

---

### Document 3: The Panchsheel Agreement (29 April 1954)

**Full title:** Agreement on Trade and Cultural Relations Between the Republic of India and the People's Republic of China

**Repository:** United Nations Treaty Series

**Historical context:** The first major bilateral agreement between independent India and Communist China. It enshrined the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence.

**Important passage:**
> "The two Governments agree to maintain peace and tranquility in the border regions and to settle all disputes by peaceful means."

**What the Indian side emphasises:** The Chinese representative initialled a map showing the McMahon Line.
**What the Chinese side emphasises:** The agreement dealt with trade, not boundary delimitation.

**Claims supported:** 2

---

### Document 4: Nehru-Zhou Correspondence (1959–1962)

**Full reference:** Government of India, White Papers on Sino-Indian Relations (1959–1962), Ministry of External Affairs.

**Repository:** National Archives of India

**Historical context:** An exchange of letters between Prime Ministers Nehru and Zhou Enlai that laid out the competing legal and historical claims.

**Important passages:** Zhou's letter of 8 September 1959 rejected the McMahon Line; Nehru's response of 26 September 1959 defended it. The correspondence reveals that both sides recognised the dispute's complexity but were unable to find common ground.

**Claims supported:** 3, 4

---

### Document 5: 1960 Officials Report

**Full title:** Report of the Officials of the Governments of India and the People's Republic of China on the Boundary Question

**Repository:** Government of India, Ministry of External Affairs

**Historical context:** After Zhou Enlai's 1960 visit, senior officials from both sides met to examine documentary evidence for the competing claims.

**What it says:** The report presented both sides' evidence without reaching agreement. It is a remarkable document: two parallel versions of history, geography, and legality, side by side.

**Why it matters:** It remains the most comprehensive statement of both sides' legal and historical cases.

---

### Document 6: Agreement on Border Peace and Tranquility (7 September 1993)

**Full text available at:** Peacemaker UN Database

**Key provisions:**
- Article 1: "Pending an ultimate solution... the two sides shall strictly respect and observe the line of actual control"
- Article 4: Contingency management through meetings and consultations

**Why it matters:** The first major post-1962 bilateral agreement on border management. It formally introduced the LAC as a diplomatic concept.

---

### Document 7: Agreement on Confidence-Building Measures (29 November 1996)

**Key provisions:**
- Article 4: No use of firearms within 2 km of the LAC
- Article 5: Prior notification of military exercises
- Article 6: Exchange of LAC maps

**Why it matters:** Established the operational framework for avoiding conflict. The map exchange provision was groundbreaking but ultimately failed.

---

### Document 8: Agreement on Political Parameters and Guiding Principles (11 April 2005)

**Key provisions:**
- "Meaningful mutual adjustments" to positions
- "Due interests of settled populations" to be safeguarded
- The settlement should be "fair, reasonable, and mutually acceptable"

**Why it matters:** The closest the two sides have come to agreeing on principles for a final settlement. It implicitly acknowledged the need for compromise.

---

### Document 9: Border Defence Cooperation Agreement (23 October 2013)

**Key provisions:**
- Direct hotline between defence ministries
- "No tailing" of patrols
- Maximum self-restraint in unclear LAC areas

**Why it matters:** Operational document designed to prevent the kind of incidents that had occurred at DBO (2013).

---

### Document 10: Henderson-Brooks-Bhagat Report (1963, classified)

**Repository:** Government of India, Ministry of Defence (classified)
**Access:** Partially leaked by Neville Maxwell (2014)

**Historical context:** Commissioned after the 1962 debacle to investigate the causes of Indian military failure.

**Key findings (from leaked portions):**
- The Indian political leadership (Nehru, Krishna Menon) overrode military advice
- The Forward Policy was implemented without adequate logistics or force levels
- The military chain of command was dysfunctional
- Intelligence failures were severe

**Why it matters:** The report's continued classification is a major evidence gap and a subject of controversy in India. Its critics argue that it scapegoats the military for political failures.

## 8. COMPETING INTERPRETATIONS

### The Indian Strategic School

**Core argument:** The India-China border is a legacy of British imperial withdrawal that India inherited in good faith. China's refusal to accept the McMahon Line and its occupation of Aksai Chin represent unremedied aggression. India insists on Chinese recognition of its territorial integrity as a precondition for normal relations.

**Key proponents:** K. Subrahmanyam, Brahma Chellaney, Manoj Joshi, Bharat Karnad, Sreeram Chaulia

**Evidence base:**
- Colonial treaties and maps
- Chinese 1949 acceptance (via Zhou Enlai) of existing boundaries
- The 1954 initialling
- Indian administrative control over the northeastern frontier since 1950

**Weaknesses:**
- Over-relies on legal formalism without adequately addressing Chinese security concerns
- Understates India's own departures from the legal position (e.g., acceptance of Chinese control of Tibet)
- Has not produced a viable diplomatic strategy for achieving its objectives

**Criticism from other schools:** The school's insistence on 100% of the Indian claim is unrealistic given the military balance and China's effective control of Aksai Chin.

**Influence:** Dominant within the Indian strategic community, Ministry of External Affairs (traditional wing), and popular discourse.

---

### The Chinese Strategic School

**Core argument:** The Simla Convention was an unequal treaty imposed on a weak China. Tibet never possessed treaty-making capacity. The McMahon Line is therefore invalid. India's "illegal occupation" of Arunachal Pradesh (South Tibet) must be resolved through Chinese sovereign recognition. Aksai Chin has historically been part of China.

**Key proponents:** Xu Yan (PLA National Defence University), Wang Hongwei (Chinese Academy of Sciences), Zhang Guohua

**Evidence base:**
- Qing imperial records of administration in Tibet
- Chinese maps showing Aksai Chin and South Tibet as Chinese territory
- Rejection of unequal treaties as a matter of PRC legal doctrine
- Effective Chinese administration of Aksai Chin since the 1950s

**Weaknesses:**
- Ignores the consensual nature of the 1914 Simla negotiations
- Overstates Qing administrative control in remote Himalayan areas
- Contradicted by Chinese pre-1959 maps showing McMahon Line
- The "unequal treaty" doctrine is self-serving and not accepted in international law

**Influence:** Dominant within Chinese strategic discourse; all Chinese official positions derive from this school.

---

### The Revisionist/International Law School

**Core argument:** Neither side has a fully coherent legal position. The boundary's ambiguity is the product of incomplete colonial cartography, inconsistent post-independence practice, and unresolved questions about Tibet's international status. A negotiated compromise is the only viable path.

**Key proponents:** Alastair Lamb, Steven Hoffmann, M. Taylor Fravel, Berenice Guyot-Rechard

**Evidence base:**
- Detailed archival research on colonial mapping practices
- Comparative study of China's border settlements with other neighbours
- Analysis of "sovereignty" as a contested concept in the Himalayas

**Weaknesses:**
- Can be seen as advocating for moral equivalence between aggressor and victim
- Has not produced a politically viable compromise formula
- Understates the domestic political constraints on both sides

**Influence:** Significant in academic circles but limited in policy discourse in both countries.

---

### The Geopolitical/Realist School

**Core argument:** The border dispute is primarily an expression of the broader India-China strategic competition. It cannot be resolved in isolation from the overall balance of power, alliance structures, and regional security architecture.

**Key proponents:** John Garver, Sumit Ganguly, Harsh Pant, Constantino Xavier

**Evidence base:**
- China's use of border pressure as a diplomatic lever
- Linkage between border tensions and India's alignment with the US/QUAD
- Role of Pakistan as China's strategic counterweight
- Nuclear deterrence dynamics

**Weaknesses:**
- Risks treating the border as purely instrumental, ignoring genuine disagreements
- May understate the importance of institutional mechanisms (WMCC, SR talks) in managing conflict

**Influence:** Growing influence in both academic and policy discourse.

---

### The Military-Technical School

**Core argument:** The border dispute is ultimately a military question of effective control, deployable power, and logistical capacity. The side that can project and sustain military force along the most advantageous terrain will determine the outcome.

**Key proponents:** Lieutenant General (Retd) D.S. Hooda, Lieutenant General (Retd) P.N. Hoon, Admiral (Retd) Arun Prakash

**Evidence base:**
- Detailed analysis of force ratios, logistics, and infrastructure
- Satellite imagery of military deployments
- Comparative assessment of Chinese and Indian capabilities

**Weaknesses:**
- Over-emphasises military factors at the expense of diplomatic and legal dimensions
- Cannot account for political decision-making and escalation risks

**Influence:** Significant within Indian military circles and defence journalism.

---

## 9. THINKERS

### Alastair Lamb (1930–2023)

**Biography:** British historian, former professor at Cambridge University. Published extensively on Himalayan history and Tibet from the 1960s onward.

**Major books:**
- *The China-India Border: The Origins of the Disputed Boundaries* (1964)
- *The McMahon Line: A Study in the Relations between India, China and Tibet, 1904–1914* (1966)
- *Tibet, China and India, 1914–1950* (1989)

**Research methods:** Archival research in London, New Delhi, and Simla.

**Core thesis:** The India-China border dispute is a product of incomplete imperial cartography and the failure of independent states to resolve colonial-era ambiguities through negotiation.

**Criticism:** Accused by Indian scholars of understating Chinese aggression and overstating Indian intransigence.

**Influence:** Foundational figure in the study of the border. His archival work remains the starting point for most serious scholarship.

---

### Neville Maxwell (1926–2019)

**Biography:** Australian journalist and scholar. South Asia correspondent for The Times (London) during the 1962 war.

**Major books:**
- *India's China War* (1970)

**Research methods:** Journalistic investigation, access to the classified Henderson-Brooks report, interviews with Indian officials.

**Core thesis:** The 1962 war was caused primarily by India's intransigence and the Forward Policy, not by Chinese expansionism.

**Criticism:** Widely accused of pro-China bias. His account is considered revisionist by the Indian mainstream. Singapore's Lee Kuan Yew called it "pro-China history."

**Influence:** Despite controversy, his book is the most widely read English-language account of the 1962 war. His leak of the Henderson-Brooks report made previously classified information available.

---

### John W. Garver

**Biography:** Professor Emeritus of International Affairs, Georgia Institute of Technology. One of the leading American scholars of Sino-Indian relations.

**Major books:**
- *Protracted Contest: Sino-Indian Rivalry in the Twentieth Century* (2001)
- *China's Decision for War with India in 1962* (2006)

**Research methods:** Multi-archival research including Chinese-, English-, and Russian-language sources.

**Core thesis:** The India-China rivalry is a protracted contest for influence, status, and security in Asia, of which the border dispute is one dimension.

**Criticism:** Relatively little direct criticism; his work is widely respected for its balance.

**Influence:** Among the most cited scholars of Sino-Indian relations. His analysis of China's decision for war is the standard reference.

---

### Steven A. Hoffmann

**Biography:** Professor, Skidmore College. Specialist in international crisis behaviour.

**Major books:**
- *India and the China Crisis* (1990, reissued 2018)

**Research methods:** Interviews with Indian participants, archival research, crisis behaviour modelling.

**Core thesis:** The 1962 crisis was driven by mutual misperception and failed communication between Nehru's and Mao's governments.

**Criticism:** Dense theoretical framework may limit accessibility.

**Influence:** Reconciled the competing Indian-blame and Chinese-blame schools by showing how both sides contributed to the crisis.

---

### M. Taylor Fravel

**Biography:** Professor of Political Science, MIT. Specialist in Chinese security policy and territorial disputes.

**Major books:**
- *Strong Borders, Secure Nation: Cooperation and Conflict in China's Territorial Disputes* (2008)

**Research methods:** Analysis of Chinese-language sources, patterns in China's settlement of territorial disputes.

**Core thesis:** China's territorial dispute behaviour is defensive, not expansionist. China has made more concessions than is generally recognised.

**Criticism:** His "defensive China" thesis is contested by Indian and Western analysts who point to China's behaviour in 2020.

**Influence:** The leading Western scholar of Chinese territorial dispute behaviour. His framework is essential for understanding China's approach.

---

### Manoj Joshi

**Biography:** Distinguished Fellow, ORF. Former journalist and foreign policy analyst.

**Major books:**
- *Unfinished Business: The India-China Border Dispute* (2021)

**Research methods:** Policy analysis, interviews, open-source intelligence.

**Core thesis:** China prefers the border to remain unsettled because the dispute serves as a strategic lever against India. India's only viable response is military strengthening combined with diplomatic realism.

**Criticism:** Critics argue his analysis risks becoming self-fulfilling — assuming China's bad faith makes negotiation futile.

**Influence:** Influential in Indian policy circles. His book is widely cited as the most up-to-date comprehensive account.

---

### Berenice Guyot-Rechard

**Biography:** Historian of modern South Asia. Formerly at King's College London and Sciences Po.

**Major books:**
- *Shadow States: India, China and the Himalayas, 1910–1962* (2017)

**Research methods:** Multi-archival research across India, UK, China, and US.

**Core thesis:** Both India and China adopted Westphalian conceptions of fixed, sovereign borders after independence — a departure from the fluid frontier arrangements of the imperial era. This shared adoption of modern statehood created the conditions for conflict.

**Criticism:** A relatively recent contribution; its influence is still emerging.

**Influence:** Widely praised as a landmark study. Sumit Ganguly described it as "the most invaluable resource for those interested in understanding the sources of this seemingly irresolvable conflict."

## 14. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### Why Negotiations Fail

The six-decade failure to resolve the border dispute can be attributed to several structural factors:

**1. Fundamental legal incompatibility:** The two sides base their claims on different legal principles. India relies on treaty rights and colonial-era agreements. China relies on historical sovereignty and the unequal-treaty doctrine. These legal frameworks are mutually exclusive.

**2. Asymmetric stakes:** For India, the territorial claim (especially Arunachal Pradesh) carries enormous domestic political weight. For China, the claim to Aksai Chin is primarily strategic (the highway), while the claim to Arunachal Pradesh is more a leverage tool than a core interest.

**3. Domestic political constraints:** No Indian government can be seen as "losing" territory to China. No Chinese leader can be seen as renouncing a historical claim. Compromise requires political capital neither side has been willing to expend.

**4. Linkage to broader competition:** The border dispute is now embedded in a larger strategic competition encompassing trade, technology, naval power, and alliance alignment. It cannot be separated from this broader context.

**5. Tibet factor:** As John Garver and others have argued, China's intransigence on the border is partly a function of its fear about India's potential to undermine Chinese rule in Tibet. Until this fear is resolved, the border cannot be settled.

**6. Pakistan factor:** China's continuing strategic partnership with Pakistan provides an incentive to maintain India under "two-front" pressure. Resolving the border would reduce this leverage.

### Military Balance

**Comparative strengths:**

| Dimension | India | China |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Total active personnel | 1.45 million | 2.03 million |
| Western Theatre Command troops | — | ~300,000 |
| Total along LAC (pre-2020) | ~100,000 | ~60,000 |
| Total along LAC (2026, estimated) | ~150,000–170,000 | ~120,000–150,000 |
| Air bases within 200 km | ~15 | ~25+ |
| Advanced aircraft (4.5+ gen) | ~250 | ~400+ |
| Logistics capacity (LAC sector) | Developing | Superior |
| Nuclear weapons | Yes (no-first-use) | Yes (no-first-use) |

**Key Indian advantages:**
- Shorter internal lines on the eastern sector
- Superior military experience (high-altitude warfare, continuous operations)
- Growing air power with modern platforms (Rafale, Su-30MKI)
- Strategic partnership with US, Japan, Australia (QUAD)
- Improved indigenous defence production

**Key Chinese advantages:**
- Superior infrastructure on the Tibetan plateau
- Shorter supply lines to the western sector (Ladakh)
- Numerical superiority in total forces
- Advanced air defence systems
- Ballistic missile and cruise missile capabilities
- Integrated Western Theatre Command structure

### Infrastructure Competition

**China's advantage:** China began building infrastructure in Tibet in the 1980s. It now has a network of all-weather roads, railways, and airfields that enable rapid troop movement to the LAC. The Sichuan-Tibet Railway (under construction) will reduce transit times dramatically. [Source: ORF analysis]

**India's response:** Since 2014, India has dramatically accelerated border infrastructure spending. BRO expenditure reached a record ~$2 billion in FY 2025–26 (₹17,900 crore). Key projects include the DBO road (complete), Sela Tunnel (complete, 2024), Arunachal Frontier Highway (under construction), and new railway lines ($3+ billion allocated). However, India started decades behind and is still catching up.

**Assessment:** China will maintain an infrastructure advantage for the foreseeable future. India's acceleration reduces the gap but does not close it.

### ISR (Intelligence, Surveillance, Reconnaissance)

Both sides have invested heavily in ISR capabilities along the LAC. India operates:
- Satellite imagery (Cartosat, RISAT series)
- UAVs (Heron, Searcher, Predator from US)
- Ground-based radar and SIGINT stations

China operates:
- Satellite surveillance (Gaofen, Yaogan series)
- High-altitude UAVs (Soar Dragon, CH-series)
- Ground-based radar, SIGINT, and electronic warfare systems

The ISR competition adds transparency to the border but also increases the risk of preemptive strikes if either side fears its ISR assets will be degraded.

### Nuclear Deterrence

Both India and China are nuclear-armed states with declared no-first-use policies. The nuclear dimension introduces both stability (neither side can escalate without nuclear risk) and instability (conventional escalation could lead to inadvertent nuclear escalation).

China has deployed nuclear-capable missiles and aircraft in Tibet. India has not confirmed any nuclear deployments along the LAC but has nuclear-capable delivery systems.

The nuclear factor has likely contributed to the restraint shown by both sides — neither Galwan nor Doklam escalated to full-scale war.

### Alliance Effects

**India's diversification:** India has deepened defence partnerships with the US (COMCASA, BECA, LEMOA), Japan (ACSA), Australia, France, and Vietnam. The QUAD provides a political framework for collective Indo-Pacific engagement. This gives India greater strategic depth and reduces its vulnerability to Chinese pressure.

**China's counter:** China has strengthened its partnership with Pakistan (CPEC, defence supplies), deepened ties with Russia (military exercises, energy), and expanded its influence in South Asian neighbours (Nepal, Sri Lanka, Maldives).

**Assessment:** India's alignment with the US has reduced its strategic vulnerability but may also have increased Chinese incentives to maintain border pressure as a counter-leverage.

### Economic Interdependence

Bilateral trade reached ~$136 billion in 2023, with a massive surplus in China's favour. India has sought to reduce dependence on Chinese imports since 2020 but progress has been slow. Economic interdependence creates incentives for both sides to avoid catastrophic conflict but does not prevent border tensions.

### Future Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Managed Cold Peace (most likely, 60% probability)**
- Both sides maintain elevated force levels
- Diplomatic mechanisms (WMCC, SR talks) continue functioning
- Periodic incidents occur but are contained
- No progress on fundamental resolution
- Infrastructure competition continues

**Scenario 2: Major Crisis (20% probability)**
- A miscalculation or deliberate escalation leads to a 1987/2017-level standoff
- Possible casus belli: a PLA incursion in a new sector, a fatal encounter, an infrastructure confrontation
- Crisis is contained through diplomatic channels
- No full-scale war

**Scenario 3: Détente and Framework Agreement (10% probability)**
- Political leadership on both sides decides to pursue resolution
- A framework agreement (e.g., accepting LAC as de facto border) is reached
- Implementation takes decades
- Requires significant concessions from both sides

**Scenario 4: Full-Scale Conflict (7% probability)**
- Escalation from a crisis leads to significant military engagement
- High casualties, potential use of tactical theatre weapons
- Economic devastation for both sides
- Nuclear risk becomes real

**Scenario 5: Unilateral Action (3% probability)**
- One side makes a significant territorial gain through military action
- E.g., China captures Tawang; India pushes into Aksai Chin
- War is almost certain to follow

## 15. COUNTERARGUMENTS

This section challenges the major conclusions of this monograph, presenting the strongest evidence that might support alternative interpretations.

### Counterargument 1: The McMahon Line is clearly invalid under international law

**Argument:** The Simla Convention was never ratified by China. Under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, a state is not bound by a treaty it has not signed or ratified. Tibet was not a sovereign state with treaty-making capacity. The 1928 suppression of Aitchison's Treaties demonstrates that even Britain acknowledged the convention's doubtful status.

**Evidence:** Vienna Convention Article 34 (pacta tertiis); Aitchison's Treaties, Volume XIV publication history; Chinese official legal positions.

**Strengths:** This is the strongest Chinese legal argument. It is consistent with standard international law principles.

**Why it may not be conclusive:** The India-Tibet boundary was a bilateral agreement between Britain and Tibet, not the tripartite Simla Convention. China's non-ratification of the general convention does not necessarily invalidate the boundary provision. The Tibet question (treaty-making capacity) remains contested. Post-1954 practice (Chinese initialling of maps) creates some evidentiary difficulty for China.

---

### Counterargument 2: The 1962 war was a legitimate Chinese response to Indian aggression

**Argument:** After years of Indian encroachments across the McMahon Line, the Forward Policy, and the establishment of Dhola post in territory China considered its own, China had no choice but to respond with force. China exercised maximum restraint by declaring a unilateral ceasefire and withdrawing 20 km.

**Evidence:** Chinese White Papers from 1962; Zhou Enlai's letters; PLA official history of the war.

**Strengths:** Consistent with China's consistent official position. The unilateral ceasefire and withdrawal are genuinely unusual in the annals of warfare.

**Why it may not be conclusive:** The scale of China's military response was disproportionate to any Indian provocation. China could have pursued diplomatic channels more vigorously. China's ceasefire was self-serving — it achieved its territorial objectives and stopped. India had no opportunity to contest the outcome.

---

### Counterargument 3: India's position on the border is unrealistic and counterproductive

**Argument:** India's insistence on the full Indian claim — including territory China has controlled for 70+ years (Aksai Chin) — makes negotiation impossible. No Chinese government could cede Aksai Chin. India is thereby perpetuating the dispute indefinitely.

**Evidence:** Aksai Chin has been under continuous Chinese administration since the 1950s. China has settled 12 of 14 land borders through compromise. India has not offered any credible compromise.

**Strengths:** Pragmatic and realistic about the military balance and the difficulty of changing effective control. Consistent with Chinese and some third-party analysts' views.

**Why it may not be conclusive:** India's principled position has domestic political legitimacy. Conceding territory to Chinese military pressure would set a dangerous precedent. The dispute's continuation is not solely India's fault — China has also refused compromise.

---

### Counterargument 4: China's Land Border Law (2021) is a routine codification, not an escalation

**Argument:** The law codifies existing Chinese legal positions and does not represent a change in policy or operational approach. Similar laws exist for China's maritime claims. The law does not alter the on-ground situation.

**Evidence:** Text of the Land Border Law; Chinese official explanations; analysis by M. Taylor Fravel.

**Strengths:** Consistent with China's pattern of codifying diverse border-related regulations into single legislation.

**Why it may not be conclusive:** The law's reference to "firmly defending national sovereignty and territorial integrity" and its repudiation of "unequal treaties" provides a legal basis for unilateral action. Its timing (after Galwan) was perceived as threatening.

---

### Counterargument 5: The border can be resolved without addressing Tibet

**Argument:** The border dispute has its own dynamics and can be resolved through bilateral compromise, as China has done with other neighbours. The Tibet question is a separate issue.

**Evidence:** China settled the border with Nepal (1962), Pakistan (1963), Myanmar (1960), and others. None of these settlements required resolving broader political differences.

**Strengths:** There is no inherent logical necessity linking the two issues.

**Why it may not be conclusive:** The Tibet factor is uniquely important in the India-China case because of historical Indian support for Tibetan autonomy, the Dalai Lama's presence in India, and Chinese perceptions of Indian intentions. Scholars like John Garver have argued persuasively that China's border intransigence is fundamentally driven by Tibet-related concerns.

## 16. OPEN QUESTIONS

### What Historians Still Debate

1. **Why did China choose war in 1962?** Was it preemptive (to secure Tibet), punitive (to teach India a lesson), or strategic (to establish a favourable border while China still had a conventional advantage)?

2. **Would Zhou Enlai's 1960 offer have led to a settlement if Nehru had accepted?** Or would China have moved the goalposts once India accepted?

3. **What was the role of the Soviet Union in the 1962 war?** Did the Sino-Soviet split encourage China to act (knowing the USSR would not support India), or constrain China (fearing Soviet intervention)?

4. **How extensive was US and UK support to India during and after the 1962 war?** Declassified Western archives reveal more than previously known, but the full picture remains incomplete.

### What Governments Dispute

1. **The precise alignment of the LAC in at least six sectors:** Neither side's maps match.

2. **Whether China violated bilateral agreements in 2020:** India says yes (patrol blocking, troop buildup); China says it acted within its interpretation.

3. **The status of Tawang:** China claims it; India controls it. Neither has changed its position since 1962.

4. **The upper Subansiri region:** Patrol incursions alleged by both sides.

### Unknown Evidence

1. **Chinese Politburo deliberations (1962):** The full record of Chinese decision-making remains classified.

2. **Full Henderson-Brooks-Bhagat Report:** India has not declassified it despite repeated demands.

3. **Special Representatives negotiations (2003–present):** No public records of what was offered, discussed, or rejected.

4. **China's Land Border Law implementation:** The law's operational impact on ground-level PLA behaviour is not fully documented.

### Archives Not Yet Public

1. **Chinese Ministry of Foreign Affairs Archives:** Partially declassified for the 1950s; 1962-era documents largely unavailable.

2. **PLA Archives:** Generally inaccessible to foreign researchers.

3. **Indian Ministry of Defence Archives:** The Henderson-Brooks report is the most significant classified document.

4. **Tibetan Government-in-Exile Archives:** Potentially significant records in Dharamshala.

### Future Research

1. **Oral history of Indian and Chinese veterans:** Much first-hand knowledge has already been lost.

2. **Comparative analysis of China's border settlements:** Why did China compromise with some neighbours but not India?

3. **Economic impact of border militarisation:** Systematic study of costs to both sides.

4. **Climate change and border security:** Melting glaciers, changing patrol routes, and new strategic vulnerabilities.

5. **Role of third parties (US, Russia, Pakistan):** Systematic analysis of external power influence on the dispute's trajectory.

## 17. LEARNING

### Key Takeaways

1. The India-China border is 3,488 km, not fully delimited, and claimed by both sides based on incompatible legal principles.

2. The 1962 war was brief but transformative — it established the LAC, humiliated India, and traumatised the Indian strategic psyche for a generation.

3. The 1993, 1996, 2005, and 2013 agreements created a CBM framework that prevented major war but did not resolve the core dispute.

4. The 2020 Galwan clash shattered trust and demonstrated that China's military advantage had grown significantly since 1962.

5. Both sides maintain elevated military forces, build infrastructure, and compete across multiple domains — but neither wants a full-scale war.

6. The dispute cannot be resolved without addressing the broader India-China strategic competition, the Tibet factor, and the Pakistan factor.

7. The "cold peace" is stable in the short term but vulnerable to miscalculation, especially in areas where the LAC is ambiguous.
