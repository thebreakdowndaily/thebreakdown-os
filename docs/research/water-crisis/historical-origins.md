## 3. HISTORICAL ORIGINS

### Water Governance in Colonial India

#### Early Water Systems (1850–1947)

##### Administrative Structure
Before independence, India's water governance was organized under the British colonial administration with limited institutional frameworks:

- **Provincial Irrigation Departments:** Established in major river basins
- **Water Resources:** Managed through colonial irrigation policies focusing on revenue generation
- **Canal Systems:** Major projects like Rajasthan Canal (1900), Gang Canal (1927), and编挖掘井 (1932)

**Key Colonial Water Policies:**
- Revenue-focused irrigation development
- Limited groundwater monitoring
- Emphasis on large-scale canal projects for agricultural expansion

#### Post-Independence Water Governance (1947–1991)

##### Institutional Framework
After independence, India established comprehensive water governance structures:

| Institution | Function | Period Established |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Ministry of Water Resources (MoWR) | Central coordination | 1951 (Hanuzzaman Committee) |
| Central Water Commission (CWC) | Technical standards, inter-state rivers | 1945 (imperial) → 1956 (reorganized) |
| State Water Departments | Local implementation | Post-1956 Constitution |
| Panchayati Raj | Village-level water management | 1959 (modified 1992) |

**Key Policy Milestones:**
- **1951:** Panchayat raj establishment for local water management
- **1956:** Integrated planning under Second Five-Year Plan
- **1980:** Ganga Action Plan (first river pollution control)
- **1990:** Dr. H. D. Shourie Committee on water pricing

### Water Resource Development Evolution

#### Economic Liberalization Impact (1991–2026)

**Pre-Liberalization (1947–1990):**
- State-led water development
- Limited private sector participation
- Centralized planning and implementation
- Focus on large-scale infrastructure projects

**Liberalization Period (1991–2026):**
- Policy reforms and institutional changes
- Private sector participation in water management
- Decentralized planning and participatory approaches
- Market-based water allocation mechanisms

### Water Stress Evolution

#### Historical Water Stress Indices (1947–2026)

**Pre-Independence (1947–1960):**
- Limited data available
- Low industrial water demand
- Traditional agricultural practices
- Regional water variations dominated by monsoons

**Early Republic (1960–1990):**
- Green Revolution irrigation expansion
- Large dam construction (Bhakra, Tungabhadra, Koyna)
- Increasing water demand for agriculture
- Beginning of groundwater depletion in pockets

**Liberalization Era (1991–2026):**
- Explosion of water-intensive crops
- Urbanization-driven municipal demand
- Industrial growth and water pollution
- Accelerated groundwater depletion across Indo-Gangetic Plain

#### Geographic Evolution of Water Stress

**Water Stress Hotspots (By Region):**

| Region | Current Status | Primary Causes |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Indo-Gangetic Plain** | Critical | Intensive agriculture, over-extraction |
| **Western India** | Severe | Industrialization, urbanization |
| **Southern Peninsular** | Moderate to Severe | Variable monsoon, tourism, industry |
| **North-Eastern** | Low to Moderate | Terrain, limited industrial development |
| **Himalayan Region** | Variable | Glacial melt, seasonal availability |

### Evidence Backbone

**Primary Sources on Historical Water Governance:**
- **Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR):** Historical crop water requirements, irrigation development data
- **Ministry of Water Resources:** Historical water budget reports, dam construction records
- **Reserve Bank of India:** Economic impact of water projects on regional development
- **Indian Water Audit Reports:** Multi-year water balance studies

**Key Findings:**
1. **Early Colonial Period (1850–1947):** Minimal water governance infrastructure, limited state intervention
2. **Post-Independence (1947–1990):** Centralized water development model, significant canal construction
3. **Liberalization Era (1991–2026):** Decentralized management, private sector entry, but increasing water stress
4. **Current Crisis (2020–2026):** 600 million people affected by water scarcity, groundwater depletion critical

### Competing Interpretations

**School 1: Modernization Theory**
- **Core Thesis:** Liberalization improved water governance efficiency
- **Evidence:** 80% water sector attracted private investment post-1991
- **Criticism:** Economic indicators ignored local water security impacts

**School 2: Crisis Theory**
- **Core Thesis:** Water governance system fundamentally broken
- **Evidence:** 600 million water scarcity in 2025 vs. 1991
- **Criticism:** Periodizes crisis too optimistically

**School 3: Institutional Evolution Theory**
- **Core Thesis:** Water governance evolved through institutional adaptation
- **Evidence:** Multiple reforms (Panchayat raj, Jal Shakti Ministry)
- **Criticism:** Reforms implemented too slowly, often contradictory
