## Six Key Questions

1. **What happened?** India has transitioned from relative water abundance to widespread water stress. Groundwater extraction remains below aggregate recharge nationally (stage ~60% in 2024), but in over-exploited blocks extraction exceeds recharge; the share of over-exploited assessment units was 11.13% in 2024 (down from 17.24% in 2017). *(C-02)*
2. **Why did this happen?** Green Revolution irrigation expansion, power subsidies enabling over-extraction, urbanization, industrialization, climate variability, and fragmented federal governance. *(C-05)*
3. **What alternatives existed?** Cooperative river-basin management, demand-side management, water pricing, payment for ecosystem services, stricter groundwater regulation.
4. **Why this path?** Political economy: agricultural vote-bank importance, fragmented federal authority over water (a State subject), and absence of pricing mechanisms.
5. **What were the consequences?** Environmental degradation, economic costs (projected ~6% GDP loss by 2050 under continued stress), social impacts (rural distress, migration), and strategic implications (transboundary tensions). *(C-04)*
6. **Why does it matter today?** Water security is linked to food security (groundwater supplies ~62% of irrigation water), energy security (thermal cooling), and climate adaptation.

## 1. HERO — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

India's water challenge is among the most consequential resource-security issues facing the world's largest democracy. India supports roughly **18% of the world's population but has access to only about 4% of global freshwater resources** *(C-01)*. The country receives abundant rainfall (~4,000 BCM/year) but can utilise only about 1,100 BCM because of storage limits, uneven distribution, and seasonal concentration of the monsoon *(C-01)*.

The challenge spans multiple dimensions simultaneously:

- **Hydrological:** declining groundwater tables, erratic monsoon patterns, Himalayan-basin variations
- **Agricultural:** over-extraction for irrigation, water-intensive cropping patterns
- **Urban:** rapid urbanization, municipal over-extraction, infrastructure gaps
- **Industrial:** thermal-power cooling, manufacturing discharge
- **Ecological:** river degradation, wetland loss, biodiversity threats

India's water insecurity cannot be understood through any single lens. It requires an evidence-first approach that analyses all drivers across all sectors.

### Why This Matters Today

According to NITI Aayog's Composite Water Management Index (2018), **about 600 million Indians face high to extreme water stress**, and the country's water demand is projected to be twice available supply by 2030, implying severe scarcity for hundreds of millions and an eventual **~6% loss in GDP** under continued stress *(C-04)*. The crisis intersects with climate change, producing population displacement, rural distress, interstate water disputes, and geopolitical tension with upstream neighbours (China on the Brahmaputra; Pakistan on the Indus). Understanding this crisis is essential knowledge for comprehending a central domestic and strategic challenge that will shape India's trajectory for decades.

### Scope

This monograph traces India's water stress from the colonial period through 2050, with emphasis on the post-1991 period to the present. Thematic areas:

- **Water resource systems** — availability, distribution, utilization
- **Agricultural irrigation** — watershed development, canal modernization, efficiency
- **Urban water delivery** — municipal stress, demand management, infrastructure
- **Industrial water use** — power cooling, heavy industry, pollution
- **Ecological damage** — river degradation, wetland loss, restoration
- **Governance** — interstate disputes, central–state coordination, treaties
- **Policy responses** — Jal Jeevan Mission, Atal Bhujal Yojana, National Water Policy

### Key Evidence Challenges

**Groundwater data.** The Central Ground Water Board (CGWB), in collaboration with State Groundwater Departments, conducts periodic Dynamic Ground Water Resource Assessments. The 2024 assessment reports:

- Total annual groundwater recharge: **446.90 BCM**
- Extractable resource: **406.19 BCM**
- Annual extraction: **245.64 BCM** (stage of extraction ~60%)
- Over-exploited assessment units: **11.13%** (down from 17.24% in 2017) *(C-02)*

Estimates diverge across agencies on *future* depletion trajectories and on the precise contribution of informal irrigation, but the directional finding — widespread over-exploitation in the northwest and parts of peninsular India — is well established.

**Agricultural water use.** Agriculture accounts for **~89% of groundwater extracted** and **~85% of total water use** in India *(C-03)*. CWC places overall water-use efficiency of major/medium irrigation projects at only ~36% (2020–21). Estimates of total irrigation demand vary (Ministry of Agriculture vs. IWMI crop-area based), but all place agriculture as the dominant user.

**Governance data gaps.** Water is a State subject (Schedule VII, Constitution). Overlapping authority (Ministry of Jal Shakti, CWC, CGWB, state ministries) and no unified implementation-status tracking impede evidence-based planning.

### Path Forward (Evidence Requirements)

1. Centralized water repository with standardized, verified metrics
2. Integrated governance platform linking national, state, and local data
3. Unified groundwater monitoring (remote sensing + standardized protocols)
4. Inter-ministerial coordination framework for evidence sharing
5. Independent verification mechanisms for water-policy claims

### Water Stress Evolution

- **Early Republic (1960–1990):** Green Revolution irrigation expansion; major dam construction (Bhakra, Tungabhadra, Koyna); onset of localized groundwater depletion.
- **Liberalization Era (1991–present):** expansion of water-intensive crops; urbanization-driven municipal demand; industrial growth and pollution; accelerated Indo-Gangetic groundwater depletion.

### Geographic Evolution of Water Stress

| Region | Current Status | Primary Causes |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Indo-Gangetic Plain | Critical (over-exploited blocks) | Intensive agriculture, over-extraction |
| Western India | Severe | Industrialization, urbanization, arid climate |
| Southern Peninsular | Moderate–Severe | Variable monsoon, hard-rock aquifers |
| North-Eastern | Low–Moderate | Terrain, limited industrial development |
| Himalayan Region | Variable | Glacial melt, seasonal availability |

*Status reflects CGWB over-exploitation classification and NITI Aayog CWMI low-performer states (e.g., Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Haryana in the Ganga basin). Specific per-state scores require the latest CWMI edition. `{EVIDENCE NEEDED: cite latest CWMI edition for per-region scores}`*

### Competing Interpretations

**School 1 — Modernization / Efficiency.** Liberalization improved water-governance efficiency via private investment and decentralization. *Criticism:* aggregate efficiency gains masked local water-security losses; groundwater development rose from 58% (2004) to 62% (2011) even as over-exploitation persisted *(C-05)*.

**School 2 — Crisis Theory.** The water-governance system is fundamentally unable to arrest depletion; ~11% of assessment units remain over-exploited and parts of the Indo-Gangetic Plain show long-term decline. *Criticism:* risks over-periodizing the crisis and understates recent recharge gains (over-exploited units fell from 17.24% in 2017 to 11.13% in 2024).

**School 3 — Institutional Evolution.** Governance adapted through reforms (Panchayati Raj, Jal Shakti Ministry, Atal Bhujal Yojana), if slowly and contradictorily. *Criticism:* reforms often incremental, not transformative, and implementation is a State responsibility with uneven uptake.

### Why Historical Context Matters

1. Infrastructure legacy (large dams/canals) created ecological debts.
2. Institutional path dependence constrains innovative response.
3. Green Revolution water use set unsustainable patterns.
4. Regional disparities trace to colonial/post-colonial development.
