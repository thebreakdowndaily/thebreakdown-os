# Editorial Style Guide

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Operational
**Governance:** Level 3 (Project Document). May not contradict the Editorial Constitution.

---

## Voice

The Breakdown writes as an institution, not a person. The voice is:

- **Authoritative** but not arrogant — claims are supported by evidence
- **Clear** but not simplistic — complexity is respected, not avoided
- **Fair** but not neutral on matters of established fact — the evidence decides
- **Transparent** but not self-referential — the chapter is about the subject, not the institution

The reader should never need to ask: "Who wrote this?" or "Why should I trust this?"

## Tone

| Quality | Standard | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Confidence | Commensurate with evidence | "The evidence suggests..." not "Clearly..." |
| Certainty | Explicitly graded | "Three independent sources confirm..." not "It is a fact that..." |
| Disagreement | Attributed | "Historian X argues Y, while Scholar Z contends W" |
| Complexity | Acknowledged | "The picture is more complicated than simple narratives suggest" |
| Uncertainty | Named | "The archival record is silent on this question" |

## Prohibited Language

The following words and phrases are prohibited in all knowledge objects. They signal false certainty or rhetorical manipulation rather than evidence-based judgment.

**False certainty:** clearly, obviously, undoubtedly, unquestionably, of course, naturally, trivially, inevitably, surely, certainly
**Rhetorical force:** it must be noted, it is important to remember, it cannot be denied, there is no doubt that, as everyone knows
**False equivalence:** both sides, on the one hand... on the other hand (when the evidence is not evenly balanced)
**Presentism:** would have thought, should have known, could have predicted, ahead of its time, backwards, primitive
**False narrative:** turning point, watershed, game-changer, paradigm shift (without defining the change)
**Vague attribution:** some say, many believe, it is widely thought, critics argue (without naming the attributed source)

## Structure

Every chapter must distinguish four layers. The reader should be able to identify which layer they are reading at any point.

### Layer 1 — What Happened

The factual sequence of events. Minimum interpretation. Supported by primary sources.

*Example: "On 15 August 1947, India became independent. The partition of the subcontinent created two new dominions."*

### Layer 2 — What the Evidence Shows

The evidentiary basis for claims. Sources are named. Confidence is indicated.

*Example: "The transfer of power papers, released in 1972, show that Mountbatten advanced the date from June 1948 to August 1947. The record does not fully explain why."*

### Layer 3 — Where Historians Disagree

Competing interpretations attributed to named scholars or schools. The basis for disagreement is explained.

*Example: "Jalal argues that Jinnah never wanted a separate state and used the Pakistan demand as a bargaining chip. This interpretation relies heavily on Jinnah's private correspondence. Singh contests this reading."*

### Layer 4 — Why It Matters

Why the reader should care. What is at stake in understanding this subject. How it connects to present concerns.

*Example: "The speed of partition matters because it shaped the institutional capacity of both states. A slower transition might have produced different border arrangements, different population movements, and a different relationship between the two countries."*

## Evidence Presentation

Every factual claim must be traceable to evidence. The reader should be able to verify the claim by following the citation.

### Inline citations

Use a numbered citation that links to the Source Registry:

```
The transfer of power was advanced from June 1948 to August 1947[1].
```

### Evidence panels

For claims where the evidence is contested or particularly important, use an evidence panel that shows:

- The claim
- The supporting evidence (with source and excerpt)
- The counter-evidence (if any)
- The confidence score
- The reasoning behind the confidence score

### Confidence language

| Confidence | Language |
|---|---|
| 5 (Conclusive) | "The evidence establishes that..." |
| 4 (Strong) | "The evidence strongly suggests that..." |
| 3 (Moderate) | "The evidence indicates that..." |
| 2 (Weak) | "Limited evidence suggests that..." |
| 1 (Insufficient) | "The evidence is insufficient to determine..." |

## Attribution

- Every factual claim is attributed to a source (implicitly via citation)
- Every interpretation is attributed to a named person or school
- Every competing interpretation is attributed to its proponents
- Anonymous sources are not used
- Sources are evaluated for reliability, and reliability informs the confidence score

## Writing Mechanics

**Paragraphs:** Maximum 8–10 lines. One governing idea per paragraph.
**Sentences:** Maximum 35 words. One clause per sentence where possible.
**Headings:** Use governing questions where possible rather than declarative headings.
**Transitions:** Every section has an entry transition (why this section exists) and an exit transition (why the next section is necessary).
**First paragraph:** The opening paragraph establishes stakes or tension. It does not begin at the chronological start.
**Last paragraph:** The closing paragraph returns to the present and states what the reader should now understand.

## Glossary

Every chapter has a glossary. Terms are defined for a non-specialist reader in one to two sentences. The first use of a glossary term in the narrative is marked (bold or linked to the glossary).

*Example: **Non-alignment:** The foreign policy doctrine of not formally aligning with either the US or Soviet blocs during the Cold War.*

## Disagreements

- Disagreements are presented at the point they arise in the narrative, not collected at the end
- Each disagreement is attributed to named scholars or schools
- The evidence basis for each side is presented
- The chapter does not resolve disagreements that scholarship has not resolved
- Where the chapter takes a position, it explains why the evidence supports that position
