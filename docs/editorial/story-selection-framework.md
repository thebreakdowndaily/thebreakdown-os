# Story Selection Framework

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Operational

How the Editorial Office decides which stories to pursue.

---

## Principles

1. **Evidence first.** A story is only worth pursuing if sufficient evidence exists to support it. If the evidence is not available, the story waits.
2. **Reader relevance.** The story must answer a question a reader is likely to ask. Not "is this interesting?" but "would a reader seek this out?"
3. **Institutional fit.** The story must belong to an existing collection or justify a new collection. Standalone chapters without collection context are not published.
4. **Capacity.** The office does not start a story it cannot finish. Available editorial resources constrain active stories.

## Decision criteria

Every story proposal is scored on five criteria. Each criterion is scored 1–5.

| Criterion | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Evidence availability** | No primary sources identified | Few sources, mostly secondary | Adequate secondary, some primary | Strong primary and secondary | Rich archival record with multiple perspectives |
| **Reader demand** | Niche academic interest | Specialist audience | Informed general reader | Broad public interest | Foundational knowledge that many readers seek |
| **Institutional value** | Tangential to collections | Supports a collection entry | Core to a collection | Definitive treatment of a collection topic | A flagship chapter that defines a collection |
| **Narrative potential** | Chronological list of events | Single clear arc | Competing interpretations enrich the story | Multiple patterns possible | A story that readers will reference and return to |
| **Differentiation** | Well-covered elsewhere | Existing coverage is thin | Existing coverage is misleading or incomplete | No existing treatment meets Breakdown standards | The Breakdown can become the definitive reference |

## Scoring

- **20–25:** Proceed. High-priority chapter.
- **15–19:** Proceed with conditions. Address gaps before starting.
- **10–14:** Hold. Reassess when evidence or capacity changes.
- **5–9:** Do not pursue. Not suitable for the platform.

## Priority matrix

Once scored, stories are prioritised by:

### Urgency

- Time-sensitive (current events connection, upcoming anniversary)
- Collection-completion (needed to finish a volume)
- Foundation (earlier chapters must exist before later ones)
- Standalone (no dependency on other chapters)

### Dependencies

A story may not start until its dependencies are met:

- Prerequisite chapters must be at least in editorial review
- Evidence acquisition must be underway or complete
- Visual assets must be identifiable (not necessarily acquired)
- The story must belong to a collection that has been started

## Capacity management

The Editorial Office maintains an active story limit. The limit is determined by:

- Number of available editors
- Current stage of each active story
- Expected completion timeline
- Review bandwidth (peer reviewers are volunteers with other commitments)

## Decision process

1. Editor proposes story (one page proposal)
2. Weekly editorial meeting reviews proposal
3. If scored 15+, story enters backlog
4. When capacity permits, story moves to active
5. Story Architect assigned
6. Research begins

## Stopping a story

A story may be stopped at any stage if:

- Evidence proves insufficient to support a confident narrative
- A key source cannot be obtained or verified
- The story proves too similar to an existing publication
- Editorial capacity shifts to higher-priority work

Stopped stories are documented with the reason and the stage at which they were stopped. They may be resumed if conditions change.
