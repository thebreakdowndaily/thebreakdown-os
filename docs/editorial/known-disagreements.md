# Known Disagreements Register

**A record of scholarly disagreements that The Breakdown does not attempt to resolve.**

**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** Active  
**Last Updated:** July 2026  

---

This register documents areas of genuine scholarly disagreement relevant to Breakdown knowledge objects. It exists for two purposes:

1. **Transparency** — Readers can see that disagreements are known, tracked, and surfaced rather than hidden.
2. **Editorial discipline** — Editors are reminded not to present contested questions as settled.

Each entry records the question, the range of positions, the scholars associated with each position, and how The Breakdown's editorial judgment handles the disagreement in its knowledge objects.

## Disagreement #0001 — Partition Death Toll

**Question:** How many people died in the Partition violence of 1947–48?

| Estimate | Source type | Supported by |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| ~200,000 | Official British figures | Contemporary government records |
| ~500,000 | Mid-range estimate | Some early scholarly accounts |
| 800,000 – 1 million | Scholarly consensus | Demographic analysis (1951 Census Commissioner), Khan, Bhagat |
| ~2 million | Contemporary press | Newspaper reports at the time |

**Status:** No scholarly consensus on an exact figure. The range reflects different methodologies (official counts vs demographic modelling vs press accounts) and different definitions (direct violence only vs deaths from displacement, disease, and starvation).

**The Breakdown's position:** Use the scholarly consensus range (800,000–1 million) as the primary figure. Note the full range and cite sources for each estimate. Label confidence as "strong" for the range, "contested" for any specific number.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0002 — Could Partition Have Been Avoided?

**Question:** Was Partition inevitable by 1946, or could a different political strategy have preserved a united India?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence base |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Inevitable by 1940s | Pakistani nationalist school; some British historians | Two-Nation theory had deep social roots; Muslim League demand was genuine and mass-supported |
| Avoidable with different Congress strategy | Cambridge revisionist school (Jalal, Seal) | Congress rejection of Cabinet Mission Plan; Nehru's 10 July 1946 statement; Jinnah's willingness to accept federal solution |
| Avoidable with different British approach | Nationalist school (Chandra, Mukherjee) | British divide-and-rule policies; accelerated Mountbatten timeline; deliberate favouring of League |
| Overdetermined — multiple causes made avoidance unlikely | Synthesis view (Khan, Guha) | Convergence of imperial decline, elite competition, communal violence, and administrative collapse |

**Status:** Deeply divided. This is the central historiographical question in Partition studies.

**The Breakdown's position:** Present all four positions fairly. Note that the revisionist position has gained significant scholarly traction but faces strong criticism from both nationalist and Pakistani schools. The chapter's narrative treats inevitability as a retrospective illusion while acknowledging that by early 1947, the range of possible outcomes had narrowed dramatically.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0003 — Jinnah's Intentions

**Question:** Did Jinnah actually want a separate Pakistan, or was he using the demand as a bargaining strategy?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Jinnah always wanted Pakistan | Pakistani nationalist school; earlier British accounts | Lahore Resolution (1940); Jinnah's public statements; Two-Nation theory speeches |
| Jinnah used Pakistan as a bargaining counter | Revisionist school (Jalal) | Jinnah's acceptance of Cabinet Mission Plan; private correspondence; ambiguous public statements; willingness to accept less than full sovereignty |
| Jinnah's intentions evolved | Middle position (Khan, Guha) | Early ambiguity hardened into genuine demand after Congress rejected power-sharing |

**Status:** The revisionist position (Jalal) transformed the field and is now widely taught but remains contested, particularly in Pakistan.

**The Breakdown's position:** Present Jalal's argument as the dominant scholarly position while noting the criticisms. Chapter 1 frames Jinnah's strategy as ambiguous and evolving, consistent with the middle position.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0004 — British Responsibility for Partition

**Question:** How responsible were British policies for causing Partition?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Primary cause — deliberate divide and rule | Nationalist school (Chandra, Mukherjee) | Separate electorates (1909); communal representation; wartime favouritism toward League |
| Partial cause — reactive rather than manipulative | Cambridge school (Seal, Bayly) | British were responding to Indian political dynamics, not creating them; empire was too weak to manipulate |
| Administrative collapse, not design | Social history (Khan) | British state was too depleted by WWII to impose any outcome; partition happened by default |
| Strategic choice — served British Cold War interests | Strategic school (Raghavan) | Accelerated partition served British interests in maintaining influence and securing Pakistan as an ally |

**Status:** Varies by school. The "deliberate design" position is strongest for earlier periods (1909–1930s) but weaker for 1946–47, where contingency and collapse dominate.

**The Breakdown's position:** Present the long arc of divide-and-rule (pre-1940) but emphasise administrative collapse and strategic calculation for the final phase (1945–47). This avoids over-attributing agency to a declining imperial state while acknowledging the structural role of British institutions.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0005 — Nehru vs Patel on Foreign Policy

**Question:** Would Indian foreign policy have been significantly different under Patel's leadership?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Yes — Patel was more realist, less idealist | Popular narrative; some strategic analysts | Patel's harder line on Pakistan; scepticism of UN; preference for military action (Hyderabad); closer ties to business interests |
| No — the differences were overstated | Raghavan, Guha | Patel supported non-alignment; approved key foreign policy decisions; the Nehru-Patel relationship was complementary, not oppositional |
| We cannot know — Patel died too early | Cautious position | Patel died in December 1950; his full foreign policy vision was never articulated; extrapolation from limited evidence is speculative |

**Status:** The question is inherently speculative. Evidence is limited.

**The Breakdown's position:** Present as an important counterfactual, not a settled question. Note that on the core questions (Pakistan, China, US relations), Patel's recorded views were closer to Nehru's than popular memory suggests, but significant temperamental differences existed.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0006 — Was the Kashmir UN Referral a Mistake?

**Question:** Did Nehru make a strategic error by referring the Kashmir dispute to the UN?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Yes — it internationalised a bilateral dispute | Critics (many Indian strategists, Raghavan) | UN failed to enforce Resolution 47; Pakistan gained diplomatic parity; the dispute remains unresolved 75+ years later |
| No — India had no better option | Defenders (Gopal, some diplomatic historians) | India lacked military capacity to expel tribal invaders; UN referral bought time and legitimacy; alternative was full-scale war India could not win |
| The error was not referral but execution | Nuanced position (Raja Mohan) | India should have been more realistic about UN politics; Nehru overestimated the power of legal argument |

**Status:** This remains one of the most debated questions in Indian foreign policy historiography. The critical position has become more influential over time.

**The Breakdown's position:** Present all three positions. Acknowledge that the critical view has strong evidence but that defenders have a plausible case about the constraints Nehru faced. The chapter treats it as a legitimate strategic choice made under severe constraints, not as naive idealism.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0007 — How Decisive Was Mountbatten's Role?

**Question:** Did Lord Mountbatten's personal decisions significantly affect the outcome, or was he implementing inevitable British policy?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Mountbatten accelerated the timeline substantially | Traditional view (many historians) | Original timeline was 1948; Mountbatten compressed it to August 1947; his personal vanity and desire for a dramatic exit drove the schedule |
| Mountbatten had limited room to manoeuvre | Revisionist view (some British historians) | British government had already decided on rapid exit; Attlee's 20 Feb 1947 statement set the timeline; Mountbatten executed policy rather than shaped it |
| Mountbatten's decisions on the boundary were consequential | Administrative historians | Mountbatten's choice of Radcliffe, the compressed boundary process, and the decision to announce the boundary after independence all had major consequences |

**Status:** The traditional view emphasising Mountbatten's personal agency remains widely held. The revisionist view has gained ground.

**The Breakdown's position:** Acknowledge the constraints Mountbatten operated under while noting that the compressed timeline was his decision and had catastrophic consequences. The chapter treats Mountbatten as neither villain nor innocent; his choices mattered within a framework he did not fully control.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

## Disagreement #0008 — Role of Violence in Driving Partition

**Question:** Was Partition driven by political negotiation or by violence on the ground?

| Position | Key scholars | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Violence was a consequence, not a cause | Political historians (many traditional accounts) | The political decisions preceded the violence; violence was a reaction to political failure |
| Violence was an independent causal force | Subaltern school (Pandey, Butalia) | Violence created realities (destroyed mixed neighbourhoods, hardened identities, produced refugees) that made partition irreversible |
| Violence was instrumental — used by political actors | Instrumentalist view (some scholars) | Violence was organised and directed by political actors for political ends |

**Status:** The subaltern position has transformed the field but the causal question remains debated.

**The Breakdown's position:** The chapter treats violence as both consequence and cause. It acknowledges that the political breakdown (Cabinet Mission failure) preceded the worst violence, but argues that post-1946 violence created its own momentum and narrowed political options.

**Last reviewed:** July 2026
