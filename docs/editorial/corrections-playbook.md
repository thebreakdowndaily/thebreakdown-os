# Corrections Playbook

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Operational

How the Editorial Office receives, triages, verifies, publishes, and notifies corrections. This playbook is the operational counterpart to the `living-update.md` workflow. It guides the humans, not the system.

---

## Principles

1. **Corrections are a strength.** A transparent correction history builds more trust than a claim of never making mistakes.
2. **Speed matters less than accuracy.** A correction published quickly but incorrectly damages trust faster than the original error.
3. **Every correction is documented.** What changed, why it changed, what evidence supports the change, and the new version number.
4. **Corrections cascade.** If a correction affects one claim, check every chapter that references that claim.

---

## Triage categories

| Category | Definition | SLA | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Critical | Factual error changes understanding | 48 hours to acknowledge, 7 days to resolve | Immediate flag. Escalate to Editor-in-Chief. |
| Standard | Factual error in supporting detail | 7 days to acknowledge, 14 days to resolve | Route through living-update workflow. |
| Cosmetic | Typo, formatting, broken link | 14 days to acknowledge, 30 days to resolve | Batch with next update cycle. |
| Interpretive | Disagreement with framing, not facts | No SLA | Respond with explanation of editorial reasoning. May become a Know Disagreement entry. |
| Out of scope | Not about Breakdown content | — | Respond with explanation. Close. |

## Receipt

When a correction submission arrives:

1. **Acknowledge receipt** — within 24 hours, confirm that the submission was received
2. **Record the submission** — in the correction ticket: date, submitter contact (if provided), exact claim or passage, what the submitter believes is wrong, what they believe is correct
3. **Assign priority** — critical, standard, cosmetic, interpretive, or out of scope

## Triage

For each submission, determine:

1. **Is it factual?** If it is an interpretive disagreement, route to Know Disagreements, not corrections.
2. **Is it within scope?** Does it concern Breakdown content? If not, close.
3. **Is it already known?** Check the Book of Record and the Claims Registry. If the error is already documented, respond with the existing correction record.
4. **Is it verifiable?** Can the claimed correction be verified against sources? If not, request more information from the submitter.

## Verification

1. **Find the source.** What is the submitter's evidence? If they did not provide evidence, the editorial team must identify it.
2. **Check the claim.** What does the current Claim Registry say? Is the original claim supported by evidence?
3. **Check the correction.** Does the corrected version have evidence? Is the evidence stronger?
4. **Resolve.** Three possible outcomes:
   - Error confirmed — the published claim is incorrect
   - Error not confirmed — the published claim is accurate
   - Ambiguous — evidence is inconclusive, correction not applied

## Scope assessment

Before making any change, determine what is affected:

- Which claims in the Claim Registry need updating?
- Which narrative sections need revision?
- Which evidence items need replacement or addition?
- Which cross-references (other chapters citing the same claim) need updating?
- Is the change surgical (one claim, one section) or extended (changes interpretation of surrounding paragraphs)?

## Approval

- All corrections require Editor-in-Chief approval
- Critical corrections require immediate notification of the Editor-in-Chief
- The approval document states: what changed, why, version increment, publication timeline

## Publication

1. **Update the Claim Registry** — mark the old claim with revision history, add the corrected claim
2. **Revise the narrative** — change only the affected paragraphs, preserve everything else byte-identical
3. **Write the change log** — what changed, before and after, why, evidence for the change, previous version, new version
4. **Add revision badge** — "This passage was revised on [date]. View change log." — display for 30 days
5. **Publish** — through the human approval gate

## Notification

After publication:

1. **Notify the submitter** — "Your correction has been reviewed and published. View change."
2. **Notify cross-referenced chapters** — inform editors of affected chapters that a correction has been made to a shared claim
3. **Log in Book of Record** — date, ticket ID, what changed, why, who submitted, new version number

## Special cases

### Anonymous submissions

Anonymous corrections are accepted and processed the same way. The submitter cannot be notified, so the correction record must be published visibly in the change log.

### Submitter disagrees with outcome

If a submitter disputes the correction outcome (error not confirmed, ambiguous), the editorial team responds with a written explanation of the evidence and reasoning. If the submitter provides new evidence, the correction is re-opened.

### Pattern of errors

If the same type of error appears across multiple chapters (e.g., a misattributed source used in several places), the correction cascades. Every affected chapter is updated through a separate living-update workflow. The pattern is reported in the Monthly Editorial Review.

### Retractions

Retractions follow the full pipeline in reverse. Only the Editor-in-Chief may initiate a retraction. The chapter is unpublished, the Knowledge Graph is updated, and a public notice is issued. Retractions are distinct from corrections — they mean the chapter should not be relied upon.
