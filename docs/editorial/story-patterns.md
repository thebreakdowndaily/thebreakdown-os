# Story Patterns

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Operational

A library of narrative archetypes. Every chapter selects exactly one pattern. The pattern governs the narrative arc, the evidence placement, and the reader's expectations.

---

## How to use this guide

1. After the research brief and knowledge model are complete, the Story Architect selects the pattern that best fits the evidence
2. The pattern determines the chapter structure — not the content
3. A pattern may be mixed with elements of another pattern only when explicitly noted
4. If no pattern fits, a new pattern may be proposed through the story architecture review

---

## 1. Crisis

**Use when:** A specific event or moment of acute threat or decision.

**Central question:** What happened, what was at stake, and how was it resolved?

**Arc:**
- Pre-crisis context — what was normal before
- The trigger — what changed
- Escalation — why it got worse
- Decision point — what choices existed
- Resolution — what happened
- Aftermath — what changed as a result

**Evidence placement:** Contemporary accounts, decision-maker records, immediate aftermath reports.

**Examples:** The 1962 War, the 1947 Partition violence, the 1991 economic crisis.

**Reader takeaway:** Understanding how a specific crisis unfolded reveals the contingency of outcomes.

## 2. Decision

**Use when:** A specific choice by an individual or institution shaped events.

**Central question:** Why was this choice made, what alternatives existed, and why was this one chosen?

**Arc:**
- The context in which the decision was made
- The alternatives that were considered
- The decision process — who was involved, what evidence was weighed
- The decision itself
- The consequences — intended and unintended
- Counterfactual — what the alternatives might have produced

**Evidence placement:** Decision-maker records, meeting minutes, contemporary correspondence, memoirs.

**Examples:** Nehru's choice of non-alignment, the decision to go to war in 1962, the choice of economic liberalisation in 1991.

**Reader takeaway:** Decisions were made by people with incomplete information. Understanding the constraints explains the outcome.

## 3. Institution

**Use when:** The subject is an organisation, its evolution, and its impact.

**Central question:** How did this institution form, how did it change, and what effect did it have?

**Arc:**
- Founding — why and how the institution was created
- Early years — formative experiences that shaped it
- Challenges — crises that tested it
- Evolution — how it changed over time
- Impact — what it has produced or enabled
- Present — where it stands now

**Evidence placement:** Founding documents, institutional records, leadership decisions, external assessments.

**Examples:** The United Nations, the Indian Constitution, the Non-Aligned Movement, the Planning Commission.

**Reader takeaway:** Institutions are not static. They evolve in response to internal and external pressures.

## 4. Biography

**Use when:** A person's life illuminates a historical period or question.

**Central question:** How did this person's choices, ideas, and circumstances shape events?

**Arc:**
- Formative context — the world they were born into
- Intellectual formation — what shaped their thinking
- Rise — how they gained influence
- Decisions — the critical choices they made
- Legacy — what they left behind and how it is contested

**Evidence placement:** Personal correspondence, contemporary accounts, speeches and writings, later assessments.

**Examples:** Nehru, Patel, Jinnah, Mountbatten, Ambedkar.

**Reader takeaway:** Individuals matter, but they operate within constraints. Understanding both explains history.

## 5. Policy Evolution

**Use when:** A policy or doctrine developed over time through multiple decisions.

**Central question:** How did this policy emerge, change, and produce its current form?

**Arc:**
- Origin — why the policy was first created
- Early application — initial results and adjustments
- Challenges — what forced changes
- Reform — how it was redesigned
- Current form — what it looks like now
- Debate — what is contested about it

**Evidence placement:** Policy documents, parliamentary debates, implementation reports, impact assessments.

**Examples:** India's nuclear doctrine, the evolution of the Indus Waters Treaty, foreign aid policy.

**Reader takeaway:** Policy is rarely designed whole — it accumulates through decisions, reactions, and revisions.

## 6. Conflict

**Use when:** A sustained adversarial relationship with multiple phases.

**Central question:** Why did this conflict persist, what drove escalation and de-escalation, and what maintained it?

**Arc:**
- Origin — the root cause
- Early phase — the initial shape of the conflict
- Escalation — what made it worse
- Turning points — what changed the trajectory
- Stalemate or resolution — where it settled
- Legacy — how it affects the present

**Evidence placement:** Multiple sources from both sides, third-party accounts, declassified records, agreements.

**Examples:** The India-China border dispute, the Kashmir conflict, the India-Pakistan rivalry.

**Reader takeaway:** Conflicts persist because they serve purposes or resolve structural tensions. Understanding those forces is necessary before resolution is possible.

## 7. Technology

**Use when:** A technology, scientific development, or technical system changed outcomes.

**Central question:** How did this technology develop, what problem did it solve, and what unintended consequences did it produce?

**Arc:**
- Pre-technology — how things worked before
- Development — how the technology was created or adopted
- Implementation — how it was deployed
- Impact — what changed as a result
- Unintended consequences — what was not anticipated
- Adaptation — how society adjusted

**Evidence placement:** Scientific publications, technical reports, deployment records, impact studies.

**Examples:** The Green Revolution, nuclear technology, the space programme, communications technology.

**Reader takeaway:** Technology is not neutral. It reshapes power, relationships, and outcomes in ways that are never entirely predictable.

## 8. Economics

**Use when:** Economic forces, structures, or decisions are the primary driver of events.

**Central question:** How did economic constraints and choices shape outcomes?

**Arc:**
- Economic context — the baseline conditions
- Challenge — the problem that needed solving
- Policy response — what was decided and why
- Implementation — how it worked in practice
- Results — intended and unintended
- Distribution — who benefited and who bore costs

**Evidence placement:** Economic data, policy documents, budget records, impact assessments, academic studies.

**Examples:** The 1991 economic reforms, the Five-Year Plans, the debt trap diplomacy narrative.

**Reader takeaway:** Economic choices are political choices. Every policy creates winners and losers.

## Pattern selection matrix

| If the subject is... | Use pattern | Because... |
|---|---|---|
| A single event with clear stakes | Crisis | The event structure drives the narrative |
| A choice that shaped history | Decision | The decision process is the narrative |
| An organisation with history | Institution | Evolution over time is the story |
| A person who mattered | Biography | The person's life illuminates the period |
| A doctrine that evolved | Policy Evolution | Accumulated decisions matter more than any single event |
| A sustained rivalry | Conflict | Phases and turning points structure the narrative |
| A technology that changed things | Technology | Before-and-after comparison is the arc |
| An economic transformation | Economics | Forces and incentives drive the narrative |

## Pattern combination rules

- One primary pattern per chapter
- Secondary pattern elements may be used in specific sections (e.g., a biography section within an institution pattern)
- The primary pattern must be declared in the story blueprint
- The chapter title and opening should signal the pattern to the reader
