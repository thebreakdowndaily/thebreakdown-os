This Book of Record records every significant editorial decision made during the creation, review, and maintenance of knowledge objects. It functions as the platform's institutional memory — future editors should consult it to understand not just what was decided, but why.

### Decision #0001

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Partition historiography — terminology
- **Decision:** Use "Partition violence" instead of "communal riots."
- **Reasoning:** The term "communal riots" implies symmetrical, spontaneous conflict between communities of roughly equal capacity. It minimises the scale of organised killing, the role of state actors, and the genocidal dimension of the violence. "Partition violence" is more accurate, carries less minimising baggage, and is supported by the dominant scholarly usage (Pandey, Butalia, Khan).
- **Alternatives considered:** "Communal riots" (rejected — minimises), "Genocide" (rejected — does not fit legal definition for the entire event, though some episodes qualify), "Mass violence" (considered but less specific).
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1 — India's Inheritance
- **Reversibility:** Reversible by consensus of the Review Board if scholarly usage shifts.

### Decision #0002

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Confidence scores — counterfactuals
- **Decision:** Counterfactuals shall never receive confidence scores.
- **Reasoning:** Counterfactuals are analytical tools for exploring contingency, not historical claims. Assigning them a confidence score would mislead readers into treating them as factual propositions. Counterfactuals shall be clearly labelled and distinguished from the historical narrative.
- **Alternatives considered:** Assigning "speculative" confidence (rejected — still risks confusion).
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Editorial Constitution Article IV; Counterfactual blocks in Chapter 1
- **Reversibility:** Permanent unless the category of "counterfactual" is itself redefined.

### Decision #0003

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Maps — disputed boundaries
- **Decision:** All historical maps must explicitly indicate disputed boundaries using standard cartographic conventions (dashed lines, labelled notes). Maps shall not present disputed borders as settled fact.
- **Reasoning:** Mapping a disputed boundary as a solid line silently endorses one side's claim. This is incompatible with the Neutrality Constitution. Dashed lines with notes preserve evidentiary honesty.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Editorial Constitution Article VIII; Visual Asset Acquisition List
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if both parties to a dispute formally settle the boundary and international recognition follows.

### Decision #0004

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Citing oral testimony as evidence
- **Decision:** Oral testimony may be cited as Level 6 evidence but shall never be the sole source for an established fact claim. It must be corroborated by at least one Level 1–3 source.
- **Reasoning:** Oral testimony is essential for recovering experiences excluded from written records (particularly women, lower castes, and subaltern groups). But memory is fallible, and retrospective testimony gathered decades after events carries well-documented reliability limitations. Corroboration protects against uncritical reliance on memory.
- **Alternatives considered:** Excluding oral testimony entirely (rejected — loses essential perspectives); treating it as Level 2 (rejected — reliability concerns).
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Editorial Constitution Article III; Butalia (s20), Pandey (s19)
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if new methodology significantly improves oral testimony reliability.

### Decision #0005

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** AI-generated images in historical chapters
- **Decision:** AI-generated images are prohibited in historical reporting. They may be used for illustrative diagrams where no authentic visual exists, but shall be clearly labelled as AI-generated in both the caption and alt text.
- **Reasoning:** Visuals are evidence (Article VIII). An AI-generated image of a historical event is not evidence — it is a synthetic reconstruction that may contain anachronisms, fabrications, or biases embedded in the training data. Using it risks misleading readers. Diagrams and explanatory graphics are a different category (they explain concepts, not depict events) and are acceptable with clear labelling.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Editorial Constitution Article VIII
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if AI image generation becomes reliably authenticated and provenance-verified.

### Decision #0006

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Presenting death toll estimates for Partition
- **Decision:** Use the range "800,000 to 1 million" as the best scholarly estimate, with a footnote detailing the full range of estimates (200,000 to 2 million) and the sources for each.
- **Reasoning:** The lower bound (200,000, from official figures) is widely regarded as an undercount. The upper bound (2 million, from contemporary press) is not supported by demographic analysis. The scholarly consensus, based on 1951 Census Commissioner demographic analysis and subsequent research by Bhagat, Khan, and others, converges on 800,000–1 million. Presenting the consensus without suppressing the debate is the correct approach.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1; evidence-summary block (b-evidence-trauma)
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if new demographic research revises the consensus.

### Decision #0007

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Referencing the Two-Nation Theory
- **Decision:** Present the Two-Nation Theory as a historical claim made by the Muslim League, not as a description of reality. Always attribute it to its proponents.
- **Reasoning:** The Two-Nation Theory is an ideological position, not an established fact. Writing "the two nations of India" without attribution endorses the theory. Writing "the Muslim League's Two-Nation Theory held that..." correctly attributes while maintaining neutrality.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1; Glossary; Myth vs Evidence entry
- **Reversibility:** Not applicable — this is a permanent application of the Neutrality Constitution.

### Decision #0010

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Quote marks on "Pakistan" in pre-1940 context
- **Decision:** Use "Pakistan" in quotation marks when referring to the concept before the Lahore Resolution (1940). Remove quotation marks for post-1940 references.
- **Reasoning:** Before 1940, the term "Pakistan" was a neologism coined by Choudhry Rahmat Ali in 1933 and was not yet the official demand of the Muslim League. Scare quotes signal to readers that the term was not yet institutionalised. After the Lahore Resolution, the term became the formal political demand and no longer requires this signalling.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1 paragraphs on pre-1940 context
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if editorial judgment on the transitional date changes.

### Decision #0015

- **Date:** 2026-07-14
- **Topic:** Language — "Nehruvian" vs "Nehru's"
- **Decision:** Use "Nehru's" for actions directly taken by Nehru. Use "Nehruvian" for broader intellectual traditions, policies, and schools of thought that extend beyond Nehru's personal tenure.
- **Reasoning:** "Nehruvian" implies a broader tradition (e.g., "Nehruvian consensus," "Nehruvian socialism") that includes followers and successors. "Nehru's" is specific to the man. Precision is important for historiographical accuracy.
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1; Volume title "The Nehruvian Era"
- **Reversibility:** Not applicable.

### Decision #0016

- **Date:** 2026-07-15
- **Topic:** Citation requirement for apparatus sections
- **Decision:** The "Research Companion" (bibliography) and "Visual Asset Acquisition List" (production plan) sections are exempt from the per-paragraph citation rule in the Evidence Standard. Each entry in these sections is self-citing: a Research Companion entry names the work it describes, and a Visual Asset Acquisition List entry names the archive, collection, or map it proposes to acquire.
- **Reasoning:** The Evidence Standard requires every paragraph to answer "How do we know this?" by tracing a factual claim to a source. These two sections are not factual claims about history — they are a reading list and a production plan. Forcing citations onto them would be either redundant (the source is named in the sentence) or fraudulent (attaching a registry ID to a work not in the source registry). The Gold Standard Review confirmed the narrative, interpretive, historiographical, and strategic sections are fully cited; only the 47 apparatus paragraphs are empty by design.
- **Alternatives considered:** Adding registry citations to apparatus entries (rejected — would require registering 40+ bibliography items and archival collections not used as evidence; would dilute the registry's meaning); removing the sections (rejected — both are required Knowledge Completeness elements).
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1; Editorial Constitution Article IV; Gold Standard Review Phase 3 (Evidence Audit)
- **Reversibility:** Reversible if the Review Board later requires formal registry entries for every bibliography item.

The paragraph-based Visual Asset Acquisition List remains as the human-readable plan.
- **Alternatives considered:** Building map/chart/image renderers now (rejected — new feature, violates freeze); using only the prose plan (rejected — not render-ready, not countable by density audit).
- **Approved by:** Managing Editor
- **Related objects:** Chapter 1; registry.ts; KnowledgeRenderer.tsx; Gold Standard Review Phase 5; Book of Record #0005, #0003
- **Reversibility:** Renderers can be built after publication; assets swapped in once acquired.
