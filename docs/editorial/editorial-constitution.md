## Preamble — Why We Exist

The Breakdown exists to build the world's most trusted, continuously updated, evidence-first digital knowledge institution.

We are not a news website. We are not a blog. We are not an AI summary generator. We are not a Wikipedia mirror.

We are a **Knowledge Operating System** — an institution dedicated to transforming information into understanding through disciplined, transparent, evidence-based editorial practice.

### Why evidence matters

In an age of algorithmic noise, manufactured outrage, and collapsing trust in institutions, evidence is the only durable foundation for knowledge. A claim without evidence is opinion. A narrative without sources is fiction. An argument without counterargument is propaganda.

The Breakdown holds itself to a higher standard because the stakes demand it. The questions we address — war and peace, democracy and authoritarianism, prosperity and poverty, rights and justice — are too important to treat with anything less than intellectual rigour.

### What differentiates us

- **From news:** We do not chase the news cycle. We build permanent knowledge objects that are continuously updated, not ephemeral articles that are forgotten tomorrow.
- **From blogs:** We do not publish opinion without evidence. Every claim is sourced, verified, and maintained.
- **From AI summaries:** We do not generate content without accountability. Every word is reviewed by a human editor who can be challenged.
- **From Wikipedia:** We do not aspire to neutral summary of all knowledge. We build curated, evidence-first collections with editorial judgment, scholarly grounding, and transparent methodology.

### The Evidence Standard

Nothing enters The Breakdown because it is interesting. It enters because it is **evidence-backed, context-rich, transparently sourced, and capable of surviving informed criticism.**

Every paragraph must answer: **"How do we know this?"**

If a paragraph cannot answer this question, it does not belong in the knowledge object.

### The Cultural Principle

We are not in the business of being first. We are in the business of being trusted.

This single sentence shall influence every editorial decision:
- whether to publish today or next week
- whether to include an uncertain statistic
- whether to simplify a complex debate
- whether to wait for stronger evidence

Trust is the only metric that cannot be replaced. Speed, volume, and novelty are secondary.

### The Motto

> **Evidence before conclusions. Context before certainty.**

The Breakdown has a motto. It is not marketing — it is identity. It appears in Mission Control, in editorial documents, and on the methodology page.

Alternatives considered and recorded in the Book of Record:
- "Knowledge deserves evidence. Evidence deserves transparency."
- "Understand deeply. Verify relentlessly."

The adopted motto was chosen because it directly encodes the two disciplines the institution must protect: evidence as the precondition for judgment, and context as the guard against overconfidence.

### Our mission

To build the world's most trusted evidence-first knowledge institution — one chapter, one claim, one source at a time.

## Article I — Core Principles

These principles shall never be amended. They define the character of the institution.

**Truth over speed.**  
We would rather be late and right than first and wrong. Breaking news is not our business. Verified knowledge is.

**Evidence over opinion.**  
Every claim must be supported by evidence drawn from the hierarchy defined in Article III. Editorial analysis and interpretation shall be clearly labelled as such.

**Context over virality.**  
We do not optimise for clicks, shares, or engagement. We optimise for understanding, retention, and verifiability.

**Transparency over certainty.**  
We shall be honest about what we know, what we do not know, and where scholars disagree. Admitting uncertainty builds trust. Pretending to certainty undermines it.

**Revision over ego.**  
Corrections are not admissions of failure. They are evidence of a functioning editorial system. Anyone may challenge a claim. When the evidence changes, the knowledge object changes.

**Multiple perspectives over single narratives.**  
Every significant historical or political question has multiple legitimate interpretations. We shall present them fairly — with the strongest arguments for each and the strongest criticisms.

**Knowledge is never finished.**  
A published chapter is not a final product. It is the current best version. It will be reviewed, updated, and improved on a regular cycle. Stagnation is the enemy of trust.

## Article II — Editorial Ethics

### Conflicts of Interest

1. Every editor, writer, and contributor shall disclose any personal, financial, professional, or political relationship that could reasonably be perceived to influence their work on a given topic.
2. Contributors shall recuse themselves from topics where they have a direct stake in the outcome.
3. Disclosure shall be published alongside the relevant knowledge object.

### Corrections

1. Corrections shall be made promptly and transparently.
2. Every correction shall be logged in the knowledge object's change log with the date, the nature of the error, and the corrective action taken.
3. Significant factual errors shall trigger a review of all related knowledge objects for similar errors.
4. Readers may submit correction requests through a public channel.

### Anonymous Sources

1. Anonymous sources shall not be used as primary evidence for factual claims.
2. In exceptional circumstances where anonymity is necessary to protect a source from harm, the editor-in-chief must approve the use in writing, and the justification shall be recorded in the editorial notes.

### Sponsored Content

1. The Breakdown shall not publish sponsored content, paid placements, or any material where financial consideration influences editorial content.
2. Funding sources shall be disclosed on the platform's About page and on individual knowledge objects where relevant.
3. No funder shall have editorial control over any knowledge object.

### AI Usage

1. AI tools may assist research, drafting, transcription, translation, and data analysis — but shall never be the final author of any knowledge object.
2. Every AI-assisted output must be reviewed, verified, and edited by a human subject-matter expert before publication.
3. AI usage in the creation of any knowledge object shall be disclosed.
4. AI-generated content must never present synthetic evidence, fabricated sources, or hallucinated claims.

### Plagiarism

1. Plagiarism in any form is grounds for immediate removal from the editorial team.
2. All sources must be cited. Verbatim quotations must be attributed. Close paraphrasing without attribution is plagiarism.
3. All knowledge objects shall be checked for plagiarism before publication.

### Image Manipulation

1. Images shall not be manipulated in any way that alters their factual content.
2. Standard adjustments for exposure, colour balance, and cropping are permitted.
3. Any compositing, retouching, or AI enhancement shall be disclosed in the caption.

### Attribution

1. Every knowledge object shall credit its primary author(s), editor(s), fact-checker(s), and reviewer(s).
2. Sources used in the creation of the object shall be listed in a canonical source registry.

## Article III — Evidence Constitution

### Evidence Hierarchy

All evidence used in The Breakdown shall be classified according to the following hierarchy. Higher levels carry greater weight. Claims supported only by lower-level evidence shall be clearly labelled as such.

**Level 1 — Primary Documents**
- Laws, statutes, and treaties
- Court judgments and tribunal rulings
- Government notifications, orders, and gazettes
- Official datasets from statistical agencies
- Original diplomatic correspondence and memoranda
- Contemporary photographs, maps, and audiovisual recordings
- Original manuscripts and archival documents

**Level 2 — Academic Scholarship**
- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- Monographs from academic presses
- Doctoral dissertations (approved)
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Scholarly editions of primary sources

**Level 3 — Institutional Reports**
- Reports from multilateral organisations (UN, World Bank, IMF, WHO)
- Reports from national institutions (RBI, CAG, NITI Aayog, US GAO)
- Reports from independent research organisations (CSIS, RAND, Brookings, CPR, ORF)
- Commissions of inquiry and official histories

**Level 4 — High-Quality Journalism**
- Investigative reporting from outlets with documented editorial standards (Reuters, Associated Press, BBC, The Hindu, Indian Express, The Economist, New York Times, Washington Post, ProPublica, The Guardian)
- Must be verifiable and attributed to named sources
- Does not include opinion columns, editorials, or punditry

**Level 5 — Books**
- Non-fiction works from established publishers
- Memoirs and autobiographies (with appropriate caveats about reliability)
- Must be treated with lower confidence than peer-reviewed scholarship

**Level 6 — Interviews and Oral Testimony**
- Original interviews conducted by The Breakdown or by reputable institutions
- Oral history collections from academic archives
- Must account for memory limitations, retrospective bias, and self-interest

**Level 7 — Expert Opinion**
- Commentary and analysis from recognised subject-matter experts
- Think-tank policy papers
- Conference presentations
- Must be clearly labelled as opinion/interpretation, not fact

### The Evidence Spine

Every paragraph in a knowledge object shall be able to answer the question: **"How do we know this?"**

Every chapter must have an invisible backbone — a logical chain that connects raw evidence to the reader's understanding. If any link in the chain is missing, the paragraph is incomplete.

```
Research Question
        ↓
Evidence (from the Evidence Hierarchy)
        ↓
Claim (registered in the Claim Registry)
        ↓
Explanation (how the evidence supports the claim)
        ↓
Counterargument (the strongest evidence against)
        ↓
Editorial Judgment (if any, with justification)
        ↓
Reader Takeaway (what the reader should understand)
```

1. Every paragraph that makes a factual assertion shall trace — explicitly or by clear implication — to a link in this chain.
2. Paragraphs that present narrative chronology without interpretation are exempt, but the research question they serve shall be evident from the chapter's structure.
3. Paragraphs that present editorial judgment shall explicitly acknowledge the counterargument.

### Evidence Rules

1. Every claim shall reference the level(s) of evidence that support it.
2. Where evidence levels conflict, the claim shall note the discrepancy.
3. A claim supported only by Level 7 evidence shall not be presented as established fact.
4. Primary sources (Level 1) are preferred whenever available.
5. Secondary sources shall be checked against their primary sources where possible.
6. Evidence from adversaries of the subject shall not be excluded, but shall be labelled with its provenance.

## Article IV — Claims Constitution

Every claim published by The Breakdown shall satisfy the following requirements. No exceptions.

### Required Fields

Every claim must carry, in its machine-readable metadata:

1. **Claim ID** — A unique, stable, canonical identifier.
2. **Claim Text** — The precise wording of the factual assertion.
3. **Evidence** — One or more pieces of evidence from the Evidence Hierarchy.
4. **Source(s)** — Specific, citable references for each piece of evidence.
5. **Confidence Score** — One of: `established`, `strong`, `moderate`, `weak`, `contested`.
6. **Counterargument(s)** — The strongest argument against the claim, with supporting evidence.
7. **Last Verification Date** — When the claim was last verified against available evidence.
8. **Responsible Editor** — The named editor accountable for the claim's accuracy.
9. **Version History** — Every change to the claim, with date, editor, and reason.

### Confidence Scores

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Established | Supported by multiple independent Level 1–2 sources. No serious scholarly dispute. |
| Strong | Supported by strong evidence. Some scholarly disagreement exists but the weight of evidence supports the claim. |
| Moderate | Evidence exists but is limited, contradictory, or subject to reasonable alternative interpretation. |
| Weak | Limited evidence. Plausible but not firmly established. |
| Contested | Significant scholarly disagreement exists. The evidence does not clearly favour one position. |

### Claim Rules

1. Claims shall be as specific as the evidence permits.
2. Weasel words ("some say," "critics argue," "it is believed") shall be replaced with specific attributions ("According to historian Ayesha Jalal," "The International Court of Justice held in 1973 that...").
3. Every factual assertion in a knowledge object shall trace to a claim in the Claim Registry.
4. No claim may be changed without updating its version history and informing all knowledge objects that reference it.

### Open Questions

Every flagship chapter shall include a section titled **"Open Questions"** that explicitly states what the evidence does not resolve.

Examples:
- Could Partition have been avoided under a different constitutional arrangement?
- Did the accelerated transfer of power make large-scale violence more likely?
- How decisive was individual leadership compared with structural forces?
- To what extent did the Kashmir decision shape India's later strategic doctrine?

1. Open questions shall be genuine — questions that reasonable scholars disagree on.
2. Open questions shall not be rhetorical. They shall be answerable in principle with sufficient evidence.
3. The chapter's interpretive stance on each open question shall be acknowledged, not hidden.

### Evidence Challenges

Every flagship chapter shall include a section titled **"Evidence Challenges"** that presents the strongest argument against the chapter's dominant interpretive position.

1. The challenge shall be presented in its strongest form — not a straw man.
2. The challenge shall be answered with the best evidence the chapter's editors can offer.
3. If the editors cannot answer the challenge, the chapter's confidence score shall be downgraded accordingly.
4. This mechanism is designed to prevent confirmation bias by forcing the editorial team to confront the strongest available criticism.

## Article V — Neutrality Constitution

### Categories of Content

Readers shall never have to guess which category a given passage falls into. Every knowledge object shall clearly differentiate:

| Category | Definition | Labelling |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| Established Fact | Supported by multiple independent Level 1–2 sources. No reasonable scholarly dispute. | Plain text. |
| Interpretation | A reasoned conclusion drawn from evidence, where alternative interpretations exist. | Must cite the scholar(s) associated with the interpretation. |
| Hypothesis | A proposition supported by some evidence but not yet established. | Must be labelled "Hypothesis:" or "One hypothesis is...". |
| Prediction | A forward-looking statement about likely outcomes. | Must be labelled "Prediction:" with confidence level. |
| Counterfactual | An examination of what might have happened if conditions had differed. | Must be labelled "Counterfactual:" and clearly distinguished from historical narrative. |
| Editorial Judgment | The Breakdown's own analytical conclusion after weighing evidence. | Must be labelled "Editorial Assessment:" with justification. |

### The Four-Layer Structure

Every flagship chapter shall distinguish four layers, structurally and visually. Readers shall never confuse these layers.

**Layer 1 — What Happened**
Pure chronology. Narrative sequence of events. No interpretation. This is the spine of events that all subsequent layers refer to. It answers: "What actually occurred, in what order?"

**Layer 2 — What the Evidence Shows**
Primary documents, official records, academic consensus. This layer presents the evidentiary foundation for the chronology. It answers: "How do we know this sequence occurred?"

**Layer 3 — Where Historians Disagree**
Different schools of thought, different evidence selections, different interpretations. This layer presents scholarly debate in good faith — the strongest arguments for each position and the strongest criticisms. It answers: "Where is the scholarly debate, and what are the stakes?"

**Layer 4 — Why It Matters**
Editorial synthesis, strategic lessons, modern implications. This is where The Breakdown may offer its own analytical conclusions, but only with transparent justification. It answers: "Why should a reader today care about this?"

### Neutrality Rules

1. The Breakdown does not take political positions. It takes evidence-based positions.
2. All sides of a scholarly debate shall be presented with their strongest arguments and strongest criticisms.
3. False equivalence is prohibited. If one side of a debate is overwhelmingly supported by evidence and the other is fringe, the evidence imbalance shall be noted — not hidden in the name of "balance."
4. The editorial team shall review for the following biases at every stage:
   - **Nationalist bias** — Favouring one nation's narrative without evidence
   - **Imperial bias** — Adopting the perspective of the coloniser
   - **Presentism** — Judging historical actors by contemporary standards
   - **Hindsight bias** — Treating known outcomes as inevitable
   - **Selection bias** — Choosing evidence that supports a preferred conclusion
   - **Confirmation bias** — Seeking evidence that confirms existing beliefs
5. Bias audit results shall be published with each flagship chapter.

## Article VI — Historiography Constitution

Every historical chapter in The Breakdown shall:

1. **Present multiple schools of thought.** No single interpretation shall be presented as the sole correct account unless the evidence is genuinely uncontested.
2. **State the strongest arguments for each school.** The presentation shall be charitable, not a straw man.
3. **State the strongest criticisms of each school.** Fair criticism, not motivated critique.
4. **State the editorial conclusion, if any.** If the editors judge that the evidence favours one interpretation, the reasoning shall be transparent and the competing views acknowledged.
5. **Identify key historians by name.** Readers should be able to trace interpretations to specific scholars.
6. **Distinguish between historiography and history.** The object shall make clear when it is presenting what happened versus how scholars have debated what happened.

## Article VII — Language Constitution

### Prohibited Words

The following words and phrases create false certainty or diminish the reader's ability to evaluate the evidence. They shall not appear in knowledge objects:

- "Clearly"
- "Obviously"
- "Undoubtedly"
- "Of course"
- "Everyone knows"
- "It goes without saying"
- "Naturally"
- "Plainly"
- "Surely"

### Preferred Constructions

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| "Clearly, Partition was..." | "According to historian Yasmin Khan, Partition was..." |
| "Obviously, Nehru believed..." | "Nehru's speeches and correspondence indicate that he believed..." |
| "Undoubtedly, the British..." | "The evidence strongly suggests that the British..." |
| "Historians agree that..." | "The dominant scholarly view, shared by X, Y, and Z, holds that..." |
| "It is widely accepted..." | "A majority of scholars in the field accept that... However, X disputes this on the grounds that..." |

### General Language Rules

1. Every sentence that makes a factual assertion shall include or imply a source.
2. Absolute statements ("never," "always," "everyone," "no one") shall be used only when the evidence genuinely supports no exception.
3. Emotionally loaded language shall be avoided in narrative passages. Direct quotations from historical actors may convey emotion; the editorial voice shall not.
4. The passive voice is permitted where the actor is unknown or irrelevant, but the active voice is preferred where the actor matters.

## Article VIII — Visual Constitution

### Every Visual Must Include

1. **Source** — Creator, date, location, and provenance.
2. **License** — Copyright status and terms of use.
3. **Date** — When the visual was created (not when it was digitised or acquired).
4. **Caption** — An editorial caption explaining what the visual depicts and its historical significance. Not merely descriptive: analytical.
5. **Purpose** — A brief note explaining why this visual is included and what it teaches. If the visual does not teach something, it shall not be included.
6. **Related Claims** — Links to the specific claims in the Claim Registry that the visual supports.
7. **Alt Text** — Complete, descriptive alt text conveying all information present in the visual.
8. **Accessibility Review** — Confirmation that the visual meets WCAG AA colour contrast and text requirements.

### Visual Rules

1. Visuals are evidence, not decoration. No visual shall be included solely to break up text or improve page aesthetics.
2. Every visual shall answer: "Does this help the reader understand the subject?"
3. Historical photographs shall be verified for authenticity and date before publication.
4. Charts and data visualisations shall include source data, methodology, and margin of error.
5. Maps shall include scale, projection, and a clear legend.
6. AI-generated images are prohibited in historical reporting. They may be used for illustrative diagrams where no authentic visual exists, but shall be clearly labelled as AI-generated.

## Article IX — Knowledge Object Constitution

### Definition

Everything in The Breakdown is a Knowledge Object. Stories, investigations, claims, evidence entries, sources, maps, documents, thinker profiles, relationship definitions, datasets, timelines, videos, and learning modules — all are Knowledge Objects.

### Requirements

Every Knowledge Object must:

1. **Have a canonical ID.** A unique, stable, machine-readable identifier that does not change if the object's title or content changes.
2. **Be reusable.** No knowledge object shall be created inside a page. It shall be created in its registry and rendered wherever needed.
3. **Be versioned.** Every change to the object shall produce a new version with a changelog entry.
4. **Be independently citable.** Each object shall have a stable URL and a recommended citation format.
5. **Belong to a registry.** Every object type has a corresponding registry (Claim Registry, Source Registry, Document Registry, etc.) that maintains the canonical version.
6. **Have a responsible editor.** A named human who can be contacted about the object.

### Prohibitions

1. Duplicate knowledge objects are prohibited. If information exists in one registry, it shall not be recreated elsewhere.
2. Pages shall not contain knowledge that exists only in that page. If a piece of information belongs in a registry, it shall be moved there.

## Article X — Editorial Workflow

### Standard Pipeline

All knowledge objects shall pass through the following stages. No shortcuts.

| Stage | Description | Gate |
|-------|-------------|------|
| 1. Research | Gather sources, identify claims, assess evidence quality. | Research Brief approved. |
| 2. Source Verification | Verify all Level 1–3 sources against originals. Flag any discrepancies. | Verification Report complete. |
| 3. Evidence Extraction | Extract claims from sources. Register each claim in the Claim Registry with confidence score. | Claims registered. |
| 4. Knowledge Modelling | Structure claims, sources, and evidence into knowledge objects in the appropriate registries. | Objects created. |
| 5. Editorial Writing | Draft the knowledge object following the Language Constitution. Link every claim to its registry entry. | Draft complete. |
| 6. Fact Checking | Every claim checked against its cited sources. Every source checked for accuracy of representation. | Fact Check Report complete. |
| 7. Technical Review | Accessibility, performance, metadata, structured data, and rendering verified. | Technical Review passed. |
| 8. Editor-in-Chief Review | Final editorial judgment. Constitutional compliance verified. | Approved by Editor-in-Chief. |
| 9. Publication | Knowledge object published with full transparency metadata. | Published. |
| 10. Refresh Cycle | Scheduled review for updates, new evidence, and corrections. | Refresh scheduled. |

### Review Board Roles

Every flagship chapter shall have an assigned Review Board with the following roles. Each role carries explicit responsibility for a specific dimension of quality.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Lead Research Editor | Sources, evidence extraction, and research methodology |
| Historical Accuracy Reviewer | Factual correctness and chronological accuracy |
| Foreign Policy Reviewer | Strategic analysis and geopolitical framing |
| Evidence Reviewer | Claim verification against cited sources |
| Language Editor | Neutrality, clarity, and compliance with Language Constitution |
| Visual Editor | Maps, archival material, charts, and visual provenance |
| Managing Editor | Final approval and constitutional compliance |

1. A single person may hold multiple roles only with Editor-in-Chief approval.
2. The Lead Research Editor and Evidence Reviewer shall never be the same person.
3. Review Board assignments shall be published with the chapter's transparency metadata.

### Working States

Every knowledge object shall have one of the following states at all times:

- **Draft** — Research incomplete. Claims not fully sourced.
- **In Review** — Structurally complete. Under fact-check or technical review.
- **Published** — Passed all gates. Available to readers.
- **Updated** — Published version has been revised. Previous version archived.
- **Retired** — Knowledge object withdrawn. Reason shall be published.

## Article XI — Gold Standard Review

Every flagship chapter shall pass the Gold Standard Review before being designated as a template for future work.

### Seven Phases

| Phase | Description | Required Participants |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1. Expert Review | Subject-matter experts review for accuracy, fairness, and completeness. Minimum 2 experts from different disciplinary or interpretive perspectives. | External experts |
| 2. Reader Review | Four different reader profiles (e.g., UPSC aspirant, MA History student, journalist, curious layperson) read and report confusion points. | Lay readers |
| 3. Evidence Audit | Every claim independently verified against its cited sources. Counter-evidence checked. | Fact-checker (not the writer) |
| 4. Bias Audit | Systematic review for nationalist, imperial, presentist, hindsight, selection, and confirmation bias. | Editor (not the writer) |
| 5. Visual Audit | Every visual reviewed for provenance, legality, pedagogical purpose, and accessibility. | Visual editor |
| 6. Knowledge Density Audit | Automated and manual check against Knowledge Density targets (claims, evidence, sources, documents, images, maps, thinkers, historiographical views, learning objectives). | Automation + editor |
| 7. Defensibility Audit | "Could we defend this?" test. For every major interpretive claim, can we produce: the claim, the evidence, the primary source, the scholarly disagreement, and our reasoning? | Editor-in-Chief |

### Pass Condition

A chapter passes Gold Standard Review only when:

- All seven phases are complete.
- All identified issues are resolved or explicitly noted as open.
- The review outcomes are published alongside the chapter.

## Article XII — Knowledge Lifecycle

### Freshness States

Every knowledge object shall carry a freshness state:

- **Current** — Last verified within the review cycle.
- **Needs Review** — Review cycle due or exceeded.
- **Under Review** — Review in progress.
- **Updated** — New version available. Previous version archived.
- **Outdated** — Superseded by new evidence but retained for historical reference.
- **Retired** — Withdrawn. Reason published.

### Review Schedules

| Object Type | Review Cycle |
|-------------|-------------|
| Flagship chapter | 6 months |
| Standard chapter | 12 months |
| Story / Investigation | 12 months |
| Claim | Continuous (updated when new evidence appears) |
| Source | 12 months |
| Statistical data | Annually, or when new data is released |

### Retirement Rules

A knowledge object may be retired when:

1. The evidence has been decisively superseded.
2. The question it addresses has been resolved.
3. It contains errors that cannot be corrected without rewriting the object from scratch.

Retired objects shall remain accessible with a clear notice explaining why they were retired.

### Versioning

1. Every knowledge object shall have a semantic version number (MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH).
   - MAJOR: Substantive rewriting or reinterpretation.
   - MINOR: Addition of new evidence or correction of factual errors.
   - PATCH: Typographical corrections, formatting, metadata updates.

2. **Pre-publication versioning.** During development, chapters shall use pre-release version labels to track editorial progress:

   | Version | Meaning |
   |---------|---------|
   | v0.1 – v0.6 | Research and drafting in progress |
   | v0.7 | Research complete. All sources identified and verified. |
   | v0.8 | Evidence verified. All claims registered and linked. |
   | v0.9 | Gold Standard Review in progress or completed. |
   | v1.0 | Published. Passed all gates and review phases. |
   | v1.1 | Corrections applied. |
   | v1.2 | New archival evidence added. |
   | v2.0 | Major historiographical revision. Substantive reinterpretation of the subject. |

3. Previous versions shall be accessible.
4. A changelog shall be maintained for every object.

### Corrections

A correction shall be issued when:

1. A factual error is identified.
2. A source was misrepresented.
3. Evidence was omitted that materially changes the conclusion.

Corrections shall:

1. Be published within the knowledge object itself.
2. Include the original text, the corrected text, and the date of correction.
3. Be logged in the changelog.
4. Trigger a review of related objects for similar errors.

## Article XIII — Transparency

### State of the Evidence

Every flagship chapter shall include a **"State of the Evidence"** block at the beginning, before the narrative. This immediately communicates to readers how mature the knowledge is.

Example format:

```
Evidence Strength: ★★★★★

Primary Documents:        18
Academic Books:           42
Peer-reviewed Papers:     27
Government Records:       13
Historiographical Schools: 6
Known Open Questions:      4
Last Verified:            14 July 2026
```

1. The evidence strength rating (1–5 stars) reflects the proportion of claims supported by Level 1–3 evidence.
2. The State of the Evidence shall be computed automatically from the chapter's metadata.
3. A chapter with fewer than 2 stars shall not be published.

### Required Transparency Metadata

Every published knowledge object shall expose:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Last Updated | Date of most recent change |
| Last Verified | Date of most recent evidence verification |
| Evidence Score | Percentage of claims supported by Level 1–3 evidence |
| Confidence | Overall confidence summary |
| Sources | Count and list of sources |
| Open Questions | Questions the evidence does not yet resolve |
| Known Limitations | Constraints of the current research |
| Version | Current version number |
| Change Log | Link to complete change history |
| Reviewers | Named editor(s) and fact-checker(s) |

### Transparency Rules

1. No knowledge object shall be published without transparency metadata.
2. Automated checks shall flag objects where transparency metadata is missing or stale.
3. Admissions of uncertainty are not weaknesses. They are essential to trust.

## Article XIV — Quality Gates

### The Twelve Gates

No chapter may be marked Published unless all twelve gates pass:

| Gate | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Historical Context | Sufficient background for a general reader to understand the subject |
| 2. Primary Sources | At least one Level 1 source referenced |
| 3. Multiple Perspectives | At least two distinct scholarly perspectives presented |
| 4. Timeline | Key events presented in chronological order |
| 5. Maps | Geographic context provided where relevant |
| 6. Claims Linked | Every factual assertion linked to a Claim Registry entry |
| 7. Evidence Verified | All evidence checked against original sources |
| 8. Counterarguments | Every major claim accompanied by strongest counterargument |
| 9. Thinkers | Key individuals profiled where relevant |
| 10. Learning Section | Key takeaways, study questions, or recommended reading provided |
| 11. Glossary | Key terms defined |
| 12. Further Reading | Curated path into the literature |

### Trust Score

The primary metric for every chapter — displayed at the top of Editorial Mission Control — shall be the **Trust Score**.

The Trust Score is a composite metric built from:

| Component | Weight | Source |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Evidence Completeness | 20% | Proportion of claims with Level 1–3 evidence |
| Source Diversity | 15% | Number of distinct sources and source types |
| Primary Source Ratio | 15% | Ratio of Level 1 to total citations |
| Review Completion | 15% | Gold Standard Review phases completed |
| Freshness | 10% | Time since last verification (relative to review cycle) |
| Correction History | 10% | Number and severity of corrections issued |
| Transparency | 10% | Completeness of transparency metadata |
| Reader Feedback | 5% | Confusion points resolved (Phase 2) |

**Rules:**

1. If Trust Score drops below the publication threshold, publication stops until the score recovers.
2. Trust Score shall be visible to readers as part of the State of the Evidence block.
3. Trust Score shall be computed automatically by Editorial Mission Control.
4. The publication threshold for flagship chapters is 80/100.

### Automation

The Editorial Mission Control shall:

1. Automatically compute gate status for every chapter.
2. Automatically compute Trust Score from available metadata.
3. Flag failing gates and low Trust Scores in the editor dashboard.
4. Block publication (in automated systems) when gates are not passed or Trust Score is below threshold.

## Article XV — Institutional Memory

### Editorial Decision Records

Every significant editorial decision shall be recorded. For example:

- Why a claim was reworded.
- Why one source was preferred over another.
- Why a controversial interpretation was included or excluded.
- Why a counterargument was judged insufficient.
- Why a particular visual was selected.
- Why a correction was issued.

### Record Format

Each record shall include:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Date | When the decision was made |
| Decision | What was decided |
| Rationale | Why this choice was made |
| Alternatives | Options considered and rejected |
| Author | The editor responsible |
| Related Objects | Knowledge objects affected |
| Reversibility | Can this decision be reversed, and how? |

### Purpose

1. Future editors should understand the reasoning, not just the outcome.
2. Institutional memory survives staff changes.
3. External reviewers can assess editorial judgment.
