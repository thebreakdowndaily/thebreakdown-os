# Visual Patterns

> **VXS v1.0** — Reusable visual spine templates per story type. Every chapter selects exactly one visual pattern. The pattern determines the recommended visual spine sequence, category mix, and transition logic. Patterns may be adapted for a specific story, but the spine must satisfy the pattern's core invariants.

---

## How to use this guide

1. After the story pattern is selected (see `docs/editorial/story-patterns.md`), choose the matching visual pattern.
2. The visual pattern determines the sequence of visual categories, not the specific assets.
3. A visual pattern may be mixed with elements of another pattern only when explicitly noted.
4. If no pattern fits, a new pattern may be proposed through the VXS governance process.

---

## Pattern Selection Matrix

| If the story pattern is... | Use visual pattern... |
|----------------------------|----------------------|
| Crisis | Conflict |
| Decision | Biography or Policy |
| Institution | Institution |
| Biography | Biography |
| Policy Evolution | Policy |
| Conflict | Conflict |
| Technology | Technology |
| Economics | Policy or Geography |
| Geography (no corresponding story pattern) | Geography |

## 1. Conflict

**Use when:** The story follows a sustained adversarial relationship with multiple phases — a war, a border dispute, a rivalry, a crisis.

**Core question:** Why did this conflict persist, what drove escalation, and what resolved it?

**Recommended spine (8–10 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | Where is the contested space? | Orientation | Map of the geographic stage |
| 2 | What did the key actors look like? | Context | Photographs of opposing leaders or forces |
| 3 | How did the conflict begin? | Timeline | Chronology of escalation — the trigger sequence |
| 4 | What were the opposing plans? | Explanation | Decision diagrams of each side's strategy |
| 5 | What happened? | Orientation | Map of the key battles, movements, or events |
| 6 | How many were affected? | Comparison | Casualties, forces committed, resources expended |
| 7 | What evidence exists of the turning point? | Evidence | Document, treaty, or agreement that changed the trajectory |
| 8 | Who was involved beyond the principals? | Network | Allies, mediators, affected third parties |
| 9 | What do historians disagree about? | Comparison | Alternative interpretations of the conflict's origin or outcome |
| 10 | Why does it matter today? | Explanation | Synthesis diagram of the current state and unresolved issues |

**Category mix:** Orientation (2), Context (1), Timeline (1), Explanation (2), Comparison (2), Evidence (1), Network (1) — minimum 6 categories.

**Invariant:** The sixth visual (Comparison — quantifying impact) is mandatory. A conflict story that does not measure the cost of the conflict is incomplete.

**Transitions:**
- Geography → Actors: "This is where it happened. These are the people who decided what would happen there."
- Timeline → Strategy: "The sequence of events was driven by deliberate choices. Here is what each side planned."
- Map → Evidence: "The event on the map was not accidental. The document below shows what was agreed."

**Examples:** The 1962 War, the Kashmir conflict, the India-Pakistan rivalry, the India-China border dispute.

## 2. Decision

**Use when:** The story centres on a specific choice by an individual or institution that shaped events.

**Core question:** Why was this choice made, what alternatives existed, and why was this one chosen?

**Recommended spine (6–8 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | What was the context? | Context | Photograph of the place and time of the decision |
| 2 | Who were the decision-makers? | Context | Photographs of the key individuals |
| 3 | What was the problem they faced? | Explanation | Causal diagram of the situation that demanded a decision |
| 4 | What were the alternatives? | Explanation | Decision tree showing options and branches |
| 5 | What did they decide? | Evidence | The document, speech, or record of the decision |
| 6 | What changed as a result? | Comparison | Before/after comparison of relevant data |
| 7 | How did it unfold over time? | Timeline | Post-decision chronology |
| 8 | What was the counterfactual? | Explanation | What the alternatives might have produced |

**Category mix:** Context (2), Explanation (3), Evidence (1), Comparison (1), Timeline (1) — minimum 4 categories.

**Invariant:** The fourth visual (decision tree) is mandatory. A decision story must show that alternatives existed and were considered. Without the decision tree, the reader cannot evaluate the choice.

**Transitions:**
- Context → Context: starts broad, narrows to specific people.
- Context → Explanation: "This is who they were. This is what they saw."
- Explanation → Evidence: "The diagram shows what they considered. The document shows what they chose."
- Evidence → Comparison: "The decision produced measurable effects."

**Examples:** Nehru's choice of non-alignment, the decision to go to war in 1962, the choice of economic liberalisation in 1991, the Radcliffe Line award.

## 3. Institution

**Use when:** The subject is an organisation, its evolution, and its impact.

**Core question:** How did this institution form, how did it change, and what effect did it have?

**Recommended spine (7–9 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | Where did it operate? | Orientation | Map of the institution's geographic scope |
| 2 | What was the founding moment? | Context | Archival photograph from the founding era |
| 3 | How was it structured? | Explanation | Organizational chart or diagram of the founding architecture |
| 4 | What was the founding vision? | Evidence | Founding document, charter, or inaugural address |
| 5 | How did it evolve? | Timeline | Chronology of key milestones, reforms, and crises |
| 6 | What did it achieve? | Comparison | Quantitative measures of impact over time |
| 7 | Who shaped it? | Network | Relationship diagram of key leaders and their connections |
| 8 | What challenges does it face today? | Explanation | Diagram of current pressures and debates |

**Category mix:** Orientation (1), Context (1), Explanation (2), Evidence (1), Timeline (1), Comparison (1), Network (1) — minimum 7 categories.

**Invariant:** The timeline (evolution) and the comparison (impact) must appear as adjacent visuals. The reader must see what the institution did before they read about who shaped it. Chronology before influence.

**Transitions:**
- Orientation → Context: "This is where the institution operated. This is the world it was born into."
- Context → Explanation: "The founding moment was shaped by a specific structure."
- Timeline → Comparison: "The institution's evolution produced measurable outcomes."

**Examples:** The United Nations, the Indian Constitution, the Non-Aligned Movement, the Planning Commission.

## 4. Biography

**Use when:** A person's life illuminates a historical period or question.

**Core question:** How did this person's choices, ideas, and circumstances shape events?

**Recommended spine (6–8 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | What world were they born into? | Context | Photograph of their birthplace or early milieu |
| 2 | What shaped their thinking? | Context | Photograph of the people, places, or events that influenced them |
| 3 | What did they believe? | Evidence | A document — their writing, speech, or correspondence |
| 4 | What decisions defined them? | Timeline | Chronology of the critical moments in their life |
| 5 | What did they achieve — or fail to achieve? | Comparison | Quantitative or qualitative comparison of before/after their influence |
| 6 | Who connected to whom? | Network | Relationship diagram — allies, adversaries, influences |
| 7 | How is their legacy contested? | Explanation | Diagram of competing interpretations of their life and impact |

**Category mix:** Context (2), Evidence (1), Timeline (1), Comparison (1), Network (1), Explanation (1) — minimum 6 categories.

**Invariant:** The Evidence visual (their writing, speech, or correspondence) must precede the Timeline visual. The reader must understand what the person believed before they can evaluate the decisions they made.

**Transitions:**
- Context → Context: "This is the world that shaped them. Then this is what shaped their thinking."
- Context → Evidence: "These experiences produced a worldview. The document below shows it directly."
- Evidence → Timeline: "This is what they believed. These are the decisions that followed from that belief."

**Examples:** Nehru, Patel, Jinnah, Mountbatten, Ambedkar.

## 5. Policy

**Use when:** A policy, doctrine, or legal framework developed over time through multiple decisions.

**Core question:** How did this policy emerge, change, and produce its current form?

**Recommended spine (7–9 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | What was the problem? | Explanation | Causal diagram of the situation that demanded a policy response |
| 2 | What was the pre-policy state? | Comparison | Key data points showing the problem before intervention |
| 3 | What was proposed? | Evidence | The original policy document, legislation, or doctrine |
| 4 | How was it designed? | Explanation | Diagram of the policy architecture — how it was intended to work |
| 5 | How did it change over time? | Timeline | Chronology of amendments, reforms, and critical moments |
| 6 | What did it actually produce? | Comparison | Data comparison of intended vs actual outcomes |
| 7 | Who shaped it? | Network | Relationship diagram of key advocates, opponents, and influence paths |
| 8 | What is debated about it today? | Explanation | Diagram of current debates, unresolved tensions, and reform proposals |

**Category mix:** Explanation (3), Comparison (2), Evidence (1), Timeline (1), Network (1) — minimum 5 categories.

**Invariant:** The Evidence visual must be the third spine position. Policy stories begin with a problem, establish the pre-policy baseline, and then present the policy document itself. If the document appears later than position 4, the policy has been discussed without being shown.

**Transitions:**
- Explanation → Comparison: "This was the problem. These data show its scale."
- Comparison → Evidence: "The data established the need. The document shows the response."
- Evidence → Explanation: "The document states the intent. The diagram shows how it was supposed to work."
- Timeline → Comparison: "Intents changed over time. Outcomes tell a different story."

**Examples:** India's nuclear doctrine, the evolution of the Indus Waters Treaty, the creation of the Planning Commission, the 1991 economic reforms.

## 6. Technology

**Use when:** A technology, scientific development, or technical system changed outcomes.

**Core question:** How did this technology develop, what problem did it solve, and what unintended consequences did it produce?

**Recommended spine (7–8 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | What was the pre-technology state? | Context | Photograph of the world before the technology |
| 2 | What problem did it solve? | Explanation | Causal diagram of the limitations the technology addressed |
| 3 | How did it work? | Explanation | Diagram of the technology's mechanism or system architecture |
| 4 | How did it develop? | Timeline | Chronology of invention, refinement, and adoption |
| 5 | What changed as a result? | Comparison | Before/after data — what the technology shifted |
| 6 | How was it implemented? | Orientation | Map of where it was adopted, deployed, or built |
| 7 | What were the unintended consequences? | Explanation | Causal diagram of second-order effects — positive and negative |

**Category mix:** Context (1), Explanation (3), Timeline (1), Comparison (1), Orientation (1) — minimum 5 categories.

**Invariant:** The comparison (before/after) and the unintended-consequences diagram must appear as adjacent visuals. The reader must see what changed before they can understand what else changed as a result.

**Transitions:**
- Context → Explanation: "This is what existed before. The diagram below shows what replaced it."
- Timeline → Comparison: "The development timeline shows the sequence. The data shows the impact."
- Comparison → Explanation: "The intended effects are visible in the data. The unintended effects require a different diagram."

**Examples:** The Green Revolution, nuclear technology, the space programme, the Aadhaar system.

## 7. Geography

**Use when:** The story is fundamentally spatial — a border, a migration, a river dispute, a region whose geography determines its history.

**Core question:** How did geography shape the outcomes described in this story?

**Recommended spine (8–9 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | Where are we? | Orientation | Regional reference map with inset showing location within India/Asia |
| 2 | What did the landscape look like? | Context | Photograph of the terrain — mountain pass, river valley, coastline |
| 3 | Who lived there? | Orientation | Thematic map of demographic distribution |
| 4 | What boundary was drawn? | Orientation | Map of the contested or defining boundary |
| 5 | How did the boundary change things? | Explanation | Diagram of how geography was re-organised by politics |
| 6 | How many people moved or were affected? | Comparison | Quantitative data on displacement, population change, or economic impact |
| 7 | What evidence exists of the geographic reality? | Evidence | Treaty, map, survey record, or settlement document |
| 8 | How does the geography look today? | Orientation | Modern map showing the current situation |
| 9 | What remains unresolved? | Explanation | Diagram of continuing geographic disputes or open questions |

**Category mix:** Orientation (4), Context (1), Explanation (2), Comparison (1), Evidence (1) — minimum 5 categories.

**Invariant:** The first visual must be an Orientation map. Every geography story must orient the reader before anything else. No geography story opens with a photograph.

**Transitions:**
- Orientation → Context: "The map shows the region. The photograph shows what it looks like on the ground."
- Context → Orientation: "This is the landscape. These are the people who lived there."
- Orientation → Explanation: "The boundary was drawn here. This diagram shows how it re-ordered everything."
- Evidence → Orientation: "The original document shows what was agreed. The map shows what that agreement produced."

**Examples:** The Radcliffe Line, the Line of Actual Control, the Indus Waters Treaty, the Siachen Glacier dispute, the Kashmir geography.

## 8. Investigation

**Use when:** The story uncovers a hidden pattern, systemic failure, or contested truth through multiple pieces of evidence.

**Core question:** What is the truth, and how do we know it?

**Recommended spine (9–12 visuals):**

| # | Question | Category | Notes |
|---|----------|----------|-------|
| 1 | What is the puzzle? | Explanation | Diagram of the question or anomaly the investigation addresses |
| 2 | Where did it happen? | Orientation | Map of the geographic scope |
| 3 | Who is involved? | Network | Relationship diagram of actors, institutions, and interests |
| 4 | What does the primary evidence say? | Evidence | Key document, data set, or source |
| 5 | What pattern does the data reveal? | Comparison | Chart or visualisation showing the hidden pattern |
| 6 | How did it unfold? | Timeline | Chronology of events the investigation uncovered |
| 7 | What was the human reality? | Context | Photograph showing the human dimension |
| 8 | What do the stakeholders say? | Comparison | Contrasting claims, statements, or interpretations |
| 9 | What is the counterargument? | Explanation | Diagram of the alternative explanation or defence |
| 10 | What is the conclusion? | Explanation | Synthesis diagram — the evidence chain and the judgement |
| 11 | What should change? | Explanation | Recommendations or implications diagram |

**Category mix:** Explanation (4), Comparison (2), Evidence (1), Network (1), Timeline (1), Context (1), Orientation (1) — minimum 7 categories.

**Invariant:** The Evidence visual must appear no later than position 5. An investigation that reaches position 5 without presenting any primary evidence has been discussing evidence without showing it.

**Transitions:**
- Explanation → Orientation: "This is the pattern we are investigating. Here is where it happened."
- Network → Evidence: "These are the actors. This is what the documents show."
- Evidence → Comparison: "A single document cannot tell the full story. The data below reveals the wider pattern."
- Comparison → Context: "The data shows the scale. The photograph shows the human reality."
- Context → Explanation: "The human cost raises a question. The diagram below answers it."

**Examples:** An investigation into electoral bond donations, a study of agrarian distress across districts, an inquiry into defence procurement patterns.

---

## 9. Pattern Mixing Rules

- One primary visual pattern per chapter.
- Secondary pattern elements may be used in specific spine positions (e.g., a network visual within a Conflict pattern to show third-party involvement).
- The primary pattern must be declared in the story blueprint.
- The opening visual must follow the primary pattern's first-position invariant.

---

## 10. Visual Pattern Verification

Before a chapter enters Gold Standard Review Phase 5 (Visual Audit), the pattern assignment must pass:

- [ ] The visual pattern matches the story pattern declared in the blueprint
- [ ] The spine follows the pattern's recommended category sequence
- [ ] The pattern's invariants are satisfied
- [ ] Category diversity meets the pattern's minimum requirement
- [ ] Transition logic between spine positions follows the pattern guidance
- [ ] If elements of a secondary pattern are used, the deviation is documented with rationale
