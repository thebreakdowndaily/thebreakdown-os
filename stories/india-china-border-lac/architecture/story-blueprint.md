# Story Blueprint — India–China Border: The Cold Peace Along the LAC

## Central question

Why is the India-China border still unsettled after 75 years, and what keeps the dispute alive despite periodic progress?

## Pattern

**Conflict** — a sustained adversarial relationship with multiple phases.

## Central conflict

India claims an inherited colonial boundary (the McMahon Line) and considers it settled international law. China rejects the colonial boundary as a product of unequal treaties imposed on a weak China. Neither position is compatible with the other. Both sides consider the territory non-negotiable for domestic political reasons. The dispute persists because it serves strategic purposes for both sides — as a bargaining chip, a domestic legitimacy tool, and a justification for military modernisation.

## Narrative arc

### Phase 1 — The Colonial Inheritance (pre-1947)

**Opening scene:** The 1914 Simla Convention. British officials, Tibetan representatives, and a Chinese delegate in a hill station drawing a line through the Himalayas — a line that one side would later consider settled and the other side would never accept.

**Governing question:** How did a line drawn by colonial administrators become a 75-year conflict between two nuclear powers?

**Evidence moments:**
- The McMahon Line as a colonial creation (C02)
- China's rejection of the Simla Convention (C01)
- The different inheritance: India adopts the line, China rejects its legitimacy

### Phase 2 — The Friendship Era (1947–1959)

**Governing question:** Why did Nehru believe the border could be settled through friendship, and was this naive or strategic?

**Evidence moments:**
- Hindi-Chini bhai bhai — the early post-independence warmth
- 1954 Panchsheel Agreement — deferring the border question (C04)
- China's hidden hand: the Aksai Chin road built in secret (C03)
- India's discovery of the road and the first cracks in trust

### Phase 3 — The Collapse (1959–1962)

**Governing question:** Was the 1962 war inevitable after the Tibetan uprising?

**Decision point:** Nehru's decision to grant asylum to the Dalai Lama (1959) — an act of conscience that China treated as hostile.

**Evidence moments:**
- Tibetan uprising and its consequences (C05)
- Nehru-Zhou correspondence — positions revealed as irreconcilable (C05)
- The 1960 officials' talks — agreed facts, no resolution (C06)
- India's Forward Policy — a risky strategy of establishing posts in disputed territory (C08)

**Competing interpretation:** Maxwell (1970) argues India's forward policy provoked Chinese retaliation. The Indian official position holds that China planned the attack regardless, and the forward policy was a legitimate assertion of sovereignty.

### Phase 4 — The War (October–November 1962)

**Governing question:** Why did China attack, and why did India lose so decisively?

**Evidence moments:**
- The timing of the Chinese offensive — during the Cuban Missile Crisis (C07)
- Indian military unpreparedness (C08)
- Chinese limited aims — withdrawal to pre-war lines (C07)

**Decision point:** Why China withdrew — was it strategic restraint, logistical constraint, or both?

### Phase 5 — The Cold Peace (1963–2019)

**Governing question:** How did two hostile nuclear neighbours manage a dispute they could not resolve for 57 years without direct conflict?

**Evidence moments:**
- 1967 Nathu La and Cho La clashes — the first test of the post-war order
- 1987 Sumdorong Chu — the crisis that nearly became another war
- 1988 Rajiv Gandhi-Deng Xiaoping rapprochement
- The 1993 and 1996 CBMs — laying the framework for managed conflict (C09)
- The crisis pattern: 2013 Depsang, 2017 Doklam (C10)

### Phase 6 — The New Normal (2020–present)

**Governing question:** What changed at Galwan, and does the old model of managed conflict still work?

**Evidence moments:**
- Galwan Valley — fatal clashes with no guns fired (C11)
- Massive force deployment on both sides
- The decoupling of the border dispute from diplomatic relations
- Why Galwan was different — and why it might happen again

**Learning exit:** The reader will understand the India-China border as a conflict sustained by incompatible positions, divergent historical narratives, and strategic incentives on both sides — a dispute that is managed rather than resolved, and that remains structurally dangerous.

## Competing interpretations to surface

| Debate | Position A | Position B | Where in chapter |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------------|
| Legitimacy of McMahon Line | Valid international boundary (India) | Colonial imposition, therefore void (China) | Phase 1 |
| Did Nehru's China policy fail? | Yes — naive trust was exploited | No — it bought time and prevented early conflict | Phase 2, revisited in Phase 6 |
| Who started the 1962 war? | China planned and executed a premeditated attack (Indian position) | India's forward policy provoked Chinese retaliation (Maxwell) | Phase 4 |
| Why did China withdraw in 1962? | Strategic restraint — China achieved limited aims | Logistical necessity — supply lines were overextended | Phase 4 |
| Does the CBM framework work? | Yes — no full-scale war since 1962 proves its value | No — crises still occur and force levels continue to rise | Phase 5 |
| Is Galwan a structural change? | Yes — it broke the norm of no fatal clashes; things will not return to pre-2020 | No — it follows the same pattern of escalation and de-escalation | Phase 6 |

## Opening scene

> **July 1914, Simla.** British surveyors finalised the alignment of a boundary line that none of the men drawing it had ever visited. The McMahon Line, named after British diplomat Sir Henry McMahon, was marked on a map with the ink of colonial cartographers — a line that would, in time, become the subject of one of the world's most intractable territorial disputes. The Chinese delegate had already left the conference, having initialled but unsigned the convention. Tibet was not a sovereign power capable of making treaties, China would later argue. The line, China said, did not exist. The ink had barely dried before the dispute began.
