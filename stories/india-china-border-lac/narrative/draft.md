# Chapter 3 — India–China Border: The Cold Peace Along the LAC

## The Line That Would Not Settle

On a July morning in 1914, British surveyors in Simla finalised the alignment of a boundary line through a landscape none of them had ever visited. The McMahon Line — named after Sir Henry McMahon, the British foreign secretary who brokered the agreement — was drawn through the eastern Himalayas, following watersheds and ridgelines that existed only on maps. A Chinese delegate had already left Simla, having initialled but not signed the convention. The Chinese government would later argue that Tibet was not a sovereign power capable of making treaties, and that the line therefore had no legal standing. The British and their Indian successors considered the line a settled international boundary.

The dispute over a line drawn by colonial administrators in a hill station has now lasted 75 years, cost tens of thousands of lives, produced one full-scale war and half a dozen major crises, and remains one of the world's most dangerous flashpoints between nuclear-armed powers. Why has it not been resolved? And why, after decades of diplomacy and confidence-building, does it remain capable of producing violence on the scale of the Galwan Valley in 2020?

## The Colonial Inheritance

The India-China border was not a single territorial question. It was three separate sectors, each with its own history.

In the eastern sector (modern Arunachal Pradesh), the McMahon Line of 1914 had defined the boundary between British India and Tibet. India, after independence, adopted this line as its international border. China rejected it on two grounds: that Tibet lacked the capacity to enter into treaties, and that the Simla Convention was never ratified by the Chinese government.

In the middle sector (Sikkim, now Uttarakhand and Himachal Pradesh), the border was comparatively well-defined through historical usage and British-era agreements. This sector has seen the fewest disputes.

In the western sector (Ladakh, Aksai Chin), the boundary had never been formally delimited. British maps showed a vague line. The region's strategic value was minimal during the colonial period — it was high-altitude desert, sparsely populated, accessible only by difficult mountain passes. It would become the site of the most intractable dimension of the dispute.

Independent India inherited the British position: the McMahon Line was settled; Aksai Chin was Indian territory. China inherited a different position: all boundaries inherited from the "unequal treaties" of the colonial period were open for renegotiation.

The two positions were fundamentally incompatible. The gap between them was not a matter of a few kilometres. In the western sector, India claimed approximately 38,000 square kilometres that China controlled. In the eastern sector, China claimed approximately 90,000 square kilometres that India controlled. Between them, the two states claimed more than 125,000 square kilometres of each other's territory.

## The Friendship Era

For the first decade after Indian independence, the border dispute was a deferred question — a problem both sides chose not to raise. Nehru's policy was based on a bet: that India could build sufficient trust with the new Chinese communist state that the border would eventually be settled through friendly negotiation.

The bet was not unreasonable in 1949. India was among the first non-communist states to recognise the People's Republic of China. Nehru saw China as a fellow Asian power emerging from colonial domination, a natural partner in the project of post-colonial development. The slogan "Hindi-Chini bhai bhai" (Indians and Chinese are brothers) captured the mood.

The 1954 Sino-Indian Agreement on Tibet — known as the Panchsheel Agreement — formalised the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence: mutual respect for territorial integrity, non-aggression, non-interference, equality, and peaceful coexistence. The agreement explicitly deferred the border question. Both sides avoided mentioning it. This was Nehru's strategy: build the relationship first, solve the territorial questions second.

The agreement had a hidden cost for India. In exchange for Chinese commitment to the Five Principles, India effectively accepted Chinese sovereignty over Tibet — ending the Tibetan buffer that had separated British India from China. The Tibetan border, which had been a sensitive but manageable frontier, was now replaced by a direct India-China border across its entire length. The geopolitical foundation of the dispute had been laid.

While Nehru pursued friendship, China was acting. Between 1954 and 1957, Chinese engineers built a 1,200-kilometre highway through the Aksai Chin region — the Xinjiang-Tibet Highway — connecting Xinjiang to western Tibet. The road crossed territory India claimed. China did not inform India.

In 1958, Indian patrols discovered the road. The discovery was a shock. It demonstrated that China had established de facto control over a large territory India considered its own, that India's intelligence apparatus had failed to detect the construction, and that China had been acting on the border while India was acting on friendship.

## The Break

The Tibetan uprising of March 1959 was the turning point. Tibetan resistance to Chinese rule, which had simmered since the 1951 occupation, erupted in open rebellion. The rebellion was crushed. The Dalai Lama fled to India, arriving in April 1959.

Nehru granted him asylum. It was a decision consistent with India's democratic values and its historical connection to Tibet. It was also a decision that China considered a direct act of hostility. From China's perspective, India was now harbouring a "rebel" leader, interfering in China's internal affairs, and providing a base for Tibetan resistance.

The exchange of letters between Nehru and Zhou Enlai in late 1959 and 1960 is one of the most remarkable diplomatic correspondences of the twentieth century — two leaders, each convinced of the justice of their position, laying out their cases with increasing frustration. Nehru argued for the McMahon Line as a settled boundary. Zhou rejected it as a colonial imposition. Nehru cited international law and treaty obligations. Zhou cited the history of unequal treaties and the principle of territorial integrity.

Neither persuaded the other. The correspondence established that the two positions were irreconcilable — not because of a misunderstanding, but because they rested on fundamentally different premises about what constituted a legitimate border.

The first clash came in August 1959 at Longju, an Indian post in the eastern sector. Chinese forces surrounded and occupied the post. Two Indian policemen were killed. It was the first death on a border that would see many more.

In 1960, Zhou Enlai visited New Delhi for a last attempt at diplomacy. The two sides agreed to hold officials-level talks to establish the factual positions. The officials met, produced a detailed joint report published in 1961, and factually documented where each side stood. The report was a model of clarity. It also demonstrated that no compromise was possible. The factual positions were too far apart. Neither side could concede territory to the other without abandoning its legal or political position.

## The Road to War

In 1961, the Indian government adopted the "Forward Policy" — a strategy of establishing military posts in disputed territory to assert Indian sovereignty. The policy was designed to prevent China from consolidating control over additional territory, to strengthen India's negotiating position, and to signal that India would not accept the status quo.

The policy was risky. Indian posts were small, isolated, and vulnerable. They were supplied by air or by long overland routes. They were positioned in territory China considered its own. From China's perspective, the Forward Policy was a provocation — an attempt to unilaterally alter the status quo by establishing military positions in disputed territory.

Neville Maxwell, in his authoritative account *India's China War* (1970), argues that the Forward Policy was the proximate cause of the 1962 war — that China, having failed to dislodge Indian posts through diplomatic pressure, decided to do so by force. The Indian official position holds that China had always intended to attack, and the Forward Policy was a legitimate response to Chinese aggression.

The evidence supports both interpretations. China had certainly prepared for military action — it had built roads, stockpiled supplies, and positioned forces along the border well before the Forward Policy began. But the timing and intensity of the Chinese offensive in October 1962 — coming after two years of India's forward deployment — is consistent with a decision to retaliate against a policy China considered unacceptable.

## The War

On 20 October 1962, Chinese forces launched a coordinated offensive across both the eastern and western sectors. The timing was strategic: the Soviet Union and the United States were deep in the Cuban Missile Crisis, and neither superpower was in a position to intervene diplomatically.

India was unprepared. The Indian Army had been deployed along the border but was equipped, trained, and supplied for a different kind of warfare. Troops wore summer uniforms in Himalayan winter conditions. Ammunition was in short supply. Logistics were inadequate. Command and control was fragmented. The strategy of forward deployment had scattered Indian forces into small, isolated positions that could be overwhelmed one by one.

The Chinese offensive achieved its objectives within weeks. In the eastern sector, Chinese forces advanced south of the McMahon Line, capturing the key town of Tawang and reaching positions that threatened the Assam plains. In the western sector, they destroyed Indian positions and advanced through the Aksai Chin region.

Then, on 21 November 1962, China announced a unilateral ceasefire. Chinese forces withdrew to positions north of the McMahon Line in the east and to the pre-war line of control in the west. The withdrawal was complete by December. China had achieved its military objectives — demonstrating its military superiority, destroying the Forward Policy, and re-establishing the status quo on its terms — without the cost of occupying disputed territory.

Why did China withdraw? The evidence supports two explanations, not mutually exclusive. The first is strategic: China demonstrated its dominance without provoking a wider war or permanent superpower intervention. The second is logistical: Chinese supply lines were stretched to their limit; continuing the campaign would have been costly and risky. China's later statements emphasised the strategic explanation; independent analysts tend to weigh both.

## The Cold Peace

The 1962 war produced a settlement of sorts — not through negotiation, but through tacit acceptance. The Line of Actual Control, the position where both armies stopped, became the de facto border. It was not legally agreed. It was not marked on maps. It was simply where the fighting stopped.

For the next 57 years, India and China managed this unresolved border through a complex system of restraint, negotiation, and periodic crisis. The management was effective enough to prevent another full-scale war. It was not effective enough to prevent escalation.

The first test came in 1967, when clashes at Nathu La and Cho La (the Sikkim border) produced casualties on both sides. The clashes were contained and did not escalate. They established a pattern: crisis, confrontation, de-escalation, return to status quo.

The pattern repeated in 1987 at Sumdorong Chu in Arunachal Pradesh, where a Chinese incursion produced a military standoff that came close to war. The crisis was defused through diplomatic channels, but both sides significantly reinforced their border deployments.

The end of the Cold War created conditions for a genuine diplomatic rapprochement. Rajiv Gandhi's visit to China in 1988 — the first by an Indian prime minister since Nehru — established the Joint Working Group on the boundary question. The 1993 Agreement on Peace and Tranquillity Along the LAC and the 1996 Agreement on Confidence-Building Measures created a framework for managing the dispute. The framework worked: it reduced the frequency and intensity of border incidents, established communication channels, and created mechanisms for disengagement.

It did not resolve the dispute. The competing territorial claims remained exactly where they were. The agreements managed the conflict; they did not end it.

## The New Normal

After 2014, the border pattern changed. Chinese assertiveness increased, driven by Xi Jinping's more muscular foreign policy and China's growing military capabilities. Indian assertiveness also increased, driven by a government that was less willing to accept Chinese unilateral actions.

The 2013 Depsang incident in Ladakh — Chinese troops crossing the LAC and establishing a camp in Indian territory — was resolved after three weeks through diplomatic channels. The 2017 Doklam standoff — a 73-day confrontation at the India-China-Bhutan tri-junction — was resolved through diplomatic de-escalation but demonstrated that the dispute could escalate rapidly.

The Galwan Valley in June 2020 was different. For the first time since 1967, border clashes produced fatalities: 20 Indian soldiers and an unconfirmed number of Chinese soldiers died in hand-to-hand combat with improvised weapons. Neither side used firearms. The violence was the product of competing patrols meeting in contested terrain, each side asserting its rights through physical presence.

Galwan was different because it broke the norm against fatal clashes. Before 2020, the border was dangerous but not lethal. After Galwan, both sides deployed massive forces — an estimated 100,000 or more troops on each side — along the LAC. The military infrastructure on both sides expanded dramatically. The old model of managed conflict, which had worked for five decades, was no longer sufficient.

## Why the Border Still Divides

The India-China border remains unsettled for three structural reasons.

The first is territorial. The two claims are irreconcilable. India cannot accept Chinese control over what it considers Indian territory. China cannot accept the McMahon Line as a settled boundary. Neither side can politically afford the concessions that a settlement would require.

The second is strategic. The border dispute serves both sides' strategic purposes. For India, it is the central justification for the military modernisation, for the partnership with the United States (Quad), and for the broader narrative of a rising power facing Chinese pressure. For China, the border dispute gives it leverage over India, a justification for its own military build-up, and a way to test Indian resolve without risking wider conflict.

The third is historical. The two countries have fundamentally different narratives about the border: India's is legal and colonial (the McMahon Line as inherited treaty), China's is anti-colonial (the McMahon Line as unequal treaty). Neither narrative allows for the kind of face-saving compromise that would be necessary for a political settlement.

The border dispute will not be resolved soon. It may not be resolved at all. What the last 75 years demonstrate is that the dispute can be managed — and that management requires constant attention, clear communication, and a shared recognition that the cost of escalation exceeds any possible benefit. The task for both sides is not to settle the border. It is to prevent the unsettled border from producing a war neither side wants.
