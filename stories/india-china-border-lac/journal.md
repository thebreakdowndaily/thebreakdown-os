# Journal — India–China Border: The Cold Peace Along the LAC

## Observations

### What went well

1. **Conflict pattern** was the correct choice — the sustained adversarial relationship with phases structured the narrative naturally.
2. **Claims registry with status map** helped visualise how claims relate to each other (Tibet → Panchsheel → Aksai Chin → 1962).
3. **Competing interpretations table** in the blueprint kept the historiographical debates visible during drafting.
4. **Editorial review passed** first time — no blockers. The structure from Week 1 (Evidence Spine, Four Layers, Knowledge Completeness) is now operating smoothly.

### What could improve

1. **Chinese sources gap** — all secondary sources are in English. Chinese-language archival research would strengthen claims about Chinese decision-making. This is a known limitation but important to document.
2. **Oral histories** — unlike Week 1 (Partition), this story has few accessible oral histories. Soldier accounts exist but are mostly in Chinese. This reduces the human dimension of the narrative.
3. **Scope management** — the India-China border spans 75 years and three sectors. Keeping the narrative focused while covering all essential phases required careful editing. The draft is 3,400 words — Gold Standard length would need expansion.

### V2 observations

1. **Pattern refinement**: "Conflict" pattern needs clearer guidance on handling long-duration disputes (75 years) with multiple phases. Current pattern definition assumes a contained conflict arc.
2. **Source diversity**: Add Chinese-language source identification to the research brief template.
3. **Claim status map**: Useful visualisation — include in the standard knowledge model template.
4. **Weekly cadence**: Producing a 3,400-word chapter in one day is possible but tight. The Editorial OS templates (research, verification, claims, timeline, blueprint) made it possible by reducing setup time.
