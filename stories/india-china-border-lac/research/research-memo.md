# Research — India–China Border: The Cold Peace Along the LAC

## Six Questions

### 1. What happened?

The India-China border dispute is a 75-year-old territorial conflict rooted in the colonial legacy of the McMahon Line (1914), competing maps of Aksai Chin, and the unresolved status of approximately 90,000 square kilometres of territory claimed by both sides. The dispute escalated to a full-scale war in 1962, and has since produced a "cold peace" punctuated by periodic crises: Nathu La and Cho La (1967), Sumdorong Chu (1987), Depsang (2013), Doklam (2017), and Galwan (2020).

### 2. Why did it happen?

The dispute has three layers. The first is territorial — where exactly the border runs. India claims the McMahon Line (1914 Simla Convention) as the international boundary in the eastern sector; China rejects it as a colonial imposition. In the western sector, China claims Aksai Chin (the region through which it built a strategic highway in the 1950s); India claims it as part of Ladakh. The second layer is political: the border dispute became entangled with the broader India-China rivalry, Tibet, and Cold War alignments. The third layer is perceptual: both sides see the border as a test of sovereignty, territorial integrity, and national prestige.

### 3. What alternatives existed?

Multiple alternatives were explored and abandoned. The 1954 Panchsheel Agreement (Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence) was meant to establish a framework but explicitly deferred the border question. Zhou Enlai and Nehru exchanged letters between 1959 and 1962 proposing various swaps and adjustments — none accepted. The 1960 officials' talks produced a joint report that identified the divergent positions with unusual clarity but did not resolve them. Post-1962, the 1988 Rajiv Gandhi-Deng Xiaoping rapprochement produced a Joint Working Group that still meets today — but has not produced a final settlement. Alternatives include: accepting the Line of Actual Control as the international border, territorial swaps (India conceding Aksai Chin for Chinese recognition of the McMahon Line), or a "one border, many systems" arrangement.

### 4. Why did India choose this path?

India's position is legally grounded in the McMahon Line, which India inherited from Britain and has treated as settled, while China's position treats the entire border as unsettled — a fundamental incompatibility. India also operates from a domestic political constraint: no government can be seen as "losing" territory, making concessions politically costly. Nehru's policy of "friendship first, border later" (1950–1959) deferred the dispute, which China exploited by building the Aksai Chin road. After 1962, the strategic lesson for India was never to be caught unprepared again — leading to infrastructure build-up, forward-defence policy, and the raising of mountain divisions.

### 5. What were the consequences?

The 1962 war was a humiliating military defeat for India, resulting in Chinese withdrawal to pre-war positions but leaving India's strategic assumptions in ruins. The "Hindi-Chini bhai bhai" era ended, replaced by deep mistrust. India abandoned non-alignment as a framework for China policy and developed closer ties with the Soviet Union. The dispute forced India to invest heavily in mountain warfare capability and border infrastructure. China's victory reinforced its position in Aksai Chin and established its view of the border as subject to Chinese terms. The dispute has cost both sides enormously in military expenditure, diplomatic energy, and the lost opportunity of cooperative Asia.

### 6. Why does it matter today?

The India-China border dispute is one of the world's most dangerous flashpoints. The 2020 Galwan Valley clashes — the first fatal conflict between the two since 1967 — demonstrated that the dispute remains explosive. With both sides deploying hundreds of thousands of troops along the LAC, nuclear-armed, and operating with deeply incompatible positions, the border dispute represents a structural risk of escalation. It also shapes the strategic alignment of Asia: India's growing partnership with the United States (Quad) cannot be understood without the China threat, and China's encirclement concerns (India, Japan, Australia, US) are partly driven by the border dispute. The resolution of the border — or its continued non-resolution — will define Asian security for the next generation.

## Central question

Why is the India-China border still unsettled after 75 years, and what keeps the dispute alive despite periodic progress?

## Key entities

- Line of Actual Control (LAC) — the de facto border established after 1962, not legally agreed
- Aksai Chin — disputed territory in the western sector under Chinese control
- Arunachal Pradesh — Indian state in the eastern sector that China claims as "South Tibet"
- McMahon Line — the 1914 boundary line in the eastern sector
- Tibet Autonomous Region — China's control of Tibet is the geopolitical foundation of the border dispute

## Key thinkers

- Jawaharlal Nehru — set India's initial China policy ("friendship first, border later")
- Zhou Enlai — China's chief negotiator and correspondent with Nehru
- Sardar Patel — argued for a harder line on China and Tibet
- Deng Xiaoping — proposed territorial swap formula (1980s)
- Rajiv Gandhi — visited China in 1988, initiated rapprochement
- Xi Jinping — presided over post-2014 assertiveness on the border
- Neville Maxwell — historian whose *India's China War* (1970) shaped the Western understanding of 1962
