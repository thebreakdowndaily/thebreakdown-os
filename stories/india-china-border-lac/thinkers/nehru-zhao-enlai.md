# Thinker — Jawaharlal Nehru

**Role:** Prime Minister of India (1947–1964)
**Period:** 1889–1964

## Significance to the Border Dispute

Nehru set the framework for India's China policy that persisted through the border dispute's most critical decades. His three pillars — recognition of the PRC, Panchsheel friendship, and deferral of the border question — were designed to build trust before tackling the difficult territorial issues. The policy failed: China exploited the trust deficit by consolidating control over Tibet and building the Aksai Chin road while India looked away. Nehru's decision to grant asylum to the Dalai Lama (1959) was an act of principle that China treated as an act of hostility. By the time Nehru adopted the Forward Policy (1961), it was too late.

## Key Decisions

- Recognition of PRC (1950) — early diplomatic bet on Chinese friendship
- Panchsheel Agreement (1954) — deferred border question; bought peace but at strategic cost
- Asylum to Dalai Lama (1959) — principled but fatal to any remaining trust
- Forward Policy (1961) — risky assertion of sovereignty that China used as casus belli
- Refusal to negotiate under Chinese military pressure (1962) — led to war; preserved principle but at military cost

## Historiographical Position

Nehru's China policy is debated between those who see him as naive and those who argue he had limited options given India's military weakness and the need to prioritise economic development. Maxwell (1970) is critical of Nehru's forward policy; Raghavan (2019) is more sympathetic, situating the policy in its Cold War context.

# Thinker — Zhou Enlai

**Role:** Premier of the People's Republic of China (1949–1976)
**Period:** 1898–1976

## Significance to the Border Dispute

Zhou Enlai was China's chief diplomat and the public face of China's border policy. He negotiated the 1954 agreement, exchanged frank letters with Nehru (1959–1962), visited India in 1960 in a last attempt to avert war, and after 1962 managed the transition to the cold peace. Zhou combined public warmth (Bandung, 1955) with hardline positions in private — a duality that has shaped interpretations of Chinese strategy.

## Key Decisions

- Negotiation of Panchsheel Agreement (1954) — China's terms: India accepts Tibet as Chinese
- Refusal to accept the McMahon Line — consistent across Zhou's entire tenure
- Decision to authorise the 1962 offensive — exact role debated but certainly approved at highest level
- Post-1962 border management — maintained Chinese territorial gains while avoiding further escalation

## Historiographical Position

Zhou is seen by Indian historians as a skilful adversary who outmanoeuvred Nehru diplomatically; by Chinese historians as a principled defender of Chinese territorial integrity. His exact role in the 1962 decision remains opaque due to limited Chinese archival access.

## Key Sources

- Nehru-Zhou correspondence (published in Indian White Papers)
- *Zhou Enlai and the Foundations of Chinese Foreign Policy* (Kuo, 2007)
- *The Sino-Indian Border Dispute and Zhou Enlai* (Hoffman, 2021)

# Thinker — Neville Maxwell

**Role:** Historian and journalist
**Period:** 1926–2019

## Significance

Maxwell's *India's China War* (1970) is the most influential single work on the 1962 war. A former correspondent for *The Times* in New Delhi, Maxwell argued that India's forward policy provoked Chinese retaliation — a thesis that contradicted the Indian official narrative. His work shaped the Western understanding of the war and remains both cited and contested.

## Key Contributions

- Documented the forward policy and its role in the outbreak of war
- Argued China's aims were limited and its withdrawal was strategic, not forced
- His evidence base (interviews with Indian officials, army officers) was strong; his interpretation remains contested

## Historiographical Position

Maxwell is considered a revisionist by Indian historians and a reliable source by many Western scholars. The Indian official position rejects his thesis.

# Thinker — Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel

**Role:** Home Minister of India (1947–1950)
**Period:** 1875–1950

## Significance to the Border Dispute

Patel died before the border dispute reached its crisis, but his warnings on China and Tibet are retrospectively significant. Patel argued for a harder line than Nehru, advocated maintaining British-era rights in Tibet, and expressed early scepticism about Chinese intentions. His letters to Nehru on Tibet (1950) are cited by those who argue India should have been more assertive from the start.

## Key Sources

- Patel-Nehru correspondence on Tibet (1950, published in *Sardar Patel's Correspondence*)
- *Patel: A Life* (Rajmohan Gandhi, 1990)
