# Learning Objectives — India's Inheritance: The Partition and Its Legacies

## Core Objectives

After reading this chapter, the reader should be able to:

1. **Explain** the political process through which the partition of India occurred (1945–1947), including the key decisions and actors.

2. **Identify** the last major alternative to partition (the Cabinet Mission Plan) and explain why it failed.

3. **Describe** the competing historiographical positions on Jinnah's intentions: whether he used the Pakistan demand as a bargaining chip (Jalal) or was committed to a separate state from the outset (Aziz).

4. **Analyse** the relationship between the speed of decolonisation (Mountbatten's accelerated timetable) and the scale of the human catastrophe that followed.

5. **Evaluate** the arguments about whether partition was inevitable or contingent on specific decisions (Nehru's press conference, Mountbatten's choices, Jinnah's strategy).

6. **Summarise** the human experience of partition: the scale of migration (10–15 million), the range of death toll estimates (200,000–2 million), and the concentrated nature of violence in Punjab versus Bengal.

7. **Explain** how the institutional inheritance of the Raj — divided by partition — shaped the different developmental trajectories of India and Pakistan.

8. **Trace** the connection between the partition framework and the Kashmir conflict, which remains unresolved.

9. **Identify** five specific legacies of partition that persist in contemporary South Asian politics: India-Pakistan rivalry, Kashmir dispute, communal identity politics, refugee demographics, and institutional heritage.

10. **Articulate** why the partition matters today — not as a historical event but as a living force that continues to structure politics, identity, and conflict in the region.

## Advanced Objectives

11. **Critique** the evidence basis for different death toll estimates and explain why the scholarly consensus (500,000–1 million) is more reliable than higher or lower figures.

12. **Compare** the partition experiences of Punjab and Bengal, identifying factors that produced more intense violence in one region than the other.

13. **Assess** the decisions of key actors (Nehru, Jinnah, Patel, Mountbatten) using the evidence available to them at the time, avoiding hindsight bias.
