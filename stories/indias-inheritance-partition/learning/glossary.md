# Glossary — India's Inheritance: The Partition and Its Legacies

**Cabinet Mission Plan** — The May 1946 proposal by a British Cabinet delegation to establish a three-tier federal structure for a united India, with a weak centre, strong provinces, and groups of provinces. Accepted by both Congress and the Muslim League in June 1946, then abandoned after Nehru's 10 July press conference.

**Direct Action Day** — 16 August 1946, proclaimed by the Muslim League to demonstrate support for the Pakistan demand. The day was marked by widespread communal violence in Calcutta, with an estimated 4,000–10,000 killed.

**Lahore Resolution** — The March 1940 resolution adopted by the All-India Muslim League calling for "independent states" in the Muslim-majority areas of northwestern and eastern India. The resolution did not use the word "Pakistan" and was deliberately ambiguous about the number and nature of the proposed states.

**Mountbatten Plan / 3 June Plan** — The plan announced on 3 June 1947 by Viceroy Lord Louis Mountbatten to partition British India, with independence and transfer of power to occur on 14–15 August 1947 — ten months earlier than the previously announced deadline.

**Partition Council** — The body established on 17 July 1947 to oversee the division of assets between India and Pakistan, including the military, civil service, financial reserves, and physical assets.

**Radcliffe Award** — The boundary demarcation produced by Sir Cyril Radcliffe's Boundary Commissions, announced on 17 August 1947 (two days after independence), which divided the provinces of Punjab and Bengal between India and Pakistan.

**Princely states** — The 565 nominally sovereign territories under British paramountcy whose rulers were given the choice of acceding to India, acceding to Pakistan, or remaining independent under the Indian Independence Act of July 1947.

**Tryst with destiny** — The phrase from Jawaharlal Nehru's speech to the Constituent Assembly at midnight on 14 August 1947, which has become the iconic statement of Indian independence.

**Transfer of Power** — The multi-volume collection of primary documents from the British archives (published as *The Transfer of Power 1942–47*) that is the most important single source for the official British record of the decolonisation process.

**Communal violence** — Violence directed at members of a different religious community. During the partition period, the primary communal divisions were between Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs.
