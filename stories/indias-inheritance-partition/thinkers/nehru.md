# Thinker — Jawaharlal Nehru

**Role:** President of the Indian National Congress, first Prime Minister of India
**Period:** 1889–1964

## Significance to Partition

Nehru was the most influential voice of the Indian National Congress during the partition negotiations. His insistence on a strong central government and his unwillingness to accept League parity demands were key factors in the breakdown of the Cabinet Mission Plan. His 10 July 1946 press conference — in which he stated Congress would enter the Constituent Assembly "completely unfettered by agreements" — is cited by some historians as the moment that doomed the last realistic alternative to partition.

## Key Decisions

- Acceptance (then effective nullification) of Cabinet Mission Plan (1946)
- Agreement to partition in early 1947, accepting Patel's assessment of inevitability
- Choice of Mountbatten as independent India's first Governor-General over Patel
- Tryst with Destiny speech (14 August 1947)

## Historiographical Position

Nehru is seen by sympathetic historians as a secular idealist facing impossible choices; by critics as naive about League intentions and careless in negotiation. His role in the Cabinet Mission collapse is the most contested aspect of his partition record.

## Key Sources

- *The Discovery of India* (Nehru, 1946)
- *Jawaharlal Nehru: A Biography* (Gopal, 1976)

# Thinker — Muhammad Ali Jinnah

**Role:** Leader of the All-India Muslim League, founder of Pakistan
**Period:** 1876–1948

## Significance to Partition

Jinnah transformed the Muslim League from a minor party into the dominant representative of Indian Muslim political aspirations. The Pakistan demand — which he formalised in the Lahore Resolution (1940) — defined the terms of the partition debate. His role remains the most contested question in partition historiography: did he genuinely demand a separate state, or was the demand a negotiating strategy to secure Muslim rights within a united India?

## Key Decisions

- Lahore Resolution calling for "independent states" (1940)
- Acceptance and subsequent withdrawal from Cabinet Mission Plan (1946)
- Call for Direct Action Day (16 August 1946)
- Acceptance of partition and leadership of Pakistan

## Historiographical Position

Two competing schools: Jalal (1985) argues Jinnah used Pakistan as a bargaining chip and was outmanoeuvred by Mountbatten and Congress; the more established view (Aziz, earlier historians) holds that Jinnah was committed to Pakistan from the beginning and achieved what he set out to do.

## Key Sources

- *Jinnah: A Life* (Wolpert, 1984)
- *The Sole Spokesman* (Jalal, 1985)
- *Complete Works of Quaid-e-Azam* (edited)

# Thinker — Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel

**Role:** Home Minister of India, key Congress leader
**Period:** 1875–1950

## Significance to Partition

Patel was the first senior Congress figure to conclude that partition was unavoidable. His November 1946 letter to Nehru stating his position is a critical document in the record. As Home Minister, he oversaw the integration of princely states into India and managed the refugee crisis. Despite being the pragmatist who accepted partition, his legacy is as the architect of Indian national integration.

## Key Decisions

- Accepting partition as the price of a strong central government (1946–47)
- Administration of the refugee crisis (1947–48)
- Integration of princely states (1947–50)

## Key Sources

- *Patel: A Life* (Rajmohan Gandhi, 1990)
- Sardar Patel's correspondence with Nehru

# Thinker — Lord Louis Mountbatten

**Role:** Last Viceroy of British India, first Governor-General of independent India
**Period:** 1900–1979

## Significance to Partition

Mountbatten was appointed Viceroy in February 1947 with a mandate to achieve a unified India if possible but not to delay British withdrawal. Within a month, he concluded that partition was inevitable. He made two decisions that determined the outcome: accepting partition, and accelerating the timetable from June 1948 to August 1947. The speed of the British withdrawal is one of the most contested aspects of his viceroyalty.

## Key Decisions

- Concluding partition was inevitable within weeks of arrival (April 1947)
- Setting 15 August 1947 as independence date — an accelerated 10-week timetable
- Appointing Radcliffe to the Boundary Commission with an impossible deadline
- Choice of leaving the princely states to decide their own allegiance

## Historiographical Position

Defended by some as achieving a peaceful transfer of power under impossible conditions; criticised by others for prioritising speed over order, contributing directly to the scale of the human catastrophe that followed. His relationship with the Congress leadership — particularly his closeness to Nehru — has also drawn criticism.

## Key Sources

- *Transfer of Power* volumes (official documents)
- *Freedom at Midnight* (Lapierre and Collins, 1975)
- *Mountbatten and the Partition of India* (Prasad, 1966)
