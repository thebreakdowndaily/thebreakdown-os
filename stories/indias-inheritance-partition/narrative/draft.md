# Chapter 1 — India's Inheritance: The Partition and Its Legacies

## Opening — The Tryst

At the stroke of midnight on 14 August 1947, Jawaharlal Nehru rose in the Constituent Assembly in New Delhi to deliver the speech that would define the birth of a nation. "Long years ago we made a tryst with destiny," he said. "At the stroke of the midnight hour, while the world sleeps, India will awake to life and freedom."

The speech was exalted, hopeful, and — by deliberate design — silent on what was happening outside the assembly hall. That same night, the largest forced migration in human history was underway. In the villages of Punjab, Bengal, and Sindh, families who had lived on the same land for generations were gathering what they could carry. The trains that would carry them across the new border were already running. Some would arrive safely. Many would not.

The contrast between the high politics of independence and the human catastrophe unfolding alongside it is not an accident of history. The two were produced by the same process. Understanding why requires understanding partition — not as a single event on a single day, but as a chain of decisions and contingencies that began years earlier and whose consequences continue to shape South Asia today.

## Before Partition

Until late in the decolonisation process, partition was not the expected outcome. The Indian National Congress, which led the independence movement, was committed to a united, secular India. The British had governed India as a single administrative unit for nearly a century. The idea that it would be divided into two sovereign states was, as late as 1945, a minority position — advocated primarily by the All-India Muslim League under Muhammad Ali Jinnah.

The League's demand for Pakistan, formalised in the Lahore Resolution of March 1940, called for "independent states" in the Muslim-majority regions of northwestern and eastern India. The resolution was deliberately ambiguous — it did not specify whether "states" meant one state or several, and it did not use the word "Pakistan." This ambiguity would later become central to one of the most contested questions in partition historiography: whether Jinnah was genuinely demanding a separate state or using the demand as a bargaining chip to secure Muslim rights within a united India.

What is not contested is that the League's political position strengthened dramatically in the 1945–46 elections. In the elections to the Central Legislative Assembly, the League won all 30 Muslim-reserved seats, capturing 86.6 per cent of the Muslim vote. This was a decisive demonstration that the League, not the Congress, represented India's Muslim electorate. It made the Pakistan demand impossible to dismiss.

## The Cabinet Mission and Its Collapse

In March 1946, the British government sent a Cabinet Mission to India — three senior ministers charged with negotiating a constitutional settlement that would preserve a united India while accommodating Muslim political aspirations. The Mission's plan, announced on 16 May 1946, proposed a three-tier federal structure: a central government handling defence, foreign affairs, and communications; provincial governments with substantial autonomy; and groups of provinces joined for common purposes. Critically, provinces would have the right to opt out after ten years.

Both the Congress and the Muslim League initially accepted the plan. Congress accepted on 24 May; the League accepted on 6 June. For a brief period, it appeared that a united India might be preserved.

The acceptance did not hold. On 10 July 1946, Nehru held a press conference in which he stated that Congress would enter the Constituent Assembly "completely unfettered by agreements" and free to make its own decisions. Jinnah saw this as proof that Congress would not honour the plan's limitations on central power. The League withdrew its acceptance on 27 July and called for "direct action" to achieve Pakistan.

Historians disagree about whether Nehru's statement was the cause of the plan's collapse or merely the occasion for it. The evidence supports both interpretations. Nehru's words were carelessly chosen and gave Jinnah the grounds to withdraw that he may have been seeking. But the underlying impasse — Congress's insistence on a strong centre and the League's insistence on parity or separation — existed before Nehru's statement and would have resurfaced regardless. The Cabinet Mission Plan, in hindsight, may have been the last realistic alternative to partition. With its collapse, the trajectory toward division accelerated.

## Direct Action and Violence

The Muslim League's call for direct action was answered on 16 August 1946 — proclaimed Direct Action Day — when communal violence erupted in Calcutta. Over three days, an estimated 4,000 to 10,000 people were killed. The Great Calcutta Killing was not spontaneous rioting. The violence was concentrated in Muslim-majority areas, directed against Hindu and Sikh residents, and involved organised elements including former soldiers and police.

The Calcutta violence broke something in the political process. It demonstrated that communal violence could occur on a scale that overwhelmed civil administration. It eroded confidence — on all sides — that a united India could maintain order. And it proved that the League could mobilise violence as a political instrument, whether or not the leadership had intended that outcome.

Violence spread. In October, Bihar — a Hindu-majority province — saw attacks on Muslim communities that killed an estimated 5,000 to 30,000 people. In the United Provinces and Punjab, communal clashes became routine. By the end of 1946, the assumption that India could transition peacefully to independence had been abandoned.

## The Plan — Mountbatten's Accelerated Timetable

In February 1947, the British government made two decisions that determined the outcome. Clement Attlee's Labour government announced that Britain would withdraw from India by June 1948 — setting a firm deadline for the first time. And Lord Louis Mountbatten was appointed Viceroy, replacing Wavell, with instructions to achieve a unified India if possible but not to delay British withdrawal.

Mountbatten arrived in India on 22 March 1947. After a month of consultations, he concluded that a united India was not achievable. By then, the Congress leadership — particularly Sardar Patel, the pragmatic Home Minister — had reached the same conclusion. Patel had been the first senior Congress figure to argue publicly that partition was unavoidable. He wrote to Nehru in November 1946 expressing his view that the League must be accepted as the representative of Muslims and that continued negotiations were futile.

On 3 June 1947, Mountbatten announced the plan for partition. The British withdrawal would occur not in June 1948 but in August 1947 — a little over two months from the announcement. The decision to accelerate the timetable is one of the most consequential choices in modern Indian history. Mountbatten's reasoning was that a longer transition would allow the administrative machinery to collapse entirely. The alternative view — held by Wavell and others — was that speed made orderly partition impossible and contributed directly to the scale of the violence that followed.

## The Border — Radcliffe's Impossible Task

The division of British India required drawing a border through Punjab and Bengal — regions where Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs lived in mixed populations that no straight line could separate. The task was given to Sir Cyril Radcliffe, a British lawyer who had never visited India before arriving for the assignment.

Radcliffe chaired two Boundary Commissions — one for Punjab, one for Bengal. Each consisted of four Indian judges, two nominated by Congress and two by the League. The Indian judges never reached agreement, dividing along predictable lines. The final decision fell to Radcliffe alone.

He produced the award in five weeks. The border was announced on 17 August 1947 — two days after independence. The delay meant that India and Pakistan came into existence without knowing where their territory began and ended. When the border was finally published, it split villages, farms, irrigation systems, and railway lines. Families found themselves on the wrong side overnight. The border that was supposed to solve the problem of communal division instead created new humanitarian crises at an unprecedented scale.

The Radcliffe Award was a product of impossible constraints: an unrealistic deadline, inadequate demographic data, and a political process that had already determined the outcome before the boundary was drawn. It has been criticised for its hasty execution and defended as the best possible outcome under impossible conditions.

## The Migration — The Human Catastrophe

An estimated 10 to 15 million people crossed the new borders between August and December 1947 — the largest mass migration in human history. Sikhs and Hindus moved east into India. Muslims moved west into Pakistan. The migration was not a single movement but thousands of local decisions made under extreme pressure: families learning that their village was now on the wrong side, hearing reports of violence in neighbouring towns, deciding between staying and leaving with whatever could be carried.

The death toll from partition violence is impossible to establish precisely. Estimates range from 200,000 to 2 million. The scholarly consensus converges on a range of 500,000 to 1 million. The uncertainty itself is a fact about partition: in the chaos of mass migration and systematic violence, many deaths went unrecorded, bodies were never recovered, and communities were destroyed so thoroughly that no accurate count survived.

The violence was not uniform. It was concentrated in Punjab, where the partition of the province produced the largest population exchange and the most intense communal conflict. Bengal's partition was comparatively less violent. The difference is attributed to the earlier exchange of populations in Bengal and the different character of communal relations in the two regions.

## The Institutional Inheritance

While the human catastrophe unfolded, the two new states were being built from the administrative inheritance of the Raj. The Partition Council, established on 17 July 1947, oversaw the division of assets: the Indian Army, Navy, and Air Force; the civil service; the treasury; railway rolling stock; office furniture.

India inherited the larger share — approximately two-thirds of the military and civil service, the bulk of the treasury, and the industrial heartland. Pakistan inherited the frontier territories, a smaller military, and the administrative challenge of building a state from scratch. The division was conducted with remarkable speed and, by most accounts, professionalism. But it created structural asymmetries that shaped both states' development trajectories.

India's institutional inheritance included the Indian Civil Service (transformed into the Indian Administrative Service), the Indian Army, the legal framework of the Government of India Act 1935 (adapted as the interim constitution), and the financial reserves of the central government. These were not neutral tools. They had been designed for colonial governance — centralised, hierarchical, and oriented toward control rather than development. India's challenge after independence was to transform this inheritance into an instrument of democratic development.

Pakistan faced a more fundamental challenge. It had inherited the institutions of a colonial state but not the territory or population those institutions were designed to govern. The Muslim-majority regions that became Pakistan — particularly the North-West Frontier Province, Balochistan, and East Bengal — had been peripheral to the British Indian state. Pakistan had to build a capital, a civil service, a military command structure, and an economic system largely from scratch.

The difference in institutional inheritance is one of the most important and least understood legacies of partition. It shaped not only the different developmental trajectories of India and Pakistan but also the different state-society relationships that emerged in the two countries.

## Kashmir — The Unfinished Business

The partition framework left princely states — nominally sovereign territories under British paramountcy — to choose their allegiance. The Indian Independence Act of July 1947 gave them a stark choice: accede to India, accede to Pakistan, or remain independent. Most chose according to geography and demography. Kashmir was the exception.

Jammu and Kashmir was a Muslim-majority state with a Hindu ruler, Maharaja Hari Singh. It bordered both India and Pakistan. The Maharaja initially attempted to remain independent. In October 1947, tribal militias from Pakistan's North-West Frontier Province invaded the state, advancing toward Srinagar. The Maharaja acceded to India on 26 October. Indian troops were airlifted to Srinagar the same day. The first India-Pakistan war over Kashmir had begun.

The Kashmir conflict was a direct consequence of the partition framework. The arbitrary drawing of borders, the unresolved status of princely states, the competing nationalisms of India and Pakistan, and the strategic importance of the region all converged in a conflict that remains unresolved more than 75 years later.

## Legacies

The partition of India was not a single event. It was a process that began before 1947 and continues today. Its legacies are woven into the fabric of South Asian politics.

The most visible legacy is the India-Pakistan relationship — a rivalry defined by partition, sustained by unresolved territorial disputes, and animated by competing nationalisms that each define themselves partly in opposition to the other. Every war, every crisis, and every peace process between the two countries traces its origins to 1947.

The second legacy is Kashmir — the unfinished business of partition. The dispute over Kashmir has caused two full-scale wars, numerous military crises, and a persistent insurgency that has cost tens of thousands of lives. It remains the most dangerous flashpoint in South Asia.

The third legacy is internal to both states. Partition established a pattern of communal identity politics that has persisted in India and Pakistan — in different forms and with different consequences. In India, partition reinforced the commitment to secularism as a state project, but also created the conditions for periodic communal violence. In Pakistan, partition created a state founded on religious identity that has struggled to define what that identity means in practice.

The fourth legacy is demographic. The refugee crisis of 1947–48 reshaped the population of Punjab, Bengal, Delhi, and Sindh. The refugees who crossed the border became, in both countries, a politically significant constituency whose experience shaped electoral politics, urban development, and national identity.

The fifth legacy is institutional. The administrative, military, and legal inheritance of the Raj — divided by partition, adapted by both states — continues to structure how India and Pakistan are governed. The trajectory of India's democracy and Pakistan's repeated cycles of military rule can be traced, in part, to the different institutions each state inherited and the different challenges each faced.

## Closing

Nehru's "tryst with destiny" speech is remembered as the founding document of independent India. But it was also a speech that deliberately avoided the subject of partition — the violence, the migration, the loss. Nehru chose hope over grief on the night of 14 August 1947. It was the right choice for that moment. But it meant that the full reckoning with partition was deferred — to later generations of historians, writers, and citizens who would try to understand what happened and why.

That reckoning is the work this chapter begins. Understanding partition — not as an event but as a process, not as a tragedy but as a chain of decisions and contingencies — is essential to understanding modern India. The partition created the India that exists today. Its legacies are not past. They are present.
