## Detailed verifications

### Transfer of Power documents (12 vols)

- **Provenance:** Nicholas Mansergh (ed.), *The Transfer of Power 1942–47*, HMSO, 1970–83. British Library shelfmark: OPE 1970/125 and subsequent. Also available digitally via the British Library's website.
- **Authenticity:** Official British government publication. Documents sourced from the India Office Records (now British Library), Cabinet Office papers, and private collections. Authenticity not in question.
- **Reliability:** Primary sources — actual correspondence, minutes, and memoranda. However, documents were selected and edited. Mansergh's editorial choices have been noted by scholars. Cross-reference with Mountbatten Papers and NAI collections recommended.
- **Confidence:** 5. The definitive collection, but no collection is complete.

### Mountbatten Papers

- **Provenance:** University of Southampton, Mountbatten Papers (MB1/ series). Also available on microfilm at the British Library (IOPP/MSS EUR F138).
- **Authenticity:** Verified. Personal and official papers of Lord Mountbatten as Viceroy.
- **Reliability:** First-hand records of Viceroy's meetings, correspondence, and decisions. Must be read critically — Mountbatten was a self-dramatising figure whose accounts were sometimes self-serving.
- **Confidence:** 5 as primary source. 4 for claims specifically about Mountbatten's motivations.

### Jalal, The Sole Spokesman (1985)

- **Provenance:** Ayesha Jalal, *The Sole Spokesman: Jinnah, the Muslim League and the Demand for Pakistan*, Cambridge University Press, 1985. ISBN 0521244629. Widely available.
- **Authenticity:** Peer-reviewed academic monograph from a leading university press. Citation practices verified.
- **Reliability:** This is the most influential revisionist account of Jinnah's intentions. Jalal argues Jinnah never wanted a separate state — he used the Pakistan demand as a bargaining chip to secure Muslim rights in a united India. The thesis is based on close reading of Jinnah's correspondence, but Jalal's interpretation has been contested by subsequent scholars. It is essential to attribute this as **one interpretation** and present the counterarguments.
- **Confidence:** 4. High-quality scholarship but contested. Must be presented as an interpretation, not established fact.

### Collins & Lapierre, Freedom at Midnight (1975)

- **Provenance:** Larry Collins and Dominique Lapierre, *Freedom at Midnight*, Simon & Schuster, 1975. Widely available.
- **Authenticity:** Journalistic account based on interviews and secondary sources. Not peer-reviewed. Some quotations are reconstructions rather than verbatim records.
- **Reliability:** Readable and influential, but scholars have identified factual shortcuts and dramatised reconstructions. Useful for narrative colour but **quarantined as a factual source**. May be cited for well-known episodes if corroborated by a primary source.
- **Confidence:** 2. Quarantined for standalone factual claims. Use only with primary source corroboration.

### Oral history collections (NMML / British Library)

- **Provenance:** Nehru Memorial Museum & Library (NMML) Oral History Project; British Library Sound Archive, partition oral histories.
- **Authenticity:** Verified recordings and transcripts. Participants include policymakers, civil servants, and partition survivors.
- **Reliability:** Oral history is inherently subject to memory decay and retrospective bias. Cross-reference with documentary sources for factual claims. Invaluable for capturing lived experience, which is their primary value.
- **Confidence:** 4 for experiential claims (migration experience, social conditions). 2–3 for specific factual claims (dates, numbers) — must be corroborated.

## Verification conclusion

The source base for this chapter is strong. The Transfer of Power volumes, Mountbatten Papers, and NAI records provide a rich primary foundation. Key secondary scholarship (Jalal, Khan, Tan & Kudaisya) is well-established. Two sources require careful handling: Collins & Lapierre (quarantined for factual use) and oral histories (valuable for experience, limited for factual claims).

The main evidentiary challenge is the asymmetry between Indian and Pakistani archives. Pakistani sources are less accessible, which creates a structural bias toward the Indian and British record. This should be acknowledged as a limitation in the published chapter.
