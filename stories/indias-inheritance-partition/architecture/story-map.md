**Pedagogical purpose:** Answer "What was it like?" — show the human reality of the violence that made a negotiated settlement impossible. Bourke-White's photograph documents the aftermath with the unsparing clarity that made her the defining visual chronicler of the period.

**Caption (analytical):** "Aftermath of the Great Calcutta Killing, August 1946. Over three days of organised communal violence, an estimated 4,000 to 10,000 people were killed. The photograph — by Margaret Bourke-White, the first female war correspondent and the only Western photographer to document the partition firsthand — shows bodies awaiting disposal in a street in Muslim-majority Calcutta. **Lead-in sentence:** "The League's call for direct action produced a result that broke the political process."

**Follow-up sentence:** "The violence did not stop in Calcutta. It spread — to Bihar, the United Provinces, and Punjab — and by the end of 1946, the assumption of a peaceful transition to independence had been abandoned."

**Follow-up sentence:** "Mountbatten concluded within a month of arriving that partition was unavoidable — a conclusion Congress had already reached, largely through Sardar Patel's pragmatism."

## 8. The Migration — Quantifying the Catastrophe

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **VXS category** | Comparison (Sequential pair) |
| **Asset 1** | D-02 — Refugee Flows, 1947–48 (Recreated, Sankey diagram) |
| **Asset 2** | D-01 — Partition Death Toll Estimates (Recreated, range/bar chart) |
| **Placement** | Sequential block — shared caption, embedded alongside the migration section |
| **Supports** | Claims partition-008, partition-009 |

**Pedagogical purpose:** Answer "How do they relate?" — show the scale of human movement and the contested scale of human loss. The Sankey diagram makes visible the scale and direction of population exchange. The death-toll chart makes visible the scholarly disagreement — the range between 200,000 and 2 million is itself a fact about partition.

**Caption (analytical):** "Two quantitative views of the human catastrophe. Left: Refugee flows across the India-Pakistan border, August–December 1947. Arrow width is proportional to volume. Punjab accounts for the largest exchange; Bengal's flows were smaller and more gradual. Right: Partition death toll estimates across four scholarly sources — from Bhatia's conservative 500,000 to the 2 million found in popular accounts. The absence of a precise figure is not a gap in scholarship; it is a consequence of the scale of the catastrophe. Many deaths went unrecorded because the communities that would have recorded them no longer existed. (Data: 1951 Census of India/Pakistan; Scholarly aggregates per Book of Record #0006)"

**Lead-in sentence:** "The numbers that define the partition migration are themselves part of the story."

**Follow-up sentence:** "The violence and the migration were not uniform across India — they were concentrated in Punjab, where the Radcliffe Line did the most damage to integrated communities."

## 9. Institutional Inheritance — Divergent Foundations

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **VXS category** | Comparison |
| **Asset** | D-05 — Military Balance, 1947 (Recreated, waffle chart) |
| **Placement** | Embedded — alongside the claim about structural asymmetry |
| **Supports** | Claim partition-010 |

**Pedagogical purpose:** Answer "How do they relate?" — show the 2:1 division of military assets and the broader institutional asymmetry that shaped both states' developmental trajectories. The visual demonstrates what text can state but cannot display: the structural advantage India inherited and the corresponding challenge Pakistan faced.

**Caption (analytical):** "Division of military assets between India and Pakistan, 1947. India inherited approximately two-thirds of the Indian Army, Navy, and Air Force, plus the bulk of the treasury and the industrial heartland. Pakistan inherited the remaining third, the frontier territories, and the administrative challenge of building a state from scratch. The asymmetry was not just in quantity — India inherited a functioning capital, civil service, and legal framework, while Pakistan had to construct all three simultaneously. (Data: Auchinleck Partition Committee records; Transfer of Power volumes)"

**Lead-in sentence:** "The new states did not begin on equal terms."

**Follow-up sentence:** "The difference in institutional inheritance is one of the most important and least understood legacies of partition — it shaped not only economic outcomes but the character of state-society relations in both countries."

## 10. Kashmir — The Unfinished Business

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **VXS category** | Evidence |
| **Asset** | A-06 — Instrument of Accession of Jammu and Kashmir, 26 October 1947 (NAI / Wikimedia Commons) |
| **Placement** | Embedded — alongside the discussion of the Maharaja's accession |
| **Supports** | Claim partition-011 |

**Pedagogical purpose:** Answer "Proof?" — provide direct, unmediated access to the document that triggered the Kashmir conflict. The facsimile shows the Maharaja's signature, the date (26 October 1947), and the single-page format of an instrument that would shape South Asian geopolitics for 75+ years. A transcribed quote is mediated; the document itself is evidence.

**Caption (analytical):** "Instrument of Accession of Jammu and Kashmir, signed by Maharaja Hari Singh on 26 October 1947. The document accedes the state to the Dominion of India in exchange for military assistance against tribal militias that had invaded from Pakistan's North-West Frontier Province. The accession was provisional — it accepted Indian jurisdiction over defence, foreign affairs, and communications while reserving the state's internal autonomy. That provisional status would become the legal and political fault line of one of the world's most intractable territorial disputes. (Source: National Archives of India, New Delhi; Evidence Level: B)"

**Lead-in sentence:** "The unresolved status of princely states created a crisis that partition's framework could not contain."

**Follow-up sentence:** "The first India-Pakistan war had begun, and the Kashmir conflict — a direct consequence of the partition framework — remains unresolved more than 75 years later."

## Gaps and Recommendations

### 1. B-03 (Radcliffe Line) — GIS recreation needed
The most critical map is the only Orientation asset not yet recreated. **Priority: top.** Without it, Section 7 (The Border) has no visual anchor.

### 2. Explanation diagram — optional enhancement
The chapter lacks a process-diagram visual for the chain of causal decisions (Cabinet Mission → Nehru press conference → League withdrawal → Direct Action → violence → partition). Consider creating an Explanation diagram (new asset: `diagram-partition-causal-chain-v1.svg`) for Section 3–4 transition. This would bring the total to 11, exceeding the density limit for a 2,800-word chapter; it could replace one Context visual (e.g., A-01) if needed.

### 3. SUP-01 provenance
SUP-01 is provisional. Its caption, photographer, and visual content match the master register criteria for the refugee-train theme. Recommend upgrading to a canonical asset ID (A-11 or merge into A-04) after verification.

### 4. Legacies section — no visual
The Legacies section (10) and Closing (11) have no assigned visual. This is acceptable — these sections synthesise what has already been shown. If a visual is desired, consider A-07 (Nehru at UN, November 1948) as a bookend Context visual, showing Nehru on the world stage, repeating the opening's structure but transformed — the leader who gave the tryst speech now representing the nation partition created.
