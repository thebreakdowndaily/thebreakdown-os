# Learning Objectives — India–China Border

## Core Objectives

After reading this chapter, the reader should be able to:

1. **Explain** the origin of the India-China border dispute in the colonial McMahon Line (1914) and the competing territorial claims both countries inherited.

2. **Identify** the three sectors of the border (eastern, middle, western) and the different status of the dispute in each.

3. **Describe** Nehru's policy of "friendship first, border later" and analyse why it failed.

4. **Explain** how the 1954 Panchsheel Agreement and China's secret construction of the Aksai Chin road created a fundamental trust deficit.

5. **Analyse** competing interpretations of the 1962 war: whether India's Forward Policy provoked Chinese retaliation (Maxwell) or China planned to attack regardless (Indian official position).

6. **Evaluate** why China withdrew after its 1962 victory — strategic restraint, logistical necessity, or both.

7. **Summarise** the post-1962 "cold peace" system, including the 1993 and 1996 CBMs, and assess whether it succeeded in managing the dispute.

8. **Identify** the pattern of periodic crises (1967, 1987, 2013, 2017) and explain what changed at Galwan in 2020.

9. **Articulate** three structural reasons the border remains unsettled: irreconcilable territorial claims, strategic utility to both sides, and incompatible historical narratives.

10. **Assess** the risk of future conflict on the India-China border and identify the mechanisms that could prevent or escalate it.

## Advanced Objectives

11. **Critique** the evidence basis for the two competing narratives of the 1962 war, identifying the strengths and limitations of Maxwell's thesis and the Indian official position.

12. **Compare** the India-China border dispute with other unresolved territorial conflicts (Kashmir, South China Sea) and identify what makes it distinct.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| LAC (Line of Actual Control) | The de facto border established after the 1962 war, not legally agreed but mutually recognised as the line separating Indian and Chinese-controlled territory |
| McMahon Line | The boundary between India and Tibet (now China) agreed at the 1914 Simla Convention between Britain and Tibet, which China rejects as a colonial imposition |
| Aksai Chin | The disputed territory in the western sector (Ladakh), under Chinese control since the 1950s, claimed by India |
| Arunachal Pradesh | The Indian state in the eastern sector, which China claims as "South Tibet" |
| Panchsheel | The Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence agreed between India and China in 1954: mutual respect for territorial integrity, non-aggression, non-interference, equality, and peaceful coexistence |
| Forward Policy | India's 1961–1962 strategy of establishing military posts in disputed territory to assert sovereignty, which China cited as a provocation for the 1962 war |
| Galwan Valley | The site of the June 2020 clashes in Ladakh where 20 Indian soldiers died — the first fatal India-China border conflict since 1967 |
| Doklam | A disputed tri-junction area where India, China, and Bhutan meet, site of a 73-day military standoff in 2017 |
| CBM (Confidence-Building Measure) | Agreements designed to reduce the risk of conflict through transparency, communication, and mutual restraint |
| Joint Working Group (JWG) | The bilateral body established in 1988 to negotiate the boundary question, which meets periodically but has not produced a final settlement |
