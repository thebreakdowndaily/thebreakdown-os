## Confidence tiers

### Tier 1 — High confidence (verified, corroborated, non-contested)
- Factual details of the 1954 Agreement, 1960 officials' talks, post-1993 CBMs
- Chronology of diplomatic exchanges
- Text of agreements
- The existence of the boundary dispute and the competing positions

### Tier 2 — Medium confidence (source limitations or minor contestation)
- Sequence and details of 1962 military operations (Indian Army sources vs Chinese sources)
- Motivations of Chinese decision-making (limited archival access)
- Casualty figures from border clashes (both sides under-report)
- Interpretation of Nehru's strategic choices

### Tier 3 — Lower confidence (single source, contested, or speculative)
- Exact chain of command decisions on the Chinese side during 1962
- Chinese internal debates on the border strategy
- Detailed casualty figures for Galwan (2020) — official figures likely incomplete
- The "what if" counterfactual of a different Indian response in 1962

## Key verification notes

1. **Simla Convention (1914):** The text is not in dispute. The dispute is about whether Tibet had treaty-making capacity under Chinese suzerainty, and whether China's representative (Ivan Chen) initialled but did not sign the agreement. India's position: the McMahon Line is an international boundary. China's position: the convention is illegal because Tibet was not a sovereign entity.

2. **Maxwell's *India's China War*:** The most influential single book on the subject. Maxwell makes a strong case that India's forward policy (1961–1962) provoked Chinese retaliation. Critics (including many Indian historians) argue Maxwell understates Chinese territorial ambitions. The evidence base is strong but the interpretation is contested.

3. **Galwan casualties:** Official figures: India 20 killed, China 4 killed. Independent estimates suggest higher Chinese casualties but cannot be verified. Both sides have incentives to misrepresent.

4. **Chinese archival access:** China's Foreign Ministry archives are not generally accessible to independent researchers. All accounts of Chinese decision-making rely on memoirs, official statements, and inferences from observable behaviour — a significant evidentiary limitation.
