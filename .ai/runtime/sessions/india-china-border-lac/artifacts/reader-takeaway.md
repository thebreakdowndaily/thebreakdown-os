# Reader Takeaway

## The core argument

The India-China border dispute is not a misunderstanding or a problem of inadequate diplomacy. It is a structural conflict between two incompatible positions — one legal (India's claim to an inherited colonial boundary), the other anti-colonial (China's rejection of that boundary as an unequal treaty). Neither position is likely to change. The dispute persists not despite attempts to resolve it, but because it serves strategic purposes for both sides.

## What the evidence establishes

1. The border dispute originates in colonial-era boundaries that both countries inherited but interpret differently
2. The 1954 Panchsheel Agreement was a deferral strategy that allowed China to consolidate control over Tibet and build the Aksai Chin road while India pursued friendship
3. The 1959 Tibetan uprising and Nehru's asylum to the Dalai Lama marked the point of no return
4. The 1962 war was caused by a combination of Chinese determination and Indian provocation — the exact weight of each factor is contested
5. China's 1962 withdrawal was likely a combination of strategic restraint and logistical necessity
6. The post-1962 cold peace system worked for five decades but has been strained by increased assertiveness on both sides since 2014
7. Galwan (2020) was a structural shift — the norm against fatal clashes has been broken

## Where historians disagree

| Question | Position A | Position B | Evidence assessment |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Who caused the 1962 war? | China planned to attack regardless of Indian actions | India's Forward Policy provoked Chinese retaliation | Both have evidence; Maxwell's thesis challenges the Indian official narrative |
| Why did China withdraw? | Strategic restraint — China achieved its limited aims | Logistical necessity — supply lines overextended | Both factors present; weight attributed by each side aligns with political narrative |
| Did Nehru's China policy fail? | Yes — naive trust was exploited, leaving India worse off | No — policy bought time, prevented early conflict, and no better alternative existed | Depends on counterfactual; no definitive answer |
| Is the CBM framework still adequate? | Yes — it has prevented war for 65+ years | No — it has not prevented crises or force build-up | Evidence shows both truths: no war + more crises |

## What the reader should do next

1. Read Maxwell, *India's China War* (1970) — the revisionist account of the 1962 war
2. Read Raghavan, *The Himalayan Moment* (2019) — the Cold War context
3. Read Jacob, *Broken Treaty* (2025) — the current analysis
4. Examine the Nehru-Zhou correspondence (published online by India's Ministry of External Affairs)
5. Ask: if the border cannot be resolved, what is the most effective system for managing it without violence?
