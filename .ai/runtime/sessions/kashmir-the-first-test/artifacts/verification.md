## Methodology

Each source was assessed across five dimensions:

1. **Provenance** — Origin, publisher, institutional backing
2. **Authenticity** — Whether the document or work is genuine and unaltered
3. **Reliability** — Scholarly rigour, methodological soundness, peer recognition
4. **Bias Profile** — Known allegiances, national framing, interpretive commitments
5. **Independence** — Whether the source corroborates or contradicts other sources on the same claims

Confidence tiers:

| Tier | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **High** | Established, widely cited, peer-reviewed or official; corroborated by multiple independent sources; no serious authenticity challenges |
| **Medium** | Published and peer-reviewed but with known limitations (access gaps, single-perspective, contested interpretations) or partial availability |
| **Low** | Unpublished, fragmentary, heavily restricted, or with unresolved authenticity/provenance concerns |

| S01 | Srinath Raghavan, *War and Peace in Modern India* (2010) | Monograph | **High** | Published by Palgrave Macmillan; peer-reviewed; Raghavan is Professor of History and International Relations at Ashoka University. Cited universally in the India-Pakistan conflict literature. Draws on Indian government archives, Nehru papers, and British sources. The most analytically rigorous secondary source on India's strategic decision-making during the Kashmir crisis. No authenticity concerns. Bias profile: Indian strategic perspective, but self-aware and critically engaged with counterarguments. |
| S02 | Prem Shankar Jha, *Kashmir 1947: Rival Versions of History* (1996) | Monograph | **High** | Published by Oxford University Press; peer-reviewed. Jha was a journalist and historian with access to Indian and Pakistani sources. The first work to systematically reconstruct the invasion from both sides. Widely cited in subsequent scholarship (Raghavan, Schofield, Ganguly). Bias profile: sympathetic to the Kashmiri perspective; critical of both Indian and Pakistani official narratives. Independently corroborated by Lamb (S03) and Schofield (S05) on key claims about the invasion's planning. |
| S05 | Victoria Schofield, *Kashmir in Conflict* (2003; updated 2010) | Monograph | **High** | Published by I.B. Tauris (now Bloomsbury). The standard single-volume history. Schofield is a journalist and historian who conducted fieldwork in Kashmir, Pakistan, and India. Her account of the tribal invasion draws on interviews with participants, British sources, and both Indian and Pakistani archives. The updated 2010 edition incorporates post-1999 developments. Widely cited and used as a textbook reference. Bias profile: broadly sympathetic to the Kashmiri civilian perspective; balanced between Indian and Pakistani official positions. |
| S09 | Instrument of Accession of Jammu and Kashmir to India (26 October 1947) | Primary document | **High** | The original document is held by the Government of India. Reproduced in the *White Paper on Indian States* (1948), in Menon (S13), and in multiple scholarly works. Its authenticity was questioned by Alastair Lamb, who argued the copy held by India may not be the original signed document. However, the legal validity of the accession is not in serious dispute — it was accepted by the Governor-General, recognized by the British government, and forms the basis of India's sovereignty claim. The signature of Maharaja Hari Singh and countersignature of V.P. Menon are attested by multiple contemporaneous witnesses. Bias: the document itself is a legal instrument; its interpretation is contested, but its existence and basic content are not. |
| S10 | United Nations Security Council Resolutions on Kashmir (1948–1951) | Primary document | **High** | Official UN documents, publicly available through the UN Digital Library and UNSC Official Records. Resolutions 39 (1948), 47 (1948), 51 (1948), 80 (1950), and 91 (1951) are the core legal documents. These are not disputed by either party — both India and Pakistan submitted cases to the UN and accepted the Commission's jurisdiction. The resolutions are the most authoritative primary sources on the international legal framework. Bias: the UN sought compromise, not vindication of either side; the resolutions reflect diplomatic language that both sides interpret differently. |
| S12 | White Paper on Indian States (Government of India, 1948) | Primary document | **High** | Official government publication documenting the accession of all princely states to India. Contains the text of the Instrument of Accession and the correspondence leading to it. Held by the National Archives of India. Bias: explicitly the Indian government's official account; partisan toward the Indian position. However, as an official contemporaneous document, it is an indispensable primary source. Its factual claims about the accession process are corroborated by Menon (S13) and Mountbatten's papers (S14). |
| S13 | V.P. Menon, *The Integration of the Indian States* (1956) | Memoir | **High** | Menon was the Constitutional Adviser to the Governor-General who personally secured the Maharaja's accession. He flew to Srinagar with the Instrument of Accession. His account is the closest thing to a first-person Indian narrative of the accession. Published by Longmans, Green. Menon had no reason to fabricate — he was describing events he participated in. Bias: Menon was an Indian civil servant; his account reflects the Indian official perspective. However, his factual claims about the sequence of events are independently corroborated by the White Paper (S12), Mountbatten's papers (S14), and subsequent scholarship. Essential for claim.kashmir.003 and claim.kashmir.005. |
| S20 | Sumit Ganguly, *The Origin of Wars in South Asia* (1986); *Conflict Unending* (2001) | Monograph | **High** | Published by Westview Press and Columbia University Press respectively. Ganguly is Distinguished Chair in Security Studies at the Naval Postgraduate School and formerly Professor at Indiana University. The dominant IR scholar on India-Pakistan conflict. His "protracted conflict" framework is cited by virtually all subsequent scholars (Raghavan, Schofield, Jaffrelot). Peer-reviewed. Bias: realist IR perspective; tends toward structural explanations. No authenticity concerns. Independently corroborated by Raghavan (S01) and Schofield (S05) on the conflict's pattern. |

### Tier 2 — Medium Confidence

| ID | Source | Type | Confidence | Notes |
|----|--------|------|-----------|-------|
| S03 | Alastair Lamb, *Incomplete Partition* (1989) | Monograph | **Medium** | Published by Hansa Publishers (now out of print but obtainable via academic libraries). Lamb was the first Western scholar to systematically use Pakistani and British archival sources for the Kashmir crisis. His argument that the tribal invasion was planned with official Pakistani support is now broadly accepted (corroborated by Jha, Schofield, Raghavan). However, his interpretation of the Instrument of Accession's authenticity is contested — he argued the copy held by India may not be the original. This specific claim has not been substantiated by subsequent scholarship. Bias: Lamb wrote from a British/Commonwealth perspective; critical of Indian official narratives. His work was initially controversial in India but has been largely vindicated on factual grounds. Medium confidence because of the Accession authenticity controversy and because some of his claims rest on Pakistani sources that cannot be independently verified. |
| S04 | Alastair Lamb, *The Kashmir Problem* (1993) | Monograph | **Medium** | Published by Routledge. Companion volume to *Incomplete Partition*, providing broader historical context. Same author and methodological strengths/limitations as S03. Useful for historical background but less analytically original. Medium confidence because it is a synthesis rather than original research. |
| S06 | Avi Shlaim, *Collusion Across the Jordan* (1988) | Monograph | **Medium** | Published by Columbia University Press; peer-reviewed. Shlaim is Professor of International Relations at Oxford. Medium confidence because it is a popular biography rather than a peer-reviewed academic work, though its primary source citations are valuable. |

Bias: Pakistan's official position is explicitly partisan. Medium confidence because the Pakistani side of the diplomatic record is available through UN channels, but internal Pakistani documents are inaccessible, limiting independent verification. |
