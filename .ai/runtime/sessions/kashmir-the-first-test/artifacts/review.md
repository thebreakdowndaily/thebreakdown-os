## 7. Narrative Quality Requirements

The draft must meet the following narrative standards:

### Tone
- Academic but accessible. Not journalistic. Not blog-like.
- The reader should feel they are reading a carefully argued account, not a news summary.
- No polemic. No advocacy. No false balance.

### Structure
- Each section should open with a claim or event, provide evidence, surface disagreement, and then advance the argument.
- Transitions between sections should be logical, not chronological-only.
- The narrative arc should move from crisis (October 1947) to consequences (1949–present) to significance (why it matters today).

### Voice
- Third-person analytical. Not first-person. Not second-person.
- No editorial "we." The institution's position is embedded in the structure, not in direct address.

### Pacing
- The invasion and accession (October 1947) should receive the most detailed treatment — this is the dramatic core.
- The UN period (1948–49) should be compressed — the resolutions are important but the proceedings are less narratively compelling.
- The consequences section should be selective — not every consequence needs equal space.

### Prohibited
- No "clearly," "obviously," "undoubtedly," or similar certainty markers.
- No editorialising ("India was right to..." / "Pakistan was wrong to...").
- No false balance ("both sides were equally wrong").
- No presentism ("by today's standards...").
- No hindsight bias ("this was inevitably going to happen").
- No single-perspective framing ("India says..." without "Pakistan counters...").
